Bali's **Foreign Tourist Levy (Pungutan Wisatawan Asing/PWA)**, which requires every international visitor to pay Rp150,000 (about USD 10) upon entering the island, has raised a total of **Rp671.35 billion** since it took effect in 2024 through early 2026. Yet behind this milestone, serious concerns have emerged after Indonesia's **Attorney General's Office** opened an investigation into alleged misuse of authority in managing the levy's funds.

## Legal Basis and Purpose of the Levy

The PWA was first introduced under **Bali Provincial Regulation No. 6 of 2023**, later reinforced through **Provincial Regulation No. 2 of 2025**. The policy requires every foreign tourist arriving in Bali—whether by air or sea—to pay a Rp150,000 fee. Officially, the collected funds are earmarked for three core priorities: **cultural preservation**, **environmental protection**, and **improving the quality of tourism services**.

Payment of the levy is processed online through the official Love Bali platform before travelers arrive, as part of the arrival declaration process. Tourists who fail to pay risk administrative sanctions, including restricted access to a number of popular tourist attractions across the island.

## New Scheme: Incentives for Third Parties

One of the key updates introduced under Provincial Regulation 2/2025 is a new incentive scheme for third parties—such as hotels, travel agents, and airlines—who assist in collecting the levy. The scheme is designed to boost compliance rates, as many tourists had previously slipped through without paying due to limited enforcement at arrival points.

## Alleged Fund Mismanagement Under Scrutiny

Amid the steadily growing collection figures, reports have emerged that Indonesia's **Attorney General's Office** is investigating alleged misuse of authority in the management of PWA funds. The investigation sends a serious signal about the governance of a levy long promoted as a cornerstone instrument for sustainable tourism in Bali. Observers say regular, transparent reporting on how the funds are used is essential to preserve public and tourist trust in the policy.

## Government Response and the Road Ahead

The Bali provincial government has reaffirmed that PWA funds will continue to be allocated to priority programs such as waste management, rehabilitation of sacred sites, and support for local arts and cultural activities. However, with the investigation now underway, public attention has turned to how the provincial government will respond to the Attorney General's findings and ensure accountability over funds sourced directly from foreign visitors.

Going forward, the PWA policy is expected to undergo further refinement, including the possible introduction of stronger independent audit mechanisms to ensure the levy funds are genuinely channeled toward cultural preservation and environmental protection, as originally intended.
