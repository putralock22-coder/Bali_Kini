Indonesia's ambition to transform Bali into a global financial hub is taking a major concrete step. The government plans to establish the **Indonesia Financial Centre (IFC)** as a standalone, independent Special Economic Zone (SEZ) in Bali—separate from the two existing SEZs of KEK Kura-Kura Bali and KEK Sanur. The announcement, made in late June and early July 2026, is one of the most consequential economic policy moves in Bali's modern history.

## A Third SEZ for Bali

Rather than integrating the IFC within the footprint of the existing Kura-Kura or Sanur SEZs, the government opted to create a dedicated new economic zone focused exclusively on international financial services.

**Coordinating Minister for Economic Affairs Airlangga Hartarto** confirmed that Bali will ultimately host three International Financial Centers simultaneously—positioning the island to compete directly with Singapore, Hong Kong, and Dubai for high-value financial services activity in the Asia-Pacific region.

Supporting regulations are currently being processed through the Indonesian Parliament (DPR) to provide legal certainty for prospective foreign investors.

## Tax Incentives Up to 0%: The Core Attraction

The IFC's competitive edge lies in its incentive structure. The government is designing a tax package potentially reaching **0% on selected tax categories**—mirroring the model adopted by the Dubai International Financial Centre (DIFC) and Singapore's financial hub framework.

These incentives are targeted at attracting global financial institutions, fund managers, wealth management firms, fintech companies, and international banks to establish regional headquarters or operational offices in Bali.

Indonesia has reportedly earmarked approximately **US$6 billion** (around IDR 97 trillion) for infrastructure development and ecosystem support for the IFC project.

## KEK Kura-Kura and KEK Sanur: Strong Foundation

The expansion decision is supported by the solid performance of Bali's two existing SEZs through Q1 2026:

### KEK Kura-Kura Bali
- Cumulative investment realization: **IDR 1.62 trillion**
- Workforce absorption: **2,146 workers**
- Positioned as a premium integrated coastal zone in southern Bali

### KEK Sanur
- Cumulative investment realization: **IDR 5.37 trillion**
- Workforce absorption: **5,444 workers**
- Tourist visits: **279,804 people**
- Established as a wellness and health tourism premium zone

Nationally, total cumulative investment across all tourism-focused SEZs in Indonesia reached **IDR 45 trillion** in Q1 2026, with **53,278 jobs** created. Bali's SEZs represent a significant share of that national achievement.

## Bali's Economic Pivot: From Tourism to Finance

This move signals a meaningful pivot in Bali's development strategy. For decades, the island's economy has rested almost entirely on tourism. The IFC introduces a second pillar: high-value international financial services.

Analysts note that Bali's existing advantages—world-class quality of life, an established international community of expatriates and digital nomads, premium hospitality infrastructure, and a globally recognized brand—create a distinctive ecosystem that financial hubs in less desirable cities cannot easily replicate.

The combination of lifestyle appeal and financial incentives could position the IFC as a compelling alternative to Singapore for professionals and firms seeking a Southeast Asian base with lower costs and exceptional amenities.

## Challenges Ahead

Despite the compelling vision, significant challenges remain. Regulatory clarity, streamlined permit processes, digital infrastructure quality, and the availability of finance professionals trained to international standards are all critical to the IFC's success.

History shows that ambitious financial centre projects have failed in cities that underestimated the importance of soft infrastructure, rule of law, and market depth. For Bali's IFC to become a genuine rival to Dubai or Singapore rather than just an aspiration on paper, execution will need to match ambition.
