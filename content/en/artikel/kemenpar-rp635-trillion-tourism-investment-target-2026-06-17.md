Indonesia's Ministry of Tourism (Kemenpar) has set an ambitious investment target for 2026: attracting Rp63.5 trillion (approximately USD 3.9 billion) in tourism sector investment throughout the year. The figure is embedded in Indonesia's National Medium-Term Development Plan (RPJMN 2025–2029) and reflects the government's determination to transform the country's tourism economy from volume-driven to value-driven — attracting fewer but more impactful investments that generate sustainable economic benefits.

## Where the Money Currently Flows

Despite the ambitious national target, Kemenpar openly acknowledges a significant challenge: investment remains heavily concentrated in just three areas — Bali, Jakarta, and the Riau Islands (Kepulauan Riau). These three regions dominate both tourism infrastructure spending and hotel development pipelines. The concentration reflects the established tourism ecosystems these regions have built over decades, but it also highlights the untapped potential of destinations elsewhere in the archipelago.

All three regions have been designated as "regenerative tourism destinations" — a classification that goes beyond conventional growth objectives. Regenerative tourism investment is specifically aimed at restoring environmental health, strengthening local communities, and ensuring that economic benefits flow back to the people and places that sustain the tourism experience.

## Ten Priority Destinations Set for Investment Push

To redistribute investment more equitably, Kemenpar has designated 10 National Tourism Priority Destinations (Destinasi Pariwisata Prioritas/DPP) as focal points for incentivized investment. The list spans the full breadth of Indonesia's extraordinary geographic and cultural diversity:

- **Danau Toba**, North Sumatra
- **Borobudur**, Central Java
- **Mandalika**, West Nusa Tenggara
- **Labuan Bajo**, East Nusa Tenggara
- **Likupang**, North Sulawesi
- **Tanjung Kelayang**, Bangka Belitung
- **Bromo-Tengger-Semeru**, East Java
- **Wakatobi**, Southeast Sulawesi
- **Morotai**, North Maluku
- **Raja Ampat**, Southwest Papua

### Infrastructure as the Key Enabler

For many of these destinations, the investment gap is not about lack of appeal — it is about lack of infrastructure. Reliable airports, adequate road networks, clean water systems, and stable electricity are prerequisites for attracting serious hospitality and tourism investment. Coordinated investment in these fundamentals is central to Kemenpar's strategy for the 10 priority destinations.

## Bali Jagadhita VII: Proof of Demand

While the broader investment map is still being redrawn, Bali's own investment story continues to impress. The Bali Jagadhita VII forum, held June 5–7, 2026, recorded potential business deals in the Bali–Nusa Tenggara tourism corridor valued at Rp6.9 trillion. The Bali Beyond Travel Fair 2026, held in late May, brought together 407 buyers from 44 countries with hundreds of sellers from across Indonesia, generating significant potential transactions in tourism products and services.

## Quality Tourism as Investment Magnet

The shift toward quality tourism has direct implications for investment patterns. As Bali and Indonesia's other top destinations move away from mass tourism models, the investment opportunities that emerge are different in character: boutique luxury resorts, wellness retreats, adventure tourism infrastructure, cultural experience centers, and MICE facilities rather than large-volume budget accommodation.

These categories attract a different class of investor — ones focused on longer-term returns, environmental sustainability credentials, and premium positioning — which aligns well with Indonesia's stated goal of attracting "quality" tourism investment that generates lasting economic and social value.

With a clear target, a defined geographic strategy, and demonstrated appetite from international investors, Indonesia's 2026 tourism investment drive carries genuine momentum — and Bali, as always, is leading the way.
