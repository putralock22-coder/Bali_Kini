Bali's most ambitious infrastructure project has undergone a major revision. The Gilimanuk–Mengwi toll road, once planned to span approximately 90 kilometres across the island, has been officially trimmed to around 42 kilometres after the government confirmed the cancellation of its first section.

## Section I Officially Scrapped

The decision to cancel Section I — the stretch connecting Gilimanuk to Pekutatan (53.6 km) — was formalised through an official letter from the Director of Toll Roads, reference number P50102/B/BK/2026/24, signed on February 11, 2026. Rather than being built as a toll road, this corridor will instead be upgraded as a revitalised national road with improved capacity.

The primary reason behind the cancellation is the lack of investor appetite. Rising construction costs, soaring land acquisition budgets, and low projected traffic volumes made Section I financially unviable without substantial government subsidies.

## Two Sections Remain On Track

While Section I has been removed from the toll road plan, Sections II and III will proceed:

- **Section II:** Pekutatan–Soka (24.3 km)
- **Section III:** Soka–Mengwi (18.9 km)

Together, these two sections total approximately 43.2 km and form the new scope of the project. The total investment is estimated at **IDR 19.36 trillion** (approximately USD 1.2 billion), which includes a dedicated motorcycle lane — a unique feature of this project.

## Timeline and Next Steps

Under the revised schedule, the toll operator (BUJT) tender process is targeted to begin in **2027**, with physical construction expected to start in **2028–2029**. The toll road is projected to become operational by **2031**.

Before that, the project still needs to complete its ongoing technical design review and restart the land acquisition location determination (penlok) process, as the previous penlok expired in 2026.

## Thousands of Residents Left in Limbo

One of the most immediate human impacts is the uncertainty facing thousands of residents whose land falls within the original project corridor. Many have been unable to sell or fully utilise their properties while awaiting a government decision. This is especially acute for those in the Gilimanuk–Pekutatan corridor of the now-cancelled Section I, who need clarity on whether their land will be affected by the national road upgrade instead.

## Strategic Importance for Western Bali

The Gilimanuk–Mengwi toll road is part of Indonesia's National Strategic Project (PSN) portfolio. The route is critical for freight logistics, particularly goods transported by ferry from Java through Gilimanuk Port to Bali's commercial centres. Chronic congestion along this west-coast corridor has long been identified as a bottleneck for the island's economy.

The Ministry of Public Works (Kementerian PU) has reaffirmed its commitment to completing Sections II and III, stating that even the reduced scope will deliver meaningful connectivity improvements for western and central Bali residents.

For Bali, this infrastructure project — even in its scaled-back form — represents a significant step toward more balanced development beyond the tourism-heavy southern corridor.
