Against the sweeping backdrop of rice terraces recognised by UNESCO as a World Cultural Heritage Site, thousands of runners tested their legs and lifted their spirits at the Jatiluwih Festival VII and Bali Tourism Run 2026. Held on 20–21 June 2026 in the village of Jatiluwih, Penebel District, Tabanan Regency, the annual event once again cemented this corner of Bali as one of Indonesia's most spectacular sport tourism destinations.

## Two Thousand Runners, One Extraordinary Stage

Around 2,000 participants registered for the Bali Tourism Run 2026, which served as the centrepiece of the broader Jatiluwih Festival VII programme. Runners came not only from across Bali but also from other provinces throughout Indonesia and from overseas — all drawn by the prospect of racing through one of the most visually arresting landscapes on earth.

The course was designed to showcase Jatiluwih at its most dramatic. Starting at the Telabah Gede zone, runners passed through checkpoints at Kedamian, Umo Duwi, and Besi Kalung before finishing at the UNESCO Monument — a landmark that symbolises the world's recognition of this landscape's extraordinary value. Along the way, participants traversed 227 hectares of stepped terraces that cascade down the hillsides in waves of green, creating a backdrop unlike any other road race in the world.

## The Theme: Nature, Balance, and Tradition

The festival carried the theme *"In Balance with Nature, Inspired by Tradition"*, a phrase that captures the centuries-old philosophy underpinning life in Jatiluwih. The Subak irrigation system — a cooperative water management tradition rooted in Balinese Hindu philosophy — is not merely a technical achievement but a living expression of community, spirituality, and environmental stewardship.

Organisers made clear that participants were guests in an active agricultural landscape. Runners were explicitly reminded not to damage the paddy field bunds and to show respect for the farmers who continue to tend their fields even as the festival unfolds around them. This respect for the working landscape is as much a part of the event's identity as the running itself.

## National Recognition: On the KEN Calendar

The Jatiluwih Festival VII received a prestigious boost when it was officially included in the Kharisma Event Nusantara (KEN) 2026 calendar — Indonesia's Ministry of Tourism's curated list of signature national events. This designation gives the festival direct promotional support from the central government and elevates its profile among both domestic and international audiences.

The Regent of Tabanan highlighted the dual significance of the event: as a tourism promotion vehicle and as an economic driver for the local community. Inclusion in KEN ensures that Jatiluwih's story reaches beyond Bali to audiences across Indonesia and abroad.

## Economic Ripple Effects: UMKM in the Spotlight

The festival's impact extended well beyond the finish line. A minimum of 40 local small and medium enterprises (UMKM) from Tabanan were given dedicated stalls to showcase and sell their products to participants and visitors. Jatiluwih's famous organic red rice, locally grown mountain coffee, handcrafted goods, and traditional Balinese culinary specialities were all on offer.

This deliberate inclusion of local producers ensures that the economic energy generated by thousands of visitors translates into direct income for Tabanan's communities — not just for accommodation and transport operators, but for farmers, artisans, and food producers at the grassroots level.

## A Century of Bali Tourism, Celebrated Here

The year 2026 carries special significance for Bali's tourism industry, marking the centenary of organised tourism on the island. In this context, the Jatiluwih Festival VII takes on added meaning as part of a broader celebration of 100 years of Bali welcoming the world.

From its UNESCO-recognised paddy fields to its thriving community of Subak farmers, Jatiluwih represents everything that makes Bali's cultural landscape irreplaceable. The festival is proof that world heritage and vibrant, living tourism experiences are not in conflict — they reinforce each other, generation after generation.
