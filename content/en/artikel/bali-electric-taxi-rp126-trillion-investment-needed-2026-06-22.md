Bali's provincial government has set an ambitious goal: all taxis operating on the island should eventually run on **battery-powered electric vehicles (EVs)**. The transition, however, comes with a hefty price tag. The Head of Bali's Transportation Agency (*Dinas Perhubungan*) has revealed that fully electrifying the island's taxi fleet would require an investment of **Rp1.26 trillion** (approximately US$ 77 million).

## Two Scenarios, Different Timelines

The Transportation Agency has mapped out two scenarios for the fleet transition:

### Optimistic Scenario: Complete by 2028
Under the aggressive timeline, Bali targets adding **3,155 new electric vehicles** to replace conventional gasoline-powered taxis between 2026 and 2028. This scenario requires rapid capital deployment, government incentives, and a robust network of charging infrastructure to be built concurrently.

### Moderate Scenario: Complete by 2030
A more conservative estimate assumes slower adoption due to financing constraints and operator hesitancy. Under this timeline, full electrification would be achieved by **2030** — still within Indonesia's broader green transport roadmap, but with less immediate environmental impact.

## Driver Concerns: Quiet Streets and High Costs

Not everyone in Bali's taxi industry is cheering for the electric transition. A number of conventional taxi drivers have voiced frustration that their current vehicles already struggle to find enough passengers, let alone afford the leap to more expensive EVs.

Electric vehicles in Indonesia's market still carry a significant price premium over comparable gasoline models. Without clear subsidy schemes, low-interest financing, or trade-in programs, the financial barrier is real — particularly for independent drivers who don't work for large fleet operators.

The Bali government's move to mandate EV adoption starting January 1, 2026, has amplified these concerns, leading some drivers to publicly object to what they see as an unfunded mandate.

## Why Bali Is Pushing Ahead Anyway

The rationale for electrifying Bali's taxi fleet goes beyond emissions reduction:

**Tourism image:** International visitors — particularly from Europe and Australia — are increasingly climate-conscious. A destination that offers EV rides alongside pristine beaches sends a powerful sustainability signal.

**Air quality:** Southern Bali's resort corridors suffer from worsening air pollution. Replacing gasoline taxis with zero-emission vehicles would deliver immediate, tangible improvements in air quality along beach roads and around major tourist hubs.

**Energy security:** Bali's power grid is increasingly powered by renewable sources, meaning EV charging in Bali has a lower carbon footprint per kilometer than charging in many other Indonesian provinces.

## The Infrastructure Challenge

Replacing the vehicles is only half the battle. A comprehensive network of **charging stations** must also be built — at Ngurah Rai Airport, major hotels, beach clubs, shopping centers, and along key tourist routes. Without convenient charging access, electric taxis would face operational challenges that undermine the entire premise.

Bali's provincial government and Bank Indonesia have included EV infrastructure among the **22 priority investment projects** they are actively pitching to domestic and international investors through events like the Bali Jagadhita Investment Forum 2026.

## Part of a Larger Green Vision

The push for electric taxis fits within Bali's broader strategy of transitioning from a high-volume, low-quality tourism model to a sustainable, high-value destination. The same logic driving mandatory waste sorting, coastal conservation, and organic farming support also underpins the EV transition: Bali's natural environment and reputation are its most valuable economic assets, and protecting them requires structural investment.

At Rp1.26 trillion, the ask is significant. But against an annual tourism foreign exchange contribution of Rp176 trillion, it represents less than one percent of Bali's tourism income — a price tag that looks increasingly reasonable when framed as infrastructure for the island's next chapter.
