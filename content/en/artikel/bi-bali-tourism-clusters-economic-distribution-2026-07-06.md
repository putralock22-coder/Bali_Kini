The Representative Office of Bank Indonesia (BI) in Bali Province has completed a strategic mapping of tourism clusters designed to distribute the economic benefits of tourism more equitably across the island. This initiative directly addresses a longstanding imbalance: the severe overcrowding in southern Bali contrasted with the largely untapped potential of the north, east, and west.

## Southern Bali Has Hit Its Ceiling

For decades, Bali's tourism industry has been concentrated in the south — Badung, Kuta, Seminyak, Nusa Dua, and Denpasar. The result is an unmistakable over capacity: chronic traffic congestion, mounting pressure on water resources, worsening waste management challenges, and skyrocketing land prices that have made it increasingly difficult for local communities to remain in their own neighbourhoods.

Meanwhile, northern, eastern, and western Bali hold extraordinary natural and cultural wealth that has yet to receive proportionate investment and attention. Bank Indonesia views this as a structural imbalance that requires urgent correction.

## Three Distinct Tourism Clusters

Based on BI's analysis, each region of Bali possesses a distinct comparative advantage that should anchor its tourism development:

### West Bali – Marine and Coastal Ecotourism

Jembrana Regency has been identified as having strong potential for marine tourism anchored in ocean ecosystem conservation. Its relatively pristine waters and rich marine biodiversity make it an appealing destination for diving, snorkelling, and nature-based tourism for visitors seeking authentic underwater experiences.

### North Bali – Wellness and Agro-Spiritual Tourism

Buleleng Regency, with its cooler highland climate and well-preserved agricultural traditions, is positioned to develop wellness tourism and agro-spiritual experiences. The combination of nature, tradition, and spirituality creates a unique value proposition to attract high-value travellers looking beyond conventional beach holidays.

### Klungkung and Bangli – Cultural Heritage and Culinary Tourism

The Klungkung-Bangli area, rich in the cultural legacy of the Klungkung Kingdom and the ancient Bali Aga traditions of Bangli, is earmarked for heritage and culinary tourism. Both regencies hold a depth of art, architecture, and authentic local flavours that remain largely undiscovered by international markets.

## Infrastructure: The Critical Bottleneck

BI Bali acknowledges that equitable tourism dispersal will not succeed without a corresponding leap in infrastructure and regional connectivity. Several flagship projects have been proposed:

- **Accelerating the east-west-north tourism corridor** to link emerging destinations with established southern hubs
- **A public transit (MRT) line from Denpasar through Klungkung and Bangli to Singaraja**, unlocking new mobility for tourists and residents alike
- **Revitalisation of Gilimanuk Port** in Jembrana for smoother connections with Java
- **Revitalisation of Padangbai Port** in Karangasem for improved sea routes to Lombok and Nusa Tenggara
- **Revitalisation of Celukan Bawang Port** in Buleleng as a northern maritime gateway

## Bali's Outsized Economic Footprint

The scale of Bali's tourism contribution to the national economy makes this redistribution effort all the more consequential. Tourism accounts for 66 percent of Bali's economic growth, based on 2024 data. Bali Province alone contributes IDR 176 trillion — approximately 55 percent — of Indonesia's total tourism-derived foreign exchange earnings of IDR 319.9 trillion.

By strategically directing development toward underserved regions, BI Bali aims to channel economic benefits to communities that have long been left behind by the tourism boom, while simultaneously alleviating the environmental and social pressures facing the south.
