## Bali's Tourist Levy Collections Grow Steadily

The Bali Provincial Government has confirmed that collections from the **Pungutan Wisatawan Asing (PWA)**, known internationally as the Love Bali Levy, continue to show positive growth throughout 2026. By mid-May 2026, the total amount collected from the IDR 150,000 per-visitor charge had reached **IDR 114 billion** (approximately USD 7 million).

This figure builds on a strong start to the year — in the first quarter alone (January through March 2026), the levy generated **IDR 71.4 billion**, representing an **11.6 percent year-on-year increase** compared to the same period in 2025. The April through mid-May collections added another significant tranche to push cumulative receipts to the IDR 114 billion mark.

## Challenging Path to Annual Target

Despite the encouraging trajectory, the IDR 114 billion collected by mid-May represents only about **23 percent** of the full-year 2026 target of IDR 500 billion. With more than half the year still ahead, the Bali government faces a significant gap to close if it hopes to meet its ambitious revenue goal from the tourist levy program.

A central challenge remains **low compliance**. Currently, only around **32 percent** of international visitors complete their PWA payment obligation before or upon arrival in Bali. With millions of foreign tourists visiting the island annually, improving this ratio is critical to significantly accelerating collections.

## Governor Koster's Creative Compliance Strategies

Bali Governor Wayan Koster has adopted an innovative, relationship-based approach to encouraging levy compliance. One notable initiative involves personally recording **thank-you video messages** sent to tourists who have completed their Love Bali payment — a gesture designed to create a positive, memorable experience that spreads word-of-mouth awareness among potential visitors.

Additionally, Governor Koster is scheduled to invite **consulates-general** from countries whose nationals are among Bali's most frequent visitors, seeking their active support in promoting the levy to outbound travelers before they leave their home countries. This diplomatic outreach aims to embed PWA awareness into the pre-trip planning stage.

The **Ministry of Tourism** of the Republic of Indonesia has also expressed support for this approach, with plans to implement a similar official thank-you acknowledgment system for tourists who fulfill their payment obligations — further legitimizing the program at the national level.

## What the Levy Funds

Revenue generated through the PWA program is earmarked for three core priorities:

- **Cultural preservation** — supporting traditional ceremonies, arts, and heritage conservation across Bali's villages and temples
- **Environmental protection** — funding waste management, water conservation, and coastal cleanup initiatives
- **Sustainable tourism development** — investing in infrastructure and capacity that benefits both visitors and local communities

These spending priorities align with Bali's broader vision of quality tourism — a model that prioritizes the depth of visitor experience and community benefit over sheer visitor volume.

## Visitor Arrivals Continue to Grow

On the demand side, Bali's appeal remains strong. A total of **1.645 million international visitors** arrived in Bali during January through March 2026, up **2.4 percent** year-on-year. Despite ongoing global geopolitical uncertainties, the island continues to attract tourists from Australia, India, China, and beyond.

With the remainder of 2026 still ahead — including peak seasons coinciding with Galungan, Kuningan, and the school holiday period — Bali's government is optimistic that an intensified compliance campaign can close the gap to the IDR 500 billion target.
