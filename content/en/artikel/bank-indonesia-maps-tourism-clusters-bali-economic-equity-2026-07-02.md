Bali is globally celebrated as a world-class tourism destination, yet the economic prosperity generated by its tourism sector has long been unevenly distributed across the island. Southern resort areas such as Uluwatu, Pandawa, and Mertasari have already exceeded their carrying capacity, while other regions remain underdeveloped. Recognizing this imbalance, Bank Indonesia's (BI) Bali Provincial Office is now actively mapping tourism clusters tailored to each region's unique potential — with the goal of achieving more inclusive economic growth.

## Overtourism in South Bali: A Real Problem

Yusuf Wicaksono, Deputy Director of BI's Bali Provincial Office, openly acknowledged that several tourist hotspots in South Bali have surpassed their capacity limits. Uluwatu, Pandawa Beach, and Mertasari Beach are cited as prime examples of destinations under excessive visitor pressure.

The consequences of overtourism are threefold: **environmental degradation**, **strain on infrastructure**, and **diminished visitor experience**. Left unaddressed, South Bali's appeal risks long-term decline — an outcome that would ultimately harm the very tourism industry it depends on.

## Strategy: One Region, One Tourism Identity

To redistribute tourist flows more equitably, BI Bali is designing a tourism cluster map that plays to each regency's distinct strengths and authentic character. The approach is not simply about diverting crowds elsewhere — it's about building genuine new attractions rooted in each region's natural and cultural assets.

### Jembrana Regency (West Bali): Maritime and Eco-Tourism
Jembrana's pristine coastline and well-preserved marine ecosystems make it ideal for developing **maritime tourism centered on marine conservation**. Activities such as responsible snorkeling, diving, and eco-marine experiences are seen as key growth areas.

### Buleleng Regency (North Bali): Wellness and Agro-Spiritual Tourism
With its mountain landscapes and black-sand beaches, Buleleng offers a tranquility that sets it apart from the bustle of the south. BI is promoting development of **wellness tourism, agro-tourism, and spiritual tourism** in this regency — experiences increasingly sought by the growing wellness travel market.

### Klungkung and Bangli Regencies: Heritage and Culinary Tourism
The Klungkung-Bangli area is rich in historical heritage and authentic local culinary traditions that remain largely underexposed to international visitors. BI is encouraging **cultural heritage and culinary-based tourism** here, including the revitalization of Klungkung's historic district and immersive village experiences in Bangli.

## Infrastructure Remains the Key Bottleneck

Tourism diversification cannot succeed without adequate infrastructure. BI has identified several critical development priorities:

- **East-west-north tourism corridor**: Improved road connectivity between emerging destinations outside South Bali
- **MRT: Denpasar–Klungkung–Bangli–Singaraja**: An ambitious public transport project that would link the city center with eastern and northern Bali
- **Port modernization**: The ports of Gilimanuk (Jembrana), Padangbai (Karangasem), and Celukan Bawang (Buleleng) require upgrades to support stronger inter-island and inter-regional connectivity

Without acceleration on these infrastructure fronts, even the best-planned tourism clusters will struggle to reach their potential.

## Why Economic Equity in Tourism Matters

Tourism contributes the largest share of Bali's economy, yet when growth is concentrated in a single zone, the benefits don't truly reach all Balinese people. Farmers in Buleleng, fishermen in Jembrana, and artisans in Bangli hold real potential to participate in and benefit from the tourism economy — if the right enabling conditions are in place.

BI Bali emphasizes that the cluster-based approach is a long-term endeavor requiring synergy between local governments, the private sector, traditional communities (desa adat), and financial institutions. Investment in new destination development is expected to create local employment, raise household incomes, and simultaneously preserve the ecological and cultural integrity of Bali as a whole.
