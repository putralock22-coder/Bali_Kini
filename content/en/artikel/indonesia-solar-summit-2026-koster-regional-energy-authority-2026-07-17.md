Bali Governor Wayan Koster opened the Indonesia Solar Summit (ISS) 2026 at the Bali Beach Convention Center, The Meru Sanur, Denpasar, on Tuesday, July 14, 2026. In his opening remarks, Koster urged the three-day forum, running through July 16, 2026, not to stop at discussion but to produce strategic partnerships, concrete investment, and policy recommendations that accelerate Indonesia's national target of 100 gigawatts (GW) of solar power capacity.

## Bali Hosts the Forum for the First Time Outside the Capital

ISS 2026 marks a milestone as the first time this annual forum, organized by the Institute for Essential Services Reform (IESR), has been held outside Jakarta. Bali was chosen as host in recognition of the province's consistent push toward a clean energy transition, in line with the Clean Energy Bali vision Koster has written into several gubernatorial policies since the start of his term.

The three-day forum focuses on accelerating solar power development toward the 100 GW target, reducing reliance on diesel-based power plants, financing strategies for solar projects, and strengthening the national solar industry ecosystem. Hundreds of stakeholders from the central government, regional administrations, investors, and energy industry players attended the meeting.

## Koster: Bali Aims for Net Zero Emission 15 Years Ahead of Schedule

In his address, Koster reaffirmed Bali's ambition to become free of fossil-fuel power plants, making solar power (PLTS) its main pathway toward Net Zero Emission (NZE) by 2045 — fifteen years ahead of the national target set for 2060. He said accelerating this transition requires stronger development of renewable energy, particularly solar, wider adoption of electric vehicles, and opening up space for investment and collaboration in the green energy sector.

"Accelerating clean energy use at the regional level requires full support and synergy from the central government," Koster said, pointing to the need for active involvement from the Ministry of Energy and Mineral Resources (ESDM) and state utility PT PLN in backing regional initiatives.

Koster stressed that the President's directive to pursue 100 GW of solar capacity should be matched by concrete steps giving regions greater latitude to manage solar energy themselves. He asked that regions with strong commitments to the energy transition, such as Bali, be given wider room to accelerate implementation on the ground, without being held back by permitting processes and authority that remain centralized in Jakarta.

## Implementation Challenges on the Ground

Behind the optimism surrounding the ambitious target, energy analysts note that accelerating solar power in the regions is often hampered by cross-ministerial permitting processes, limited transmission grid capacity for PLN to absorb electricity from large-scale solar plants, and financing schemes for renewable energy projects that remain less than fully accommodating for domestic and foreign investors alike.

For Bali, these challenges are especially pressing given the island's heavy reliance on electricity supplied through the Java-Bali interconnection system via undersea cable, even as demand keeps climbing alongside tourism growth and population increase. Large-scale solar deployment, including in strategic tourism areas, is seen as key to reducing that dependence while reinforcing Bali's image as a sustainable tourism destination.

The outcomes of ISS 2026 are expected to serve as a concrete reference for the central government in drafting regulations more accommodating to the regions, while paving the way for large-scale solar energy investment that has so far been constrained by various structural obstacles.
