As Indonesia's central government plans to transform Bali into an **international tourism superhub** over the next two decades, Bali's Council of Traditional Villages (Majelis Desa Adat/MDA) is sounding an urgent alarm. At a capacity-building training for traditional village leaders (prajuru desa adat) held in Denpasar on Wednesday, June 10, 2026, the message was unambiguous: village leaders must remain **critical and vigilant** against investments that could damage Bali's natural environment and cultural fabric.

## Bali's Ambitious Development Blueprint

The scale of planned development is substantial. Bali has set an investment target of **Rp 47.8 trillion** for 2026, up from Rp 45 trillion the year before. Major infrastructure projects are moving forward, including a **new international airport planned for North Bali** and the **Gilimanuk–Mengwi Toll Road**, both intended to support the island's transformation into a globally competitive tourism hub over the next 20 years.

This blueprint represents an enormous opportunity for economic growth—but also an equally enormous pressure on Bali's land, ecosystems, and the communities that have shaped its identity for centuries.

## No Land Stands Outside Traditional Territory

A fundamental point raised during the MDA training: **there is no single piece of land in Bali that falls outside a traditional village's jurisdiction**. Every resort, hotel, villa, highway, and infrastructure project is physically located within the boundaries of a traditional village (desa adat). This is not merely symbolic—it means traditional village leaders have both the right and the moral authority to engage with, scrutinize, and conditionally approve or challenge investment projects within their territory.

The message from MDA was direct: village leaders must be **active participants** in development, not passive bystanders. Bali's traditional villages must not be reduced to mere objects of large-scale investment, but must act as deliberate subjects who shape the terms under which development occurs.

## Investment Heavily Concentrated in Sarbagita

Current data reveals a troubling imbalance in where investment flows. **93% of Bali's investment is concentrated in the Sarbagita region**—the urban and peri-urban corridor spanning Denpasar, Badung, Gianyar, and Tabanan—while five other regencies collectively receive just **7%**.

This geographic concentration has produced severe side effects in Sarbagita: intractable traffic congestion in areas like Canggu, rapidly escalating land prices that price out local residents, and the continued conversion of agricultural and green land into commercial property. Meanwhile, other parts of Bali remain economically marginalized.

## Students Also Demand Accountability

Concern about this imbalance extends beyond the traditional leadership community. Recent months have seen student organizations call on the Indonesian Parliament's Special Committee on Inter-Island Spatial Planning to protect the rights of traditional communities as mass tourism investment accelerates. The issue, it seems, resonates across generations.

## A Formal Role for Traditional Villages?

This call for greater village involvement in investment decisions gains additional weight from developments at the national level. Indonesia's Parliamentary Legislative Body (Baleg DPR) is exploring legislation that would give traditional villages **formal authority to participate in the investment permitting process** within their territories. If passed, this would represent a landmark shift in Indonesia's development governance—and one that has significant implications for Bali.

As investment pressure intensifies on one of the world's most recognized destinations, Bali's traditional villages remain an irreplaceable guardian of the island's identity. The question is whether the institutions that protect that identity will be empowered to keep pace with the speed of capital entering Bali's shores.
