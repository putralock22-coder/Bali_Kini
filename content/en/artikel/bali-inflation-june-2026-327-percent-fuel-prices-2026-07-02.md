Bali's consumer prices climbed to an annual inflation rate of **3.27 percent** in June 2026, ticking up from 2.99 percent in May but remaining below the national average. Bank Indonesia's (BI) Bali Regional Office described the reading as within manageable bounds.

## Below the National Rate

Achris Sarwani, Head of Bank Indonesia's Bali Regional Office, confirmed that the province's June inflation is still lower than Indonesia's national figure of **3.34 percent** and sits comfortably within the government's target band of **2.5% ± 1%**.

On a monthly basis, Bali recorded a **0.71 percent** price increase in June, accelerating from 0.42 percent in May. The steeper monthly jump was driven by two forces that converged in the same calendar window: the Galungan religious holiday and an upward adjustment in non-subsidised fuel prices.

## Fuel Overtakes Food as the Main Culprit

In a notable departure from the usual pattern, this time it was **non-subsidised fuel** — not staple foods — that emerged as the primary inflation driver. Higher petrol prices, particularly for premium grades widely used by tourists and Bali's growing middle class, cascaded into transport costs and the pricing of goods that depend on physical distribution.

This marks a shift worth watching: as Bali's consumer base becomes more affluent and EV adoption rises, the sensitivity of its inflation to fuel price moves may become more pronounced.

## Food Prices Also Played a Role

Several food commodities added to the upward pressure, particularly around the Galungan preparations:

- **Shallots** — demand surged for ceremonial cooking, lifting prices
- **Garlic** — import-dependent and exposed to exchange rate fluctuations
- **Carrots and green beans** — highland vegetables affected by seasonal weather disruptions

## Risks on the Horizon

BI Bali flagged three emerging risks that could push inflation higher in the coming months:

1. **School holidays** — Bali's domestic tourism high season intensifies demand for goods and services
2. **Moderate El Niño potential** — the dry-season transition, combined with weather anomalies, could reduce local agricultural output
3. **Global freight volatility** — higher international shipping costs may filter through to the price of imported goods sold in Bali

## The 4K Strategy

To pre-empt further price pressure, BI works alongside the Regional Inflation Control Team (TPID) on a framework known as **4K**:

- **Keterjangkauan** (Price Affordability) — subsidised market operations targeting essential goods
- **Ketersediaan** (Supply Availability) — ensuring commodity stocks do not run thin
- **Kelancaran** (Distribution Flow) — smoothing supply chains from production hubs to end consumers
- **Komunikasi** (Communication) — publishing transparent price information to guide market expectations

Inter-regional food distribution agreements facilitated through regional state-owned food companies (Perumda Pangan) are also being stepped up to move surplus produce from one district to underserved areas.

## Bali's Economic Resilience

Despite global headwinds — from geopolitical uncertainty to energy price volatility — an inflation reading that stays below the national average reflects the underlying resilience of Bali's economy. Tourism, which accounts for **66 percent of the province's economic growth**, has continued to function as a consumption anchor, supporting purchasing power even as commodity prices rise.
