A wave of student-led demonstrations under the hashtags **#MenujuIndonesiaBangkrut** (Towards Indonesia Bankruptcy) and **#IndonesiaBangkrut** (Indonesia Bankrupt) continued to sweep major Indonesian cities on Friday, June 19, 2026. Protests erupted in Jakarta, Surabaya, Yogyakarta, Bandung, Medan, and Makassar—driven by anger over soaring fuel prices and worsening economic conditions. Bali, however, has been confirmed as calm and stable, with all tourist activities running normally.

## What Sparked the Protests?

The trigger was a dramatic increase in the price of Pertamax (RON 92) from **IDR 12,300 to IDR 16,250 per liter**, alongside a rise in Pertamax Green 95 from IDR 12,900 to IDR 17,000 per liter. These hikes, implemented by state energy company Pertamina in stages since early June, were seen as too steep by millions of Indonesians already struggling with rising food costs, a weakening rupiah, and higher electricity bills.

On June 19 specifically, another adjustment pushed fuel prices up by an additional **IDR 4,100**, sending fresh shockwaves through communities and intensifying street protests nationwide.

## Five Demands from the Streets

The demonstration movement, which has involved thousands of protesters from universities and civil society groups across the country, centers on five core demands:

1. **Reverse the non-subsidized fuel price hike** to an affordable level
2. **Address rising staple food prices** that are eroding household purchasing power
3. **Stabilize the rupiah exchange rate**, which has been trading at IDR 17,800–17,840 against the US dollar
4. **Cut government budget waste** and improve fiscal discipline
5. **Reassess the Free Nutritious Food program (MBG)**, widely criticized as poorly targeted and financially inefficient

Universities represented in the demonstrations include Bogor Agricultural University (IPB), State University of Jakarta (UNJ), the Jakarta State Polytechnic, Pancasila University, Gunadarma University, and various national student organizations.

## Bali Stays Calm

While protests grip major urban centers across the archipelago, Bali's situation is markedly different. The Bali Regional Police confirmed that the island remains fully secure and that no significant protest activity has disrupted public order. Tourism infrastructure—beaches, temples, cultural performances, and the ongoing **Bali Arts Festival (PKB XLVIII)**—continue operating without interruption.

"Bali is business as usual. The island is safe and welcoming," confirmed local tourism operators, as the June–July school holiday period—one of Bali's busiest domestic travel seasons—kicks into high gear.

## Impact on Bali's Tourism Sector

While Bali itself is calm, the fuel price increases are being felt in the industry's operating costs. Transportation services—car rentals, tour buses, and ride-hailing fleets—have been the most affected, as most of these vehicles run on Pertamax.

Some tour operators have already adjusted package prices upward by around **5 to 10 percent** to absorb higher fuel costs. Despite this, demand remains robust. The combination of school holidays, the ongoing Bali Arts Festival running through July 11, and continued strong international arrivals is keeping occupancy rates and bookings healthy.

## Government's Position

The central government, through the Ministry of Energy and Mineral Resources, maintained that the non-subsidized fuel price adjustments are in line with market mechanisms and necessary to reduce the fiscal burden of subsidies on the state budget. Authorities have pledged to strengthen social safety nets for the most vulnerable households.

Bali's provincial government echoed calls for calm, urging local businesses and residents not to participate in actions that could undermine the island's reputation as a world-class tourist destination. Preserving Bali's image of safety and tranquility remains the top priority for local authorities during this period of national economic tension.
