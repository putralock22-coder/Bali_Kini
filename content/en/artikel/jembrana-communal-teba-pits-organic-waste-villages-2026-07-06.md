Jembrana Regency in western Bali has taken a decisive step to tackle its growing organic waste problem. At a coordination meeting held on Sunday, July 5, 2026, the regional government agreed to roll out a system of communal teba — traditional Balinese composting pits — across every village and urban ward in the regency.

## Coordination Meeting Sets the Framework

The meeting took place at the Jembrana Regional Government meeting room and was chaired by Regional Secretary I Made Budiasa. In attendance were representatives from all relevant Regional Government Work Units (OPDs), subdistrict heads (camat), village heads (perbekel), and urban ward heads (lurah) from across Jembrana.

Several key decisions were reached, including the optimisation of organic waste management at village level and the permitted use of provincial government-owned land within villages to support the programme.

## What Is a Teba?

A teba is a traditional Balinese concept — a designated pit or area for organic waste disposal and composting, long embedded in the rural culture of Bali's villages. The Jembrana administration is reviving and scaling this practice into a communal form: one teba communal per village or ward, shared by all residents of that area.

Plt. (Acting) Head of the Jembrana Environment, Housing and Settlement Service, I Wayan Putra Mahardika, explained the practical arrangement:

*"The teba, or organic waste disposal pit, will use government-owned land within the village or urban ward. That site will be used communally by local residents."*

## Organic Waste Diverted Away from Landfills

One of the most significant shifts under this new policy is the rerouting of organic waste away from the Final Disposal Site (TPA). Instead, organic waste will be sent to Integrated Waste Processing Facilities (TPST), where it will be processed into compost for agricultural use.

This represents a meaningful change in Jembrana's waste management chain, as organic waste typically accounts for the largest share of household refuse. Reducing the organic fraction flowing to TPA should extend the operational lifespan of existing landfill capacity and reduce associated environmental impacts such as leachate and methane emissions.

### Government Equipment and Land Support

To ensure the programme succeeds at the village level, the regency government committed to providing concrete support:

- **Heavy equipment** to excavate the teba pits
- **Permission to use provincial land** located within each village
- **Technical assistance** to village heads and ward heads for ongoing management

Village and ward leaders expressed willingness to take responsibility for waste management in their areas through the teba communal system.

## Addressing the Spread of Illegal Dump Sites

A key driver behind this initiative is the proliferation of illegal dump sites (TPS liar) that have appeared across Jembrana. Without a structured and accessible system for disposing of organic waste, residents have resorted to dumping on vacant land, creating informal waste sites that are difficult to monitor and clean up.

By establishing an official communal teba in every village, the regency aims to give residents a legitimate, clearly designated alternative — one rooted in traditional Balinese practice and supported by modern infrastructure.

## A Replicable Model for Bali

Jembrana's approach — combining the cultural resonance of the teba concept with coordinated government support and village-level accountability — may offer lessons for other regencies across Bali facing similar pressures from rising waste volumes. As tourism and population growth continue to drive up household waste generation, grassroots solutions anchored in local tradition represent a promising complement to large-scale infrastructure investment.
