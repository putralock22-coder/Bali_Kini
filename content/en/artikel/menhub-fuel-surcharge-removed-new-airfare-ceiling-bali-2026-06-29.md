In welcome news for travelers heading to Bali and other domestic destinations, **Minister of Transportation Dudy Purwagandhi** announced on Monday (29/6/2026) that the fuel surcharge currently applied to domestic airline tickets will be **eliminated** once Indonesia's new maximum airfare ceiling (Tarif Batas Atas, or TBA) officially comes into force.

## What Is the Fuel Surcharge?

The *fuel surcharge* is an additional fee that domestic airlines are permitted to charge on top of the base ticket price to offset fluctuating jet fuel costs. Under **Ministerial Decree KM 1041 of 2026**, airlines may levy a fuel surcharge of up to **50 percent of the maximum fare** depending on the service class.

This surcharge has been in place since May 13, 2026, following a government evaluation that placed the average jet fuel price at **Rp 29,116 per liter** as of May 1, 2026.

## Why the Current System Is Outdated

Minister Dudy explained that Indonesia's current airfare ceiling was last updated in **2019** — at a time when the rupiah exchange rate and fuel prices were dramatically different from today's conditions. As a result, the existing TBA no longer accurately reflects the true operating costs of airlines, creating a mismatch that airlines compensate for through surcharges.

"The new TBA will already incorporate fuel cost components," Dudy said. "Once it takes effect, there will be no need for a separate fuel surcharge."

The government is currently finalizing a new TBA formula that accounts for current jet fuel prices, the exchange rate, and updated service standards. It will be implemented after global geopolitical conditions and fuel prices stabilize.

## What This Means for Bali Tourism

The policy has significant implications for Bali's tourism industry. Domestic visitors — who make up a large share of Bali's annual arrivals — have long complained about unpredictable ticket prices, particularly during peak travel seasons like the current **June–July school holiday period**.

By folding the fuel surcharge directly into a revised TBA, ticket prices will become more **transparent and predictable**. Travelers will no longer encounter surprise fees at checkout, and it will be easier to compare prices across airlines.

Industry observers and travel agents have welcomed the announcement, with many saying that clearer pricing structures will encourage more Indonesians to plan trips to Bali, particularly during shoulder seasons when demand tends to dip.

## A Step Toward Competitive Domestic Aviation

The reform is part of a broader government effort to create a healthier, more competitive domestic aviation market that benefits consumers without undermining the financial sustainability of airlines. Integrating all cost components into a single TBA gives airlines a realistic framework while giving consumers a single, honest price.

Indonesia's domestic aviation sector has faced pressure in recent years from rising operational costs, currency depreciation, and post-pandemic demand shifts. A new, reality-based TBA is seen as a long-overdue step toward a more stable and trusted air travel ecosystem.

The Ministry of Transportation has not yet announced an exact date for when the new TBA will come into effect, but says the finalization process is ongoing and an official announcement will follow in due course.
