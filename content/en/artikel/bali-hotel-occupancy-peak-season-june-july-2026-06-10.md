The Indonesian Hotel and Restaurant Association (PHRI) Bali is entering the peak tourism season with confidence. PHRI Bali Chairman Tjokorda Oka Artha Ardana Sukawati — known as Cok Ace — has predicted that hotel occupancy across Bali will rise between **10 and 12 percent** during June-July 2026, driven by school holidays and the international peak travel season.

## Positive Momentum Since Q1

Early signs of recovery were visible even before the peak season began. In the first quarter of 2026, Bali hotel occupancy rose by **2.4 percent** compared to the same period in 2025, signaling that the accommodation sector is trending in a healthier direction despite lingering external challenges.

Cok Ace emphasized that this optimism is grounded in actual booking trends, particularly from foreign visitor segments using online platforms and travel agencies.

## Foreign Tourists Set to Dominate

### Currency Advantage Drives Demand

One of the primary drivers of foreign visitor arrivals during this period is the exchange rate advantage. The weakening of the Indonesian rupiah against the US dollar makes Bali more affordable for international travelers, particularly those using dollar, euro, or yen as their base currency.

Cok Ace noted that foreign tourists automatically receive better value when spending on accommodation, dining, and activities across Bali. Upscale hotels in Seminyak, Nusa Dua, and Jimbaran have been capitalizing on this to drive premium bookings.

### Rising Airfare Remains a Challenge

On the other hand, the industry continues to face headwinds from rising airfare. Global aviation fuel price increases have pushed up airline operating costs, which are passed on to passengers in the form of higher ticket prices.

This factor could dampen domestic tourist arrivals to Bali, although its impact on international visitor numbers remains comparatively limited.

## Hotels Hold the Line on Room Rates

Despite the promising occupancy projections, the majority of PHRI Bali member hotels have chosen **not to raise room rates** in the near term. Cok Ace explained that this is a defensive strategy to protect Bali's competitiveness against other Southeast Asian destinations.

"Businesses are still cautious. Raising rates too quickly could erode Bali's competitive edge compared to other regional destinations," Cok Ace said.

This approach is also designed to sustain long-term booking volumes while waiting for the global economic climate to stabilize and airfare trends to ease.

## Year-End Projections

PHRI Bali projects occupancy rates could reach as high as **95 percent** in premium resort areas at the height of the peak season — approaching full industry capacity and signaling that Bali remains a top-tier destination in the Asia-Pacific region.

Industry players hope that the second half of 2026 will outperform last year, generating a multiplier effect across supporting sectors including culinary, transportation, and the local creative economy.
