Indonesia's Ministry of Tourism (Kemenpar) has formally pushed for an expansion of the country's **Visa-Free Visit (BVK)** policy, targeting seven strategic tourist-sending markets. With Bali serving as Indonesia's premier international gateway, the proposal carries significant implications for the island's tourism recovery and long-term growth trajectory.

## Seven Countries on the Proposal List

Tourism Minister **Widiyanti Putri** outlined the seven groups proposed to receive visa-free entry to Indonesia:

1. **China** — Asia's largest outbound tourism market
2. **India** — the world's fastest-growing outbound market
3. **South Korea** — a high-value, premium tourism contributor
4. **Japan** — known for high per-trip spending and quality travel
5. **Australia** — Bali's single largest source of international visitors
6. **New Zealand** — an underutilized high-potential market
7. **Singapore Permanent Residents (PRs)** — a niche segment not previously well-served

Widiyanti positioned the proposal as more than an immigration measure — framing it as a **tourism competitiveness instrument** and a proven tool for job creation.

## 32.4 Percent Projected Increase in Arrivals

Kemenpar drew on a landmark study by the **World Travel & Tourism Council (WTTC)** in collaboration with Oxford Economics, which evaluated the impact of Indonesia's sweeping 2016 visa-free initiative — when the country extended visa-free access to 169 nations. That policy drove a **24 percent increase in foreign tourist demand** and supported the creation of approximately **400,000 jobs**.

Using a refined methodology based on 2018 actual arrival data, Kemenpar projects that a new round of BVK expansion could boost foreign tourist arrivals by as much as **32.4 percent** — a revision upward from earlier estimates. In concrete terms, this would mean millions of additional international visitors annually flowing through Indonesia's airports, hotels, restaurants, and tourism businesses.

## Indonesia Falling Behind Regional Peers

A key argument in Kemenpar's case is the stark reality that Indonesia **lags behind its ASEAN neighbors** in terms of visa accessibility. Malaysia, Thailand, Vietnam, and Singapore have all implemented far more permissive visa regimes, giving them a competitive edge when attracting international travelers who have multiple destination options at similar price points.

With roughly comparable travel costs to neighboring destinations, international tourists — particularly from Asia — tend to gravitate toward countries with the simplest entry procedures. Indonesia's more restrictive approach has, by Kemenpar's assessment, cost the country significant market share.

## Three Evaluation Criteria

Kemenpar indicated that the selection of target countries is based on three objective indicators:

- **Tourism volume potential** — the scale of the outbound travel market
- **Per-trip spending** — the economic quality of visitors from each country
- **Security and reciprocity considerations** — national security assessments and diplomatic reciprocity

## Debate with Immigration Directorate

The proposal has not gone unchallenged. The Directorate General of Immigration under the Ministry of Law has taken a more cautious stance, raising concerns that excessively liberal visa policies could be exploited for non-tourism purposes. The public debate between the two government bodies reflects the genuine tension between maximizing tourism revenue and maintaining robust border control.

Kemenpar, however, argues that Indonesia's current surveillance and digital verification systems are sufficiently advanced to allow responsible BVK expansion without compromising national security.

## What This Means for Bali

For Bali specifically, the visa-free proposal represents a potential game-changer. The island has already seen dramatic surges from markets like China — up 255 percent in visitor numbers in Q1 2026 — and India, which recorded strong double-digit growth. Removing the remaining administrative friction of visa applications from these key markets could unlock an entirely new tier of growth.

Higher arrival numbers, combined with Bali's well-established tourism infrastructure, would create stronger multiplier effects for local communities — from villa operators and restaurateurs to souvenir artisans and cultural performers.
