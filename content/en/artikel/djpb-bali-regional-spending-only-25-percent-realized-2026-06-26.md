The Regional Office of the Directorate General of Treasury (DJPb) Bali has issued a firm warning to local governments across the province, urging them to accelerate the realization of their regional budgets. With the year already past its midpoint, spending has reached only **25.19 percent** of the total allocated budget — a figure that falls well short of the expected pace.

## The Numbers Tell a Concerning Story

Data compiled by DJPb Bali reveals that from January through May 2026, regional governments in Bali have collectively spent only **Rp9.78 trillion** out of a total budget ceiling of **Rp38.8 trillion**. That leaves more than three-quarters of the year's planned spending yet to be executed.

Breaking it down further, operational expenditure — which covers salaries and routine government costs — stands at **Rp7.49 trillion**, or **29.74 percent** of its Rp25.1 trillion ceiling. Capital expenditure, the category most directly tied to physical infrastructure and development projects, lags even further behind.

## DJPb Sets Clear Quarterly Benchmarks

The Head of DJPb Bali's Regional Office has provided specific quarterly targets to help local governments track and improve their performance:

| Quarter | Recommended Realization |
|---|---|
| Q1 (Jan–Mar) | 15% of budget ceiling |
| Q2 (Apr–Jun) | 40–50% of budget ceiling |
| Q3 (Jul–Sep) | 70% of budget ceiling |
| Q4 (Oct–Dec) | Above 95% of budget ceiling |

By those benchmarks, Bali's regional governments should be approaching the 40–50% mark right now. At 25.19%, they are behind by a considerable margin.

## Why This Matters for Bali's Economy

Slow budget absorption is more than a bureaucratic problem — it has real consequences for the local economy. Government spending is one of the primary drivers of economic activity in areas where private investment and tourism revenue fluctuate seasonally.

When spending is heavily back-loaded toward the end of the year, the economic stimulus it provides is compressed into a narrow window — typically November and December. This creates an uneven economic cycle in which contractors, suppliers, construction workers, and service providers experience extended dry spells in the first half of the year, only to face rushed, potentially lower-quality work in the final months.

For Bali specifically, strengthening non-tourism economic activity through steady public investment is increasingly important as the province works to reduce its structural dependence on visitor numbers.

## The Broader Fiscal Picture

Despite the slow realization of regional spending, Bali's overall fiscal position through May 2026 shows some strengths. The national budget (APBN) recorded a surplus of **Rp679.98 billion** in the province, and an additional **Rp333.6 billion** has been channeled through Sharia State Securities (SBSN) to finance priority government projects.

However, DJPb officials caution that these figures should not be read as a sign of fiscal health if they are not matched by effective spending at the regional level. Fiscal resources sitting unused in government accounts do not contribute to growth, employment, or improved public services.

## A Call to Action for Nine Regencies and Cities

DJPb Bali is calling on all local governments — from the Provincial Government of Bali to its nine regencies and cities — to urgently audit the bottlenecks slowing their budget execution. Recommended steps include:

- Expediting public procurement processes for goods and services
- Ensuring that project administrative requirements are prepared and cleared in advance
- Pushing budget line managers to be more proactive in releasing approved funds

With six months remaining in 2026, there is still an opportunity to course-correct. But the window is narrowing. DJPb's message is clear: the time to act is now, before year-end budget rushes once again undermine the quality and impact of public investment across Bali.
