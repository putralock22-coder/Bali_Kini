Bank Indonesia's (BI) Regional Representative Office in Bali Province is developing a comprehensive tourism cluster map tailored to the unique potential of each region. The initiative aims to distribute economic benefits more evenly across the island, as Bali's southern areas — long the epicenter of mass tourism — are now facing serious overcrowding.

## Southern Bali Has Reached Its Limit

Yusuf Wicaksono, Deputy Director of BI's Bali Representative Office, stated that popular southern Bali destinations such as Uluwatu, Pandawa Beach, and Mertasari have exceeded their ideal visitor capacity. The overcrowding is taking a visible toll on the environment, local infrastructure, and the overall quality of the tourist experience.

"We need a tourism diversification strategy that directs visitors to other regions rather than continuing to concentrate them in one area," Yusuf said.

The cluster mapping exercise goes beyond simply redirecting tourist flows — it focuses on developing each region's authentic strengths as distinct, competitive destinations.

## Four Clusters, Four Distinct Identities

### Western Bali – Maritime Tourism and Conservation
Jembrana Regency is being positioned as a maritime tourism hub, emphasizing water sports and marine ecosystem conservation. The region holds significant untapped potential for diving, snorkeling, and eco-marine tourism.

### Northern Bali – Wellness and Agro-Spiritual Tourism
Buleleng Regency is being developed as a center for fitness tourism and agro-spiritual experiences. The combination of mountain scenery, productive farmlands, and deep spiritual traditions creates a compelling draw for travelers seeking more meaningful, authentic adventures.

### East-Central Bali – Culture and Culinary Tourism
The Klungkung and Bangli areas are earmarked for culture-and-culinary-based tourism, leveraging their rich artistic traditions and distinctive local cuisine to attract quality visitors.

### Southern Bali – Quality Management
The established southern zone — covering Badung and Denpasar — remains Bali's primary gateway but will shift focus from volume growth toward crowd management and elevating visitor experience quality.

## Connectivity Is the Critical Enabler

A key obstacle to achieving equitable tourism distribution is inadequate inter-regional connectivity. BI is advocating for accelerated development of the east-west-north Bali corridor, including a proposed MRT line linking Denpasar–Klungkung–Bangli–Singaraja.

Without improved transport links, even the best-planned cluster strategy risks failure, as visitors will gravitate back toward the most accessible destinations in the south.

## Tourism Remains Bali's Economic Engine

BI data shows that tourism contributed approximately 66 percent of Bali's economic growth in 2024. The island welcomed 7.05 million international visitors in 2025 — a new record, up from 6.33 million the previous year. Combined with domestic arrivals, total visits reached approximately 16.3 million in 2025.

With visitor numbers continuing to rise, distributing the economic benefits becomes increasingly critical to prevent widening inequality between regions and to protect overcrowded destinations from environmental degradation.

## Local Economies Stand to Gain

A well-planned cluster development strategy is expected to generate a strong multiplier effect in regions outside southern Bali. New employment opportunities, growing micro and small businesses, and increased local income in currently underserved areas are among the tangible outcomes BI envisions from this approach.

Ultimately, BI hopes that tourism diversification will not only protect Bali from destination fatigue but also ensure that the economic rewards of being the world's most-visited island are shared by every district and community across the Island of the Gods.
