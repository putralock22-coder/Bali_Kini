Bali's economy entered 2026 with contradictory signals: robust GDP growth alongside declining international arrivals, rising hotel occupancy despite fewer foreign tourists, and an inflation anomaly that broke a decade-long seasonal pattern. Here is what the latest BPS data reveals.

## The Headline Numbers

Bali's PDRB (Regional GDP) expanded **5.58% year-on-year** in Q1 2026, reaching **Rp44.28 trillion** (approximately USD 2.7 billion). This comfortably outpaces Indonesia's national growth rate of roughly 4.9%.

| Indicator | Value | Change |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Regional GDP (Q1 2026) | Rp44.28 trillion | **+5.58% yoy** |
| International visitors (Jan–Apr) | 2.02 million | **−1.11% yoy** |
| International visitors (April) | 553,000 | **−6.41% yoy** |
| Hotel & restaurant tax (Jan–May) | Rp2.9 trillion | **+Rp300M yoy** |
| May inflation (month-on-month) | 0.42% | Anomalously high |
| May inflation (year-on-year) | 2.99% | Within BI target |

## The Visitor Paradox

Fewer international tourists, yet higher hotel tax revenue. How?

BPS Bali's analysis points to **domestic tourists (wisatawan nusantara)** absorbing the shortfall left by declining foreign arrivals. Indonesia's expanding middle class increasingly chooses Bali for domestic holidays, and their spending patterns — longer stays, higher F&B consumption — partially compensate for lower international headcount.

**Top international source markets (April 2026):**
- 🇦🇺 Australia: 146,414 (+22.24% yoy) — clear market leader
- 🇮🇳 India: 46,513 (+9.55% yoy)
- 🇨🇳 China: 44,447 (recovering, but still ~60% of pre-pandemic peak)
- 🇰🇷 South Korea: ~28,000

Australia's dominance is structural: direct flights, established Bali culture among Australians, and a favourable AUD/IDR exchange rate. India's surge reflects growing outbound tourism from tier-2 Indian cities now served by direct Bali routes.

## May Inflation: An Anomaly Explained

Bali typically experiences **deflation or flat prices in May** — no major religious holidays, steady supply chains. Yet May 2026 recorded a **+0.42% month-on-month** increase, the highest May figure in years.

**Three factors converged:**

**1. Red chili prices (+11.76% MtM)** — Unusually high rainfall from April into May disrupted chili harvests across Bali and East Java, squeezing supply sharply.

**2. Air fares (+8.61% MtM)** — Transport Ministry Regulation KM 1041/2026 allowed carriers to impose additional service charges, passed directly to passengers.

**3. Idul Adha demand pull** — The feast day fell on 26–27 May, pulling forward household meat and grocery purchases into late May, inflating the monthly basket.

Year-on-year inflation of **2.99%** remains within Bank Indonesia's target band of 2.5% ± 1%, so monetary policy is unlikely to respond.

## Structural Risks on the Horizon

**Drought season.** BMKG forecasts 2026's dry season (June–September) to be hotter than 2025. By September, up to 90% of Bali's land area is expected to experience drought conditions. Agricultural output — particularly rice, chili and vegetables — may face further pressure, which could sustain food-price inflation into Q3.

**Wage gap pressure.** Bali's minimum wage (UMR) stands at approximately Rp3.3 million/month, while the estimated cost of living (KHL) is Rp5.25 million — a gap of Rp1.95 million. This structural imbalance constrains domestic consumption and fuels labour migration out of the island's lower-wage sectors.

**Chinese visitor recovery.** China remains Bali's second-largest potential source market, but 2026 arrivals are still roughly 60% of 2019 levels. The constraint is airlift: direct China–Bali flight capacity has not fully recovered. A full normalisation of Chinese arrivals would meaningfully accelerate Bali's tourism revenue.

## Full-Year Outlook

The provincial government's 2026 target is **6.625 million international visitors**. After four months, the pace of arrivals suggests this target is achievable but not guaranteed.

Key upside drivers for H2:
- Peak travel season (July–August)
- Bali Arts Festival (13 June–11 July) drawing cultural tourists
- Galungan religious holiday (17 June) boosting ceremonial tourism

Bali's economy has demonstrated remarkable resilience: a near-decade of tourism-led growth has created sufficient ancillary industries — retail, logistics, digital services — to buffer short-term visitor fluctuations. The more pressing structural question is whether wage growth and cost-of-living pressure will erode the island's labour supply advantage over the next five years.
