## A Ritual Born from Battle, Kept Alive by Devotion

As Bali prepares for **Hari Raya Kuningan on June 26-27, 2026**, the village of Munggu in Mengwi District, Badung Regency, readies itself for one of the island's most visually stunning and spiritually significant ceremonies: **Mekotek**. Equal parts ritual and spectacle, this centuries-old tradition draws curious visitors from around the world while remaining deeply sacred to the village community that has kept it alive.

## Rooted in the Mengwi Kingdom

Mekotek traces its origins to the **Mengwi Kingdom of the late 17th century**, when it reportedly began as a triumphant welcome for soldiers returning victorious from battle against the Blambangan Kingdom in Java. Warriors celebrated by raising their weapons skyward in a collective display of victory — an act that gradually transformed into a community purification ritual passed down through generations.

Today, Mekotek is understood as a **niskala** (invisible realm) ceremony — a spiritual offering to cleanse the village of negative forces and honor ancestral spirits during the sacred Galungan–Kuningan period.

## The Spectacle: Human Pyramids and Bamboo Poles

The heart of Mekotek is breathtaking. Hundreds of men and boys from the village form **towering human pyramids**, stacking on each other's shoulders several layers high. While maintaining their precarious balance, participants brandish long bamboo poles called **ung-ungan**, pointing them toward the sky.

These living formations then move through the village in a grand procession, accompanied by the crashing of bamboo poles, the sound of gamelan music, and the chants of villagers. The atmosphere is electric — raw, communal, and profoundly Balinese.

## When and Where

Mekotek 2026 coincides with **Kuningan Day, June 26-27, 2026**, held in **Desa Munggu, Mengwi, Badung Regency**. Proceedings typically begin in the morning and continue through the afternoon.

Munggu Village sits approximately **20 minutes northwest of Seminyak**, easily reachable by motorbike or private car.

## Visiting Tips

Mekotek is open to the public. There is **no entrance fee**, no ticketing booth, and no tourist infrastructure — you simply show up and stand alongside the villagers as the ritual unfolds. That raw authenticity is precisely what makes it memorable.

Visitors are asked to dress modestly out of respect for the ongoing religious ceremony, to avoid obstructing the procession route, and to be mindful that this is, above all, an act of worship rather than a performance staged for tourists.

## Kuningan: When the Ancestors Return to the Heavens

Kuningan marks the final day of the Galungan festival — the moment when Balinese Hindus believe their ancestral spirits, who have been visiting the mortal world for ten days, return to the heavens. Every 210 days on the Balinese Pawukon calendar, the community of Munggu reaffirms its bond with the sacred through Mekotek, transforming a moment of religious transition into an unforgettable display of collective identity.

For travelers in Bali around the Galungan and Kuningan period, witnessing Mekotek is an opportunity to see living cultural heritage — not in a museum or on a stage, but exactly where it belongs.
