Bali's 2026 high school enrollment season has once again exposed a troubling inequality in the island's education system. Data from the Student Admission System (SPMB) Phase II residential zone track reveals a vast gap between sought-after schools in urban centers — flooded with applications — and schools on the periphery that sit nearly empty. In the most extreme case, one school received **zero applicants** through the residential zone pathway.

## The Extreme Case: SMAN 1 Marga Gets No Applicants

The starkest example of this divide is **SMAN 1 Marga**, which recorded zero prospective new students through the residential zone track during SPMB Phase II 2026. This outcome is a striking illustration of how imbalances in school facilities and reputation create extreme disparities in the student intake process.

Meanwhile, several public high schools in city centers — particularly in Denpasar, Gianyar, and Badung — are struggling to accommodate surging applicant numbers. Some schools have been forced to implement a **double-shift system**, running two sessions of classes per day to handle the overflow.

## Root Causes of the Imbalance

### Unequal Facility Distribution

For years, budgets and public attention have been disproportionately directed toward a handful of elite schools. As a result, many schools in rural or peri-urban areas have fallen behind in terms of physical infrastructure, science laboratories, sports facilities, and teaching quality. This infrastructure gap has entrenched the public perception that peripheral schools are inferior.

### The Reputation Trap

Bali's Commission IV of the Regional House of Representatives (DPRD) had previously flagged the injustice of unequal facility distribution between city and rural schools. Once a school has developed a negative reputation in the public mind, the reluctance of parents to enroll their children becomes a self-reinforcing cycle that is difficult to break.

## Enrollment Timeline

Phase II of SPMB 2026 registration officially closed on **Thursday, July 2, 2026**. The verification and validation team from Bali's Education, Youth, and Sports Agency (Disdikpora) is currently processing the final data. The announcement of results is scheduled for **July 9, 2026**.

## A Call for Systemic Reform

This disparity is a stark reminder that Bali, despite its reputation for cultural excellence, still has significant ground to cover in ensuring equal access to quality education across the island. Policymakers are being urged to take concrete steps — from **redistributing high-performing teachers** to rural schools, to increasing budget allocations for underserved institutions and enforcing a fairer zoning system.

Without serious intervention, the same pattern will likely repeat itself next year: city schools growing ever more crowded while rural schools continue to struggle — and the right of every Balinese child to a quality education remains threatened by systemic inequality.
