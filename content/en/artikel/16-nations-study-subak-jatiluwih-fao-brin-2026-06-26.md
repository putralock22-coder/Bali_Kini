Delegates from 16 countries gathered in the traditional rice terraces of Jatiluwih village, Tabanan, Bali on Friday (June 26, 2026) as part of the *Regional Workshop on Knowledge Exchange on Frontier Technologies for Low-Emission Rice and Livestock Systems*, jointly organized by the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) and Indonesia's National Research and Innovation Agency (BRIN).

## Bali Hosts Landmark International Agricultural Forum

The three-day workshop, held June 24–26, 2026, brought together researchers, policymakers, and agricultural experts from across Asia, Africa, and Latin America. Its closing field visit to Jatiluwih was deliberately chosen to showcase one of the world's most enduring examples of sustainable community-managed agriculture.

Participants observed red rice cultivation as part of an integrated food systems program and gained firsthand insight into how the subak system coordinates water distribution across thousands of rice paddies — guided not just by engineering, but by centuries of spiritual and communal tradition.

## Subak: A Living Blueprint for Sustainable Farming

Jatiluwih's rice terraces were inscribed as a UNESCO World Cultural Landscape in 2012, recognizing the subak irrigation system as a masterpiece of human ingenuity that balances agricultural productivity with environmental stewardship and community social life.

The forum highlighted Bali's Tri Hita Karana philosophy — the principle of harmony between humans and God, humans and fellow humans, and humans and nature — as a practical framework for sustainable food systems that the global community can adapt. FAO Indonesia has formally recognized Tri Hita Karana as a model for agricultural transformation, demonstrating how deeply rooted local values can anchor food production that is both productive and ecologically sound.

## Bridging Global Climate Commitments with Farmer-Level Action

BRIN, which co-hosted the forum, stated that the event was designed to close the gap between high-level global emission reduction pledges and on-the-ground implementation at the farm level. Rice paddies, paddy soils, and livestock are major sources of agricultural greenhouse gas emissions, making this sector critical to meeting global climate targets.

In this context, the subak system of Bali offers rare and valuable lessons: how a community of smallholder farmers can collectively manage water resources, maintain soil health, and preserve biodiversity — largely without dependence on chemical inputs or industrial-scale mechanization.

## Global Recognition of Local Wisdom

The presence of delegates from 16 nations at Jatiluwih underscores the growing global appetite for indigenous and community-based agricultural knowledge as a resource for climate adaptation. As industrial farming increasingly struggles with soil degradation, water scarcity, and vulnerability to climate shocks, systems like subak are attracting serious attention from governments and international organizations alike.

The FAO-BRIN collaboration is expected to yield joint research programs, knowledge-sharing networks, and policy recommendations that integrate traditional agricultural wisdom with modern low-emission technologies — promoting Indonesia's rich agricultural heritage on the world stage while delivering practical tools for farmers facing climate change globally.
