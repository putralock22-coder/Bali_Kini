Behind the glittering image of Bali as a world-class travel destination lies a persistent structural challenge: **investment on the island remains deeply uneven**. Nearly all capital flows — both domestic and foreign — continue to concentrate in **southern Bali**, particularly in Canggu, Seminyak, Uluwatu, and Ubud, while central and northern regions lag far behind.

Latest data shows that nearly **7 million international tourist arrivals** out of Indonesia's total 15.39 million are recorded in Bali. Yet the economic benefits of this massive tourism footfall have not translated into prosperity across the entire island.

## The Roots of Inequality

The concentration of investment in southern Bali is not a new phenomenon. For decades, tourism infrastructure — five-star hotels, luxury resorts, premium restaurants, and digital nomad coworking spaces — has grown explosively along the Seminyak–Canggu–Uluwatu corridor. Meanwhile, central Bali areas like Tabanan and Bangli, and northern regions such as Buleleng, have received comparatively little investor attention.

This imbalance creates a stark economic divide: communities in the south enjoy direct benefits from tourism revenues, while those in more remote areas remain on the margins.

**The Bali Provincial Government** has been clear that all investment must respect local customs, traditions, arts and culture, sacred sites, environmental sustainability, and intergenerational principles. While vital for protecting Bali's identity, these requirements also present challenges for investors seeking to enter new territories.

## Special Economic Zones as Equalizers

The central government is actively promoting two **Special Economic Zones (KEK)** as strategic instruments to more broadly distribute economic benefits:

### KEK Sanur — Health Tourism Hub

**KEK Sanur** has shown promising results. By the first quarter of 2026, its cumulative investment realization reached **Rp5.37 trillion** (approximately USD 330 million), generating **5,444 jobs** and attracting **279,804 tourist visits** to the zone.

KEK Sanur is focused on building a **health tourism ecosystem**. Its flagship facility, **Bali International Hospital (BIH)**, has been operating since April 2025, offering a range of internationally accredited specialist medical services.

### KEK Kura-Kura Bali — International Finance Center

**KEK Kura-Kura Bali** is being developed as an international financial center for eastern Indonesia. As of Q1 2026, it has realized **Rp1.62 trillion** in investment with **2,146 workers** employed. At full operation in 2052, total projected investment in this zone reaches a staggering **Rp104.4 trillion**.

## National Tourism Investment Target

The Ministry of Tourism (Kemenpar) has set an ambitious national tourism investment target of **Rp63.5 trillion** for 2026. This comes with growing emphasis on directing capital beyond Bali toward other priority destinations across the archipelago — a recognition that Bali alone cannot, and should not, carry the full weight of Indonesia's tourism economy.

## Bali's Economic Outlook Remains Solid

Despite the equity challenge, **Bank Indonesia's Bali representative** projects the island's economic growth in 2026 will hold firm at **5.4–5.9 percent** — a resilient performance amid global geopolitical uncertainty. BI Bali Head **Achris Sarwani** noted that Bali's strong regional competitiveness and supportive government incentives continue to attract investor interest.

## The Long Road Ahead

Economists and development observers agree that meaningful investment redistribution requires a comprehensive approach: improving road infrastructure and connectivity in central and northern Bali, developing alternative tourism products beyond southern beaches, and building local human capital capacity.

Without a deliberate equalization strategy, Bali risks a dual failure: saturation and over-development in the congested south, and persistent underutilization of the island's vast potential in the north and center.
