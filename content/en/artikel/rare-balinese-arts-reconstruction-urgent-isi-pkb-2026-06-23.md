## An Endangered Heritage: Bali's Rare Arts on the Brink

Behind the spectacular performances of the 18th Bali Arts Festival (PKB XVIII 2026) that captivate thousands of spectators every evening lies a sobering reality: hundreds of rare and ancient Balinese art forms are on the verge of extinction. **Prof. Ida Ayu Trisnawati**, a senior academic and Professor at the Institut Seni Indonesia (ISI) Bali, has issued a stark warning that the reconstruction of these rare art forms is urgently needed and can no longer be delayed.

"Reconstructing rare arts is not merely about reviving lost performance forms," Prof. Trisnawati explained during a discussion held on the sidelines of PKB XVIII 2026. "It is an academic and cultural process of understanding the philosophy, artistic structure, and social-cultural functions that underlie the emergence of these art forms."

## Six Major Challenges Standing in the Way

According to Prof. Trisnawati, at least **six major obstacles** have long hindered the reconstruction of Bali's rare arts:

### 1. Limited Data Sources
Many rare art forms lack adequate documentation. No video recordings, audio archives, or written records detailed enough to serve as reliable references exist. Researchers often can only rely on the fading memories of elderly master artists whose numbers continue to dwindle.

### 2. Inadequate Regeneration of Artists
Rare arts are typically associated with aging masters. The near-absence of artistic regeneration means that the knowledge and skills embedded in these traditions risk being "buried" alongside their last practitioners.

### 3. Shifting Social and Cultural Functions
Many rare art forms were once integral parts of adat (customary) rituals or religious ceremonies. However, as Balinese society modernizes, these art forms lose their original context, and interest in preserving them naturally wanes.

### 4. Insufficient Funding
Art reconstruction demands significant resources—from field research and recording to documentation and training of young artists. Unfortunately, budget allocations for such preservation programs remain severely limited relative to what is actually needed.

### 5. Conflicting Interpretations
Due to sparse documentation, reconstruction efforts often involve divergent interpretations among artists, researchers, and traditional communities. These interpretive conflicts can slow down or even derail the reconstruction process entirely.

### 6. Dominance of Popular Arts
In an era where social media and the creative economy have turned art into a commodity, popular art forms that are easy to package and sell receive disproportionate attention and support. As a result, rare and more complex art forms are frequently sidelined.

## Proposed Solutions: A Bali Art Reconstruction Bank

To address these challenges, Prof. Trisnawati proposed two strategic measures:

**First**, Bali needs to **revive periodic art inventory programs** to continuously monitor the existence and development of rare arts. Up-to-date inventory data would serve as the foundation for all subsequent preservation efforts.

**Second**, the government and relevant institutions should establish a **Bali Art Reconstruction Bank**—a long-term program in which **one to two rare art forms** are researched, reconstructed, and comprehensively documented each year. This bank would function as a living archive that not only stores data but also produces young artists capable of passing these traditions on to future generations.

## PKB as a Platform for Preservation

The Bali Arts Festival, which each year serves as a prestigious platform for thousands of Balinese artists, is seen as a golden opportunity to elevate rare art reconstruction into a more systematic endeavor. PKB XVIII 2026 has demonstrated that Balinese society remains deeply enthusiastic about their traditional arts.

The challenge now is to ensure that beyond the grandeur of the PKB stage, quiet but crucial work is being done by academics, artists, and government institutions to safeguard a cultural heritage that is slowly but surely being eroded by the tide of modernization.
