A powerful call for environmental action rang out in front of the Bajra Sandhi Monument on Jalan Puputan, Renon, East Denpasar on Monday (July 6). Three civil society organizations — **KEKAL Bali**, **Frontier Bali**, and **WALHI Bali** — staged a joint rally under the banner **"Grubug Agung Pulihkan Bali & Indonesia"** (Grubug Agung: Restore Bali & Indonesia), drawing attention to what they describe as a deepening ecological and human rights crisis on the island.

## What Is "Grubug Agung"?

*Grubug Agung* is a Balinese phrase meaning great chaos or catastrophe. The name was chosen to symbolize the ecological emergency now facing Bali as a result of unchecked, profit-driven tourism development. Demonstrators argued that the island's environmental carrying capacity has long since been exceeded — and that policymakers have failed to act.

## Core Demand: Halt Groundwater Extraction

The rally's central demand was an immediate **moratorium on groundwater (ABT) extraction** across Bali. Representatives from KEKAL Bali argued that the tourism industry's insatiable thirst for water has overwhelmed the island's natural aquifers, triggering a freshwater crisis that directly threatens the daily needs of local communities.

Hotels, villas, and large-scale tourism facilities are identified as the dominant consumers of groundwater, operating under minimal regulation. While residents in multiple areas are already struggling to access clean water, commercial exploitation of aquifers continues largely unchecked.

Beyond a groundwater moratorium, demonstrators also called on the Bali Provincial Government to impose a **moratorium on new tourism sector development**, allowing space for the island's ecosystems to recover.

## Symbolic Performances Carry Deep Meaning

The protest featured a series of theatrical performances rich in symbolism. Frontier Bali staged an **acid water-pouring ritual**, representing a democracy they say has soured and turned against the people. Participants also struck the **kulkul** — a traditional Balinese communication drum carved from coconut or hardwood trunks — in unison, sounding an alarm call for the entire community to awaken and act.

## Stop Criminalizing Environmental Defenders

Beyond water, the rally raised another urgent issue: the **criminalization of human rights defenders and environmental activists**. Data presented at the action recorded at least **325 cases of violence** against human rights defenders and environmental activists across Indonesia between 2025 and 2026.

Activists who oppose land conversion, resist ecosystem destruction, or question the legal basis of development permits have repeatedly found themselves targeted with legal threats, intimidation, and prosecution. The three organizations called on the state to protect — not persecute — those fighting for Bali's long-term survival.

## A Crisis Behind the Tourism Gloss

Beneath Bali's dazzling tourism boom, the conversion of agricultural land and green spaces into accommodation facilities shows no meaningful sign of slowing. Environmental researchers warn that if current trends continue, Bali risks losing its ecological and cultural identity within a single generation.

WALHI Bali, as an environmental organization with a deep advocacy network, framed the struggle as one not simply about water supply, but about Bali's right to remain livable — for its people, not just for the industry built around them.

The "Grubug Agung" action serves as a sharp reminder to government that behind every record-breaking tourist arrival figure lies a real and worsening crisis eroding the very ecological and social foundations of the Island of the Gods.
