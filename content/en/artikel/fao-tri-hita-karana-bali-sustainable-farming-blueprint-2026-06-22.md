Bali's ancient philosophical framework, Tri Hita Karana, has gained international recognition as a practical model for sustainable agriculture. The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) for Indonesia and Timor-Leste spotlighted the concept during a regional knowledge-exchange workshop on frontier technologies for low-emission rice and livestock systems, held in Sanur, Denpasar, from June 24–26, 2026.

## Global Dialogue in Paradise

The multi-day workshop brought together agricultural experts, researchers, and government representatives from 16 countries to share innovations in rice farming technology centered on farmer empowerment and climate resilience. At the heart of Indonesia's contribution was Bali's centuries-old Tri Hita Karana — a philosophy that has quietly guided sustainable land use on the island for generations.

FAO Representative for Indonesia and Timor-Leste Rajendra Aryal put it plainly: "Tri Hita Karana spans generations and that is a classic example we want the world to recognize."

## The Three Pillars of Harmony

Tri Hita Karana teaches that human well-being depends on maintaining balance across three fundamental relationships:

- **Parahyangan** — harmony between humans and the divine
- **Pawongan** — harmony among people
- **Palemahan** — harmony between humans and nature

These principles are not merely spiritual ideals. They form the operational backbone of Bali's **Subak** irrigation system — a collective water management network recognized by UNESCO as a World Heritage Site since 2012 — enabling thousands of farmers to share water resources equitably and sustainably across the island's terraced rice fields.

## Jatiluwih: A Living Laboratory

As part of the forum, delegates visited Jatiluwih Village in Tabanan Regency on Friday, June 26, 2026, to observe the Subak system in action. Participants watched communally managed red rice terraces irrigated through channels that have functioned without major conflict for hundreds of years — a living testament to how indigenous governance can outperform top-down infrastructure management.

The Jatiluwih UNESCO World Heritage landscape demonstrated firsthand that agricultural productivity, environmental stewardship, and cultural preservation can all coexist within a single, community-driven framework.

## Cutting Methane Emissions by Up to 30 Percent

A key technology gaining traction in Bali's rice paddies is **Alternate Wetting and Drying (AWD)** — a scientifically validated method that reduces methane emissions from rice cultivation by **20 to 30 percent** while also cutting water use significantly. When paired with the Subak system's communal governance model, AWD becomes an adaptable solution other rice-growing nations can realistically replicate.

## Indonesia as a Global Reference Point

The collaboration between FAO and Indonesia's National Research and Innovation Agency (BRIN) is expected to strengthen Indonesia's — and particularly Bali's — standing as a living reference for culture-rooted, climate-smart agriculture. The forum is part of a broader global push to accelerate the transition to farmer-centered, low-emission food systems in alignment with international climate targets.

Bali's Tri Hita Karana, it turns out, may be one of the most timely contributions the island can offer to the world: proof that harmony with nature is not a poetic ideal, but a practical agricultural strategy that works.
