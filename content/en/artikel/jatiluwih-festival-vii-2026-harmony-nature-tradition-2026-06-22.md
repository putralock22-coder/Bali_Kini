The UNESCO World Heritage site of Jatiluwih in Penebel, Tabanan Regency, once again captured global attention as the seventh edition of **Jatiluwih Festival** was officially opened on Saturday, June 20, 2026. Held under the theme **"In Balance with Nature, Inspired by Tradition,"** the two-day celebration reaffirmed the region's commitment to developing tourism in harmony with its ancient cultural landscape.

## A World-Class Destination That Keeps Shining

Jatiluwih is far more than a picturesque backdrop of terraced rice paddies. The area is home to the **Subak** irrigation system — a traditional Balinese water management practice rooted in the Tri Hita Karana philosophy — which UNESCO recognized as a World Cultural Heritage in 2012. Over recent years, its international accolades have continued to accumulate:

- **2024** — Named *Best Tourism Village* by UN Tourism
- **2025** — Ranked in the *Top 100 Green Destinations* globally
- **2026** — Awarded *Leading UNESCO Culture Landscape Tourism Destination* at the Asian Tourism and Hospitality Awards in Kuala Lumpur

These consecutive honors affirm that Jatiluwih is not merely a local treasure, but a cornerstone of Indonesia's global tourism identity.

## A Packed Two-Day Program

The first day of festivities treated visitors to vibrant traditional Balinese performing arts and a local culinary fair. Uniquely, guests were invited to participate in rice harvesting directly in the terraced fields — a rare hands-on experience that connects urban visitors to Bali's agrarian roots.

More than **40 small and medium enterprises (SMEs) from Tabanan** participated, showcasing signature products including Jatiluwih's prized red rice and locally grown coffee. Adding a clever community touch, every Bali Tourism Run participant received a **Rp 25,000 shopping voucher** redeemable directly at the SME stalls — a mechanism designed to channel festival spending straight into local hands.

The celebration peaked on Sunday, June 21, with the inaugural **Bali Tourism Run 2026**, a 5-kilometer trail run weaving through Jatiluwih's iconic terraced landscape. Within roughly one month of registration, more than **1,700 runners** signed up to experience the magic of running through a UNESCO World Heritage setting.

## A National Milestone: Entering the KEN Calendar

For the very first time in its history, Jatiluwih Festival secured a place in the **Karisma Event Nusantara (KEN) 2026** — Indonesia's Ministry of Tourism's prestigious national events calendar. This recognition was warmly welcomed by Tabanan Regent **I Komang Sanjaya**, who described the festival as a powerful new engine for regional economic growth.

"This festival is not just entertainment. It is a vehicle to promote Jatiluwih on the national and international stage while directly stimulating the economic activity of Tabanan's communities," Regent Sanjaya said at the opening ceremony.

## Billions of Rupiah in Economic Circulation

The financial impact of the festival is substantial. The Association of Indonesian Travel Agents (ASITA) Bali estimated that the combined festival and running event would generate **Rp 1.5 billion to Rp 2 billion in economic circulation** — drawn from entry fees, SME purchases, dining, and accommodation in the surrounding area.

The festival was also organized as part of the broader commemoration of **100 Years of Bali Tourism**, lending every activity an extra layer of historical significance and shared purpose among organizers, participants, and the local community alike.

## A Living Heritage, Not a Museum Exhibit

What truly sets Jatiluwih Festival apart is its insistence that cultural heritage must remain alive and relevant, not frozen behind glass. The Subak system underpinning the terraced agriculture here is not merely a photogenic backdrop — it is a living social and spiritual network connecting farmers, water, land, and the divine in one continuous flow.

With its first inclusion in the national events calendar and its growing roster of international awards, Jatiluwih continues to prove that cultural preservation and tourism progress are not in opposition. When done right, they strengthen each other — one harvest season at a time.
