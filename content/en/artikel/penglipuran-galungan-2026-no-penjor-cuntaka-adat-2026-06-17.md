Wednesday, June 17, 2026 — as Bali's streets across the island transformed into corridors of towering penjor bamboo poles for **Hari Raya Galungan**, one beloved village stood apart. **Desa Wisata Penglipuran** in Bangli Regency observed Galungan 2026 without a single penjor lining its main road — a first in the village's recorded history.

This extraordinary moment was not an oversight or a shift in tradition. It was a profound act of communal respect rooted deep in Balinese adat (customary law).

## The Reason: Cuntaka — A State of Ritual Impurity

According to the customary rules (adat) governing Desa Penglipuran, when a resident passes away, the entire village enters a state of **cuntaka** — a period of ritual impurity known as sebel in Balinese — lasting three days. This Galungan, a villager passed away shortly before the holy day, placing the entire community under cuntaka.

Under cuntaka, the community cannot perform normal Galungan prayers or erect penjor — the sacred bamboo poles central to the Galungan ritual. Penjor are not decorations; they are spiritual offerings symbolizing Mount Agung and representing gratitude to Sang Hyang Widhi (the Supreme Deity). Their sacred nature means they cannot be installed during a period of ritual impurity.

"Because penjor are sacred, they were not erected," local adat leaders explained.

## A Historic Moment for Penglipuran

This marks the first time in memory that Penglipuran — consistently ranked among the world's cleanest and most beautiful villages — has observed Galungan without its signature penjor. For decades, the spectacle of hundreds of penjor lining the village's main lane has been one of Bali's most iconic Galungan experiences, drawing thousands of domestic and international visitors each cycle.

This year, that visual wonder was absent. The village lane, usually adorned with janur (young coconut leaves), hanging fruits, rice stalks, and green ornaments, felt different — quieter, yet filled with a humanity that runs far deeper than any decoration.

## Solidarity in Adat: Shared Joy, Shared Grief

The collective decision not to erect penjor reflects Penglipuran's philosophy that joy and sorrow are communal experiences. When one resident grieves, the entire village grieves. Even the greatest celebration must yield to compassion for a family in mourning.

This principle aligns with the **Tri Hita Karana** philosophy — maintaining balance between humans and the Divine, humans with each other, and humans with nature. In this case, the human dimension (Pawongan) demanded genuine empathy, even in the midst of Bali's most important holiday.

### About Penglipuran Village

Located in Kubu District, Bangli Regency, at approximately 700 meters above sea level, Penglipuran is celebrated for its immaculate traditional layout — houses arranged symmetrically along a single main lane, free from motorized vehicles. Its consistent cleanliness and preservation of Balinese architecture have earned it international recognition.

Every Galungan, the village typically hosts thousands of visitors drawn specifically by the penjor spectacle and the authentic religious atmosphere. This year, despite the absence of penjor, the village offered something rarer: a glimpse of Bali's moral compass — a community that stays true to its values even when tradition might look different to the outside world.

## A Lesson for Visitors

For travelers who came to Penglipuran expecting the iconic penjor-lined street and found something unexpected, this is actually a rare privilege. It is an encounter with the real Bali — not curated for tourism, but authentic to its own conscience.

The Galungan-Kuningan period continues through **June 27, 2026 (Kuningan)**. In villages and neighborhoods across Bali, penjor still stand tall and ceremonies unfold with full devotion. Penglipuran will return to its full ceremonial observance once the cuntaka period concludes.
