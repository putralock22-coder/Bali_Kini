A striking paradox is unfolding in Bali's tourism sector — one that challenges the conventional assumption that fewer tourists automatically means less money for the island's economy. Bali Governor Wayan Koster laid out the data at the Joint Commitment Strengthening Forum for the 2026 Economic Census, held at the Wiswa Sabha Building in Denpasar on Saturday, July 11.

## The Numbers Tell Two Contradictory Stories

Governor Koster presented figures showing that total tourist arrivals — both international and domestic — fell by **11 percent** during the March to May 2026 period compared with the same months in the previous year.

Yet simultaneously, the **Hotel and Restaurant Tax (Pajak Hotel dan Restoran/PHR)** — one of the primary sources of regional government revenue — maintained a stable or even upward trend. Star-rated hotel occupancy, particularly at four- and five-star properties, also recorded positive growth over the same period.

"This is an anomaly we need to study carefully so that our tourism policies are genuinely grounded in accurate data," Koster said.

## Soaring Airfares Are Squeezing Domestic Travelers

Koster identified the main driver of the tourist decline: surging airfare prices that have hit the domestic tourism segment hardest, especially family groups.

Round-trip ticket prices to Bali have risen by approximately **Rp 1 million per person**. For a family of four, that translates into **Rp 4 million in extra transport costs alone** — a sum significant enough to push many Indonesian families to postpone or cancel their Bali holiday plans entirely.

### International Tourists Are Unaffected — or Even Benefiting

While domestic visitors are pulling back, foreign tourists are largely insulated from domestic airfare increases. A weakening rupiah against major foreign currencies has, if anything, made Bali a more affordable destination for international travelers. Their purchasing power stretches further, making accommodation, dining, and activities comparatively cheap — a dynamic that helps explain why higher-end hotels and restaurants are still seeing strong revenues.

## The Informal Accommodation Market Is Shrinking

Another layer of the anomaly lies in a structural shift in accommodation preferences. For years, a significant share of Bali's visitors — particularly long-stay travelers — chose to book unlicensed villas or informal private rentals that operated outside the tax system.

That informal market is now contracting. Visitors who continue to travel to Bali are increasingly opting for **formal, licensed accommodations** — branded hotels, registered resorts, and compliant villas that pay the required taxes. This shift is driving PHR collections higher even as overall headcounts decline.

### A Sign That Quality-Over-Quantity Tourism Is Working?

From one perspective, this phenomenon is a validation of Bali's long-stated goal of shifting from mass tourism to higher-value tourism. The provincial government has repeatedly emphasized that it wants fewer but higher-spending visitors, staying longer and choosing regulated accommodation. The rising tax revenue despite falling numbers suggests this reorientation may be bearing fruit — structurally, if not yet in terms of volume.

## Data Must Drive Policy

Koster used the forum to deliver a pointed message to district heads across Bali: policy decisions cannot be made on gut feeling or anecdote. They must be grounded in verified data, which is precisely what the 2026 Economic Census is intended to provide.

"Regional leaders must not speak off the cuff. Policy must be based on data from the Economic Census," he said, underscoring that sustainable and equitable development in Bali depends on moving beyond impressions and into evidence-based governance.
