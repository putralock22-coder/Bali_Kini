The Regional Office of the Directorate General of Immigration in Bali has recorded a significant law enforcement achievement in the first half of 2026. A total of 342 foreign nationals were forcibly repatriated from the Island of the Gods between January and June 2026 — a figure that underscores the government's firm commitment to maintaining order and safety in one of the world's most popular tourist destinations.

## Six Immigration Offices Working in Unison

This crackdown was not carried out by a single unit but involved all immigration offices operating across Bali. The Class I Special Immigration Office at Ngurah Rai International Airport served as the frontline, given that the airport is the primary entry point for international visitors. The Class I Immigration Office in Denpasar also played an active role, alongside the Class II Immigration Office in Singaraja, which covers northern Bali.

Two additional units contributed to the operation: the Class III Non-Port Immigration Offices in Tabanan and Klungkung, as well as the Denpasar Immigration Detention Center (Rudenim), which serves as a temporary holding facility for foreigners awaiting deportation.

## A Range of Immigration Violations

The deported foreign nationals were found guilty of various immigration infringements. Overstaying the permitted duration of their visas was among the most common offences. Many others were caught misusing their visa categories, staying under tourist or visit visas while engaging in activities beyond what those visas permit.

Working without a proper work permit was another prevalent violation. A number of foreigners exploited tourist visas to operate informal businesses — from running surf schools and freelancing digitally to opening cafés and shops without the required business licenses. Additional violations included involvement in fictitious investment schemes and activities that disrupted public order.

## A Firm Stance from the Regional Head

The Head of the Regional Office of the Directorate General of Immigration in Bali, Felucia Sengky Ratna, left no ambiguity in her message: "For those who do not respect the applicable law and disrupt social order, we ensure there is no safe space in Bali."

This statement signals that while Bali continues to welcome millions of international visitors each year, respect for Indonesian law is non-negotiable. The principle of balancing openness with strict law enforcement remains central to managing the foreign community in Bali.

## Context: Bali's Booming Tourism Landscape

These deportations come against the backdrop of Bali's continued tourism growth. In 2025, international visitor arrivals to Bali reached 7.05 million people. As the numbers rise, so does the complexity of immigration oversight. High-profile cases of foreigners working illegally, behaving inappropriately in sacred sites, or engaging in criminal activities have repeatedly made international headlines and raised concerns about the quality and safety of Bali's tourism environment.

The swift deportation of violators serves both as a deterrent and as a corrective measure to protect Bali's reputation as a premium, safe, and well-managed destination.

## A Message for All Visitors

Bali's immigration authorities remind all foreign visitors to strictly adhere to the conditions of their visa, respect local culture and customs, and only engage in activities permitted under their visa category. Any violation, no matter how minor it may seem, can result in deportation and a ban on re-entering Indonesia.

With 342 deportations in just six months — an average of more than 57 per month — Bali's immigration apparatus is clearly in high gear, working to ensure that the island's tourism ecosystem remains safe, sustainable, and welcoming for those who play by the rules.
