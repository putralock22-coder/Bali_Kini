Bali has once again made history. On **June 8–9, 2026**, the Island of the Gods hosted the **Ocean Impact Summit (OIS) 2026** — the world's first high-level forum dedicated entirely to sustainable ocean-based economies. Co-organized by the **Government of Indonesia** and the **World Economic Forum (WEF)**, the event has been widely described as the **"Blue Davos"** of the global community.

President **Prabowo Subianto** personally extended invitations to world leaders at the Davos 2026 forum back in January, underscoring Indonesia's ambition as the world's largest archipelagic nation to lead the global ocean sustainability agenda.

## Why Bali?

The choice of Bali as the venue reflects Indonesia's unique geographic position: an archipelago of more than **17,000 islands** with the second-longest coastline on Earth. Bali, as Indonesia's most recognized face to the world, symbolizes the balance between development, tourism, and nature preservation that the summit itself advocates.

The summit's overarching theme centered on the vital role of oceans and freshwater ecosystems in maintaining **global stability**, food security, international trade, and climate resilience — issues that have never been more urgent in the face of accelerating climate change.

## Thousands of Global Leaders Converge

The summit drew thousands of participants from hundreds of countries, including:

- **Heads of state and government** from multiple regions
- **International political leaders and diplomats**
- **CEOs of multinational corporations** in energy, fisheries, and marine technology sectors
- **Scientists and academics** in marine biology and climate science
- **Environmental advocates** and coastal community representatives

Rather than a platform for declarations alone, OIS 2026 was designed as an **action-oriented forum** — every participant was expected to bring concrete commitments to advance sustainable ocean-based economic development.

## The WEF–Indonesia Partnership

WEF's formal endorsement of OIS 2026 was sealed through the signing of a **Letter of Intent (LoI)** between **Indonesia's Minister of Marine Affairs and Fisheries, Sakti Wahyu Trenggono**, and WEF Managing Directors **Sebastian Buckup** and **Maroun Kairouz**.

This institutional partnership provides the foundation for Indonesia to sustain its leadership role in **blue economy** discussions — a development model that treats the ocean as both an engine of economic growth and an ecosystem deserving rigorous protection.

## Key Agenda: From Climate to Capital

OIS 2026 addressed several critical areas:

### Protecting Marine Ecosystems
Delegates discussed the global target to protect **30 percent of the world's oceans by 2030**, in line with the "30x30" commitment adopted under international biodiversity frameworks.

### Green Investment in the Ocean Economy
The forum pushed for new capital flows into environmentally friendly maritime sectors, including offshore renewable energy (wind and tidal power), sustainable aquaculture, and marine biotechnology.

### Technology and Innovation
Participants explored the use of cutting-edge technologies — including artificial intelligence, underwater sensor networks, and satellite monitoring systems — to protect coral reefs, combat illegal fishing, and track ocean temperature changes.

## Impact for Bali and Indonesia

The Ocean Impact Summit strengthens Indonesia's standing as a **global leader in maritime diplomacy**. For Bali specifically, hosting thousands of high-level delegates over two days delivered immediate economic benefits through hospitality, transportation, and the MICE (Meetings, Incentives, Conferences, and Exhibitions) sector.

More profoundly, OIS 2026 places Bali on the map as a venue for the world's most consequential strategic dialogues — not merely a vacation paradise, but a center for intellectual and policy leadership with global reach.

With two-thirds of its territory covered by ocean, Indonesia has an existential stake in healthy marine ecosystems. The commitments forged in Bali are hoped to mark a new milestone in global ocean governance.
