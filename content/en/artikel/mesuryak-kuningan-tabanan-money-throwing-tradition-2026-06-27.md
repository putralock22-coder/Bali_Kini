On Saturday, June 27, 2026 — Hari Raya Kuningan — the village of Bongan Gede in Tabanan, Bali was alive with an extraordinary sight: hundreds of villagers scrambling to catch coins and rice raining down from the air. The occasion was **Mesuryak**, one of Bali's most distinctive and joyful ceremonial traditions, observed only once every six months during the Kuningan holy day.

## The Spiritual Meaning of Mesuryak

The word *mesuryak* comes from the Balinese word for shouting or cheering with great joy — and that is precisely the atmosphere that fills Bongan Gede on this sacred day. The tradition is rooted in the Hindu Balinese belief that ancestral spirits descend to earth during the Galungan holiday period and return to Suarga Loka (the realm of heaven) on Kuningan, exactly ten days later.

As a farewell offering, families gather and toss coins, paper money, and rice into the air as symbolic provisions for their ancestors' journey back to the afterlife. The act of throwing — and then joyfully scrambling to catch — the offerings is an expression of love, gratitude, and spiritual generosity.

## A Festival of Community

The 2026 celebration drew an especially vibrant crowd. Young and old alike filled the village courtyards and designated ceremonial spots as the morning sun climbed higher. The moment coins and grains of rice flew into the air, the crowd erupted with shouts and laughter, hands reaching skyward in a spirit of shared celebration.

The ceremony typically begins around 9:00 AM local time and must conclude before noon. This timing is intentional: in Balinese belief, ancestral spirits officially depart for the heavenly realm by midday, making the pre-noon window the sacred window for offerings and farewell.

### More Than a Ritual

Beyond its spiritual significance, Mesuryak serves as a powerful social bond. There are no social hierarchies in this moment — neighbors, relatives, and strangers all share in the joy of the scramble. Each family prepares their own offering of money and rice, ensuring that the tradition remains a communal act of devotion rather than a spectacle organized from above.

## A Cultural Attraction with Growing Appeal

Mesuryak has long attracted visitors from outside Bali, both Indonesian and international, who are drawn to its raw, unscripted energy. Unlike many performances staged for tourists, Mesuryak happens the way it always has — organically, in the streets of a real Balinese village.

The Tabanan Regency government has consistently supported the preservation of this tradition as part of the region's cultural identity. Mesuryak is listed in the local cultural heritage registry and regularly featured in Bali's official tourism calendar as an authentic immersive experience.

## Kuningan Across Bali

The 2026 Kuningan celebration — falling on the sacred day of Saniscara Keliwon Wuku Kuningan — was observed island-wide with prayers, offerings at temples, and family gatherings. Villages across Bali marked the occasion with their own ceremonial practices, but few are as visually striking or communally exuberant as Mesuryak in Bongan Gede.

In a world of rapid modernization, traditions like Mesuryak stand as living proof that Bali's spiritual and cultural heritage continues to thrive — not preserved behind museum glass, but breathed into life by the people who carry it forward.
