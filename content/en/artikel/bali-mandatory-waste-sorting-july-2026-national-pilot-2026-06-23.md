A new chapter in Indonesia's approach to waste management has officially begun — and it starts in Bali. On **June 10, 2026**, Indonesia's Minister of Environment and Head of the Environmental Control Agency (BPLH), **Moh Jumhur Hidayat**, joined **Bali Governor I Wayan Koster** and all regents and mayors across the island to formally declare the **Bali 100% Waste Sorting Movement** at a high-level coordination meeting in Denpasar.

The declaration designates Bali as Indonesia's **national pilot project** for 100% waste sorting. Starting **July 1, 2026**, every resident, business, and institution in Bali is required to sort waste at the source.

## Who Must Comply?

The mandate spans every sector of Balinese society:

- **Households**: separating organic, inorganic, and residual waste from home
- **Banjar** (traditional community organizations): serving as frontline enforcers and educators
- **Traditional markets**: managing organic waste from produce and food stalls
- **Hotels, restaurants, and cafes (HOREKA)**: the highest-volume waste generators in tourist destinations
- **Government and private offices** throughout the island

Enforcement operates in layers, from neighborhood to district level, ensuring broad accountability.

## Proof of Concept: Landfill Volumes Down 60%

This declaration did not come out of nowhere — it was built on a foundation of measurable success. Since **April 1, 2026**, Bali implemented a "shock therapy" policy: an **outright ban on dumping organic waste at landfills (TPA)**.

The results were swift and striking. Within weeks, landfill volumes in the **Denpasar and Badung areas dropped by 60 percent**. This proved that with clear policy and community commitment, transformation at scale is possible in a remarkably short time.

Minister Jumhur praised Bali's citizens for their response: public compliance with waste sorting has already reached **87 percent** — an exceptional rate for any new policy. The local government has also distributed more than **100,000 composting units** to households, enabling communities to process organic waste independently at home.

## A Three-Phase Roadmap

The waste sorting mandate is part of a structured transformation timeline:

### Phase 1 — April 2026: Organic Waste Landfill Ban
The shock therapy approach immediately cut landfill intake by 60%. Bali proved the concept works.

### Phase 2 — July 1, 2026: Universal Waste Sorting Mandate
All residents and businesses are required to sort waste at source. Bali becomes the national model for 100% compliance.

### Phase 3 — August 1, 2026: End of Open Dumping
All open-dumping practices at Bali's landfills will be permanently halted — a milestone that will also become a national target for replication across Indonesia.

## Waste-to-Energy Plant Under Construction

Complementing this transformation, Bali is building a **Waste-to-Energy Power Plant (PSEL)** at Pesanggaran, South Denpasar, on a 6-hectare site. The facility, which reached groundbreaking in June 2026, is designed with a processing capacity of **1,000 to 1,500 tonnes of waste per day** — converting what was once a liability into electricity that benefits local communities.

## Protecting Bali's Global Reputation

Behind this policy lies a larger mission: **safeguarding Bali's reputation as a clean, sustainable world-class destination**. The Ministry of Environment and the Ministry of Tourism are working hand in hand, fully aware that litter on beaches and roadsides is not merely an aesthetic issue — it poses a direct threat to the tourism brand that has been built over decades.

By leading as Indonesia's national pilot, Bali hopes to demonstrate that a culture of responsible waste management is both achievable and worth replicating across the archipelago.
