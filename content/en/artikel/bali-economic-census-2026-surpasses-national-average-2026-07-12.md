Bali's provincial government and Statistics Indonesia (BPS) held a commitment-strengthening gathering for the 2026 Economic Census (SE) in Denpasar on Saturday, July 11, 2026. The overall picture was encouraging — Bali outpaced the national average — yet a troubling paradox emerged: the island's three main tourism hubs are the slowest to comply.

## Bali Surpasses the National Benchmark

As of early July 2026, Bali's overall progress on the 2026 Economic Census stood at **43.35 percent**, edging ahead of the national average of **42.75 percent**. This ten-yearly exercise by BPS covers all non-agricultural business units and economic activities across Indonesia, making it the most comprehensive economic snapshot the country produces.

To conduct the census on the ground, Bali deployed **3,774 census officers** — 3,735 for door-to-door data collection and 39 specialists for large enterprises.

## The Paradox: Tourism Capitals at the Bottom

Despite the province's above-average performance, Governor Wayan Koster flagged a concerning pattern: the regions that contribute most to Bali's economy — and whose data is most urgently needed — are the ones falling furthest behind.

Progress in the three key districts as of early July:

- **Denpasar**: 31.38% — lowest in Bali
- **Gianyar**: 32.27%
- **Badung**: 36.65%

These three districts form the so-called **Sarbagia triangle**, the epicentre of Bali's tourism economy and the primary source of Hotel and Restaurant Tax (PHR) revenues. Their underperformance in the census creates a data gap that could undermine evidence-based policymaking for years to come.

## Koster: Policy Cannot Be Built on Guesswork

Governor Koster did not mince words during the reinforcement session. "Regional leaders must not speak without data. All policies must be grounded in accurate economic census figures," he said, directing the provincial government apparatus to step up oversight in the three lagging districts.

Koster emphasized that once the Sarbagia triangle has real, verified data, collecting comprehensive information on tourism businesses — **hotels, villas, guesthouses, travel agencies, restaurants** — will become far more accurate and efficient, enabling sharper policy targeting.

## Why This Census Matters for Bali

The Economic Census is no routine paperwork exercise. For Bali, whose economy is overwhelmingly tourism-dependent, the 2026 results will serve as the statistical backbone for crucial decisions in the years ahead: how to diversify investment beyond South Bali, how to regulate short-term rental accommodations, how to track the true scale of the informal tourism economy, and how to design more equitable regional development.

The data will also help Bali measure the actual employment footprint of its tourism sector — a figure that is frequently cited but rarely verified with precision.

### Time Is Running Out

With progress in Denpasar still below one-third of the target, census officers face mounting pressure to accelerate door-to-door visits and leverage digital channels. BPS Bali praised the provincial government's overall commitment and urged particular attention to Denpasar, which as the provincial capital and economic hub carries an outsized share of the island's business activity.

If the three districts can close the gap in the coming weeks, Bali will be well-positioned to emerge from the 2026 Economic Census with the most accurate and usable dataset in the island's recent history.
