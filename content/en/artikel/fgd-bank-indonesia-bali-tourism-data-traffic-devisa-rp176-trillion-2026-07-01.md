Behind Bali's impressive tourism revenue figure of **Rp176 trillion per year** — representing more than 55 percent of Indonesia's total tourism foreign exchange — lie unresolved structural challenges that continue to test the island's hospitality sector. Stakeholders from across the tourism industry joined **Bank Indonesia's (BI) Bali Representative Office** for a Focus Group Discussion (FGD) to diagnose the problems and chart a path forward.

## Strong Macro Numbers, Real Ground-Level Challenges

On the surface, Bali's economy appears robust. The island recorded **5.58 percent year-on-year growth** in the first quarter of 2026, while May 2026 inflation stood at a manageable **2.99 percent** — below the national average. Foreign visitor numbers continue to recover steadily.

Yet participants at the FGD were candid: these headline figures mask persistent friction points that, if left unaddressed, could erode Bali's competitiveness as a world-class destination.

## Three Challenges Under the Spotlight

### 1. The Tourism Data Gap

Perhaps the most discussed issue was the **lack of reliable, real-time tourism data**. Currently, visitor statistics are often released with significant delays, siloed across different government agencies, and fail to capture key segments of the informal tourism economy — including independent villas, unregistered homestays, and small-scale local guides.

Without trustworthy data, policymakers struggle to calibrate responses to demand shifts, allocate infrastructure investments correctly, or design promotions that target the right traveler profiles.

### 2. Traffic Congestion Driving Tourists Away

Traffic congestion — particularly in Bali's southern resort corridor spanning Kuta, Seminyak, Canggu, and the Ubud route — was cited as one of the top complaints from visitors. Some tourists reported spending hours in traffic covering distances that should take minutes.

The problem extends beyond inconvenience. In an era of social media reviews and rating platforms, a single bad experience stuck in gridlock can translate into negative posts that reach thousands of potential visitors. **The visitor experience is the brand** — and congestion is damaging it.

### 3. Waste Management Still Lagging

Despite the Bali provincial government's formal declaration mandating household waste sorting from July 1, 2026, implementation at the level of tourism businesses and public spaces still requires sustained oversight and behavioral change. Waste and cleanliness remain recurring themes in tourist feedback about Bali.

## Chinese Market Still Below Pre-Pandemic Peak

On the visitor side, China remains Bali's largest single inbound market at approximately **400,000 to 500,000 visitors annually** — but this is still far below the millions who visited Bali before the COVID-19 pandemic. Recovering the Chinese market, particularly high-spending independent travelers, was flagged as a strategic priority.

## What Experts Recommended

Economist and ISEI Bali representative **Trisno Nugroho**, who presented at the FGD, stressed the urgent need for **cross-agency data integration** as the foundation for sound policymaking. He called for investment in digital tourism infrastructure that enables real-time monitoring of visitor flows and economic impact.

Other key recommendations from the session included:

- Accelerating corridor-based public transportation to ease road pressure
- Strengthening Destination Management Organizations (DMOs) at the regency level
- Creating unified data pipelines between the Tourism Office, BPS Statistics, and private sector operators

## Quality Tourism as the Way Forward

The FGD reflects a growing consensus: Bali cannot afford to continue pursuing visitor volume at the expense of experience quality. With enormous foreign exchange stakes on the table, the transformation toward **quality tourism** — emphasizing longer stays, higher spending, and deeper cultural engagement — is not optional.

Bank Indonesia's Bali office reaffirmed its commitment to facilitating ongoing dialogue among tourism stakeholders as part of the central bank's role in supporting regional economic stability and sustainable growth.
