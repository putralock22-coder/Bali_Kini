Wednesday, June 17, 2026 — Bali has transformed overnight. Thousands of towering bamboo penjor line every road and alley, each one adorned with woven coconut leaves, flowers, fruit, and rice — all pointing skyward in a graceful arc. Today is **Galungan**, one of the most sacred celebrations in the Balinese Hindu calendar.

More than a religious ritual, Galungan is a living declaration of Balinese cultural identity. And this year, thousands of international visitors deliberately timed their trips to witness this rare and profound celebration firsthand.

## What Is Galungan?

Galungan marks the victory of **Dharma (righteousness) over Adharma (evil)**. According to Balinese Hindu belief, on this day the spirits of deceased ancestors descend to earth to visit their families, before returning to the spirit realm ten days later during **Kuningan** — observed on June 27, 2026.

The celebration follows a cycle of **every 210 days**, based on the Pawukon calendar — a traditional Balinese timekeeping system. This means Galungan can occur once or twice within a single Gregorian year.

## The Full Celebration Cycle

Galungan is not a single-day event. Its rituals span several weeks:

- **Sugihan Jawa & Sugihan Bali** — Spiritual purification of self and surroundings
- **Penyekeban** — Bananas are ripened for ceremonial offerings
- **Penyajaan** — Traditional Galungan sweets and snacks are prepared
- **Penampahan (June 16)** — The day before: penjor poles are erected, lawar (spiced meat salad) and satay are prepared, and pigs are slaughtered as a symbolic sacrifice
- **Galungan (June 17)** — The peak day: prayers at the family shrine (*merajan*), followed by temple visits throughout the community. Women carry elaborate offerings; gamelan music fills the air
- **Umanis Galungan (June 18)** — A day for family gatherings, visits, and recreation
- **Kuningan (June 27)** — The closing ceremony: ancestors return to the spirit world

## The Penjor: Galungan's Most Iconic Symbol

No Galungan is complete without the penjor. These 8–10 meter bamboo poles are stripped and bent into elegant arches, then decorated with young coconut leaves (*janur*), seasonal fruits, rice, flowers, and a small shrine at the base.

The penjor is an offering of gratitude to **Hyang Mahadewa**, the divine manifestation who rules the mountains. Every household plants one penjor in front of their gate. When thousands stand side by side along every road, the effect is extraordinary — a living green corridor stretching as far as the eye can see.

The traditional village of **Penglipuran** in Bangli district is among the most visited spots during Galungan. Its famous single paved lane becomes a stunning tunnel of penjor, with villagers dressed in full ceremonial attire.

## Galungan and Bali's Tourism Economy

Galungan 2026 coincides with Bali's **peak tourist season** in June–July. The Indonesian Hotel and Restaurant Association (PHRI) Bali projects hotel occupancy rates to rise **10–12 percent** compared to the previous month, driven by both the travel season and the cultural momentum of the festival.

Australian tourists — consistently Bali's largest visitor group — are among those who actively plan trips around Galungan. They come specifically for the temple processions, village traditions, and an authentic cultural experience that no other destination in the world can replicate.

### Top Cultural Sites During Galungan

- **Pura Besakih** (Karangasem) — Bali's largest temple complex on the slopes of Mount Agung
- **Pura Luhur Uluwatu** (Badung) — Clifftop ceremonies overlooking the Indian Ocean
- **Pura Tirta Empul** (Gianyar) — Sacred spring rituals and purification ceremonies
- **Penglipuran Village** (Bangli) — The world's cleanest traditional Balinese village
- **Ubud** — Center of Balinese arts, home to vibrant community ceremonies

## Bali Today

This morning, before sunrise, the sound of gamelan could already be heard from multiple directions across Denpasar and Ubud. Balinese women walked in procession toward the temples, balancing intricately woven offerings on their heads — a scene that has repeated itself for centuries, and continues to captivate the world.

Galungan was never designed as a tourist attraction. It is the living breath of Balinese society. Yet the presence of respectful visitors who come to witness and honor it has become proof that Bali — in all its cultural depth — remains one of the most meaningful destinations on earth.
