The representative office of Bank Indonesia (BI) in Bali Province has unveiled a strategic plan to map distinct tourism clusters aligned with the natural potential of each region. The initiative aims to redirect economic growth toward Bali's northern, eastern, and western areas — parts of the island that have historically lagged behind the booming south.

## Southern Bali Is Reaching Its Limits

Popular tourism spots in southern Bali — including Uluwatu, Pandawa Beach, and Mertasari — are increasingly showing signs of overcrowding. The strain is no longer merely a logistical inconvenience; it is causing measurable damage to the environment and pushing local infrastructure to its breaking point.

These concerns are amplified by the sheer scale of the tourism sector's contribution to the provincial economy: **tourism accounts for 66 percent** of Bali's economic growth, according to 2024 figures. The island also generates **Rp176 trillion** in foreign exchange for Indonesia — a remarkable 55 percent of the national total of Rp319.9 trillion.

## Three Priority Clusters

BI Bali's mapping divides the island's untapped tourism potential into three geographic clusters:

### West Bali — Jembrana Regency
Jembrana is positioned as a hub for **marine-based tourism** focused on ocean ecosystem conservation. With a largely unspoiled coastline, this region is well-suited for ecotourism experiences centered on marine biodiversity.

### North Bali — Buleleng Regency
Buleleng is earmarked for **wellness and agro-spiritual tourism**. The combination of fertile highlands, expansive plantations, and deeply spiritual local culture makes this area ideal for travellers seeking physical and mental rejuvenation.

### East Bali — Klungkung and Bangli
The Klungkung-Bangli cluster is focused on **cultural heritage and culinary tourism**. The region's rich artistic traditions, historical sites, and authentic Balinese food culture offer a compelling draw for visitors hungry for deep cultural immersion.

## Infrastructure: The Critical Bottleneck

BI Bali acknowledges that infrastructure gaps remain the most significant barrier to realising this vision. Key challenges include:

- Completing an **east-west-north tourism corridor** that connects the island's outlying regions
- The planned **MRT line from Denpasar to Klungkung, Bangli, and Singaraja**, which remains in the planning stage
- Revitalisation of **Gilimanuk Port** (Jembrana), **Padangbai Port** (Karangasem), and **Celukan Bawang Port** (Buleleng)

Without faster progress on these connections, the cluster strategy risks remaining a roadmap with no road.

## A More Balanced Bali

The BI initiative aligns with a broader policy shift away from chasing raw visitor numbers toward a quality-first approach. A more even distribution of tourists across the island's nine regencies would reduce ecological stress in the south while unlocking economic opportunities for communities that have long been left out of Bali's tourism boom.

With the right infrastructure investment and investor incentives, the island's outer regions could prove that Bali's appeal extends far beyond Kuta and Seminyak.
