The World Bank has issued a candid warning about the long-term sustainability of Bali as a world-class tourism destination. In a high-level meeting with Bali Governor Wayan Koster on **May 22, 2026**, World Bank Country Director for Indonesia **Carolyn Turk** presented five critical infrastructure gaps that require immediate and serious attention to ensure Bali remains a global tourism leader.

## A Meeting at the Governor's Office

Carolyn Turk acknowledged Bali's remarkable achievements in attracting international visitors—the island is on track to receive more than 6.6 million foreign tourists in 2026. However, she stressed that this rapid growth is placing enormous pressure on the island's infrastructure and natural environment. Without decisive action, Bali risks compromising the very qualities that make it unique.

## The 5 Gaps the World Bank Wants Fixed

### 1. Wastewater Management and Recycling
Inadequate wastewater treatment from households, hotels, and commercial areas continues to pollute Bali's rivers and coastal waters. The World Bank urged the provincial government to dramatically expand wastewater treatment capacity and enforce stricter standards for developers in tourism zones.

### 2. Solid Waste Management
The volume of waste generated by tourism is growing faster than Bali's current disposal capacity can handle. The island needs an integrated waste management system incorporating large-scale recycling, composting, and advanced treatment technologies. A waste-to-energy plant—backed by Indonesia's sovereign investment platform Danantara—is already being planned, with construction expected to begin in mid-2026.

### 3. Transportation Mobility
Chronic traffic congestion in southern Bali—particularly in Kuta, Seminyak, Canggu, and Ubud—remains one of the top complaints from visitors and locals alike. The World Bank called for accelerated development of integrated, eco-friendly public transportation to relieve gridlock, including electric bus networks and light rail connections between key tourist hubs.

### 4. Clean Water Supply
Competition for water between the tourism sector, agriculture, and local households is intensifying. As more resorts and villas are built, pressure on aquifers and water distribution systems increases. The World Bank recommended sustainable water management policies that balance the needs of all stakeholders and prevent overextraction that threatens Bali's rice paddies and ecosystems.

### 5. Electricity Availability Across Regions
Power supply remains uneven across Bali, with rural areas and smaller surrounding islands experiencing regular outages. The World Bank pushed for faster renewable energy transitions—including solar and geothermal development—to ensure reliable, clean electricity that can support both residents and the tourism industry's growing appetite for power.

## More Than a Tourism Problem

Carolyn Turk was emphatic that these five challenges extend far beyond the tourism sector. "These are fundamental issues affecting the quality of life for every person living in Bali," she said. "Water, waste, and transportation aren't just tourist amenities—they are basic rights. Local communities feel these gaps every single day."

Governor Koster welcomed the World Bank's assessment and reaffirmed the provincial government's commitment to tackling these systemic issues. Several major projects are already in the pipeline, including the expansion of waste management facilities, the development of electric public transit corridors, and a comprehensive water recycling program for high-density tourism areas.

## A Race Against Time

The World Bank's message is clear: **Bali must act now**. Tourism growth is accelerating faster than infrastructure can keep pace. If the island fails to close these five gaps over the next few years, the consequences—environmental degradation, visitor dissatisfaction, and erosion of Bali's premium brand—could prove difficult and expensive to reverse.

Concrete progress is expected to be visible by the second half of 2026, as a number of public-private partnership infrastructure projects move from planning into construction.
