Bali has taken a decisive legal step to protect its vanishing rice fields. The Bali Provincial Government has enacted Regional Regulation (Perda) Number 4 of 2026 on the Control of Productive Land Function Changes and Prohibition of Nominee Land Ownership Transfers. Under this new law, converting agricultural land into villas or tourism facilities is no longer merely an administrative violation — it is now a criminal matter.

## Criminal Penalties for Violators

Citing Law No. 41 of 2009 on the Protection of Sustainable Agricultural Food Land (Articles 72–74), perpetrators who illegally convert designated LP2B (Sustainable Agricultural Food Land) zones now face a maximum prison sentence of five years and fines of up to Rp1 billion (approximately USD 62,000). Governor Wayan Koster emphasized that penalties extend beyond the main investors to include brokers, facilitators, and anyone who assisted in enabling the illegal conversion.

Administrative sanctions remain in place as well — including permit cancellation, revocation of fiscal incentives, and mandatory demolition of any structures built on converted land.

## A Shrinking Agricultural Heritage

The regulation addresses a crisis that has been mounting for years. Official data shows that Bali's total paddy field area had already shrunk to approximately 64,474 hectares by early 2026, down from 70,996 hectares recorded in 2019. That represents a loss of more than 6,500 hectares in just seven years. The annual rate of land conversion is estimated at around 1,254 hectares per year — a figure that poses serious risks to both food security and Bali's cultural identity, which is deeply tied to its iconic subak irrigation system and terraced rice landscapes.

## Cracking Down on Nominee Schemes

Beyond physical land conversion, Perda 4/2026 specifically targets nominee land ownership schemes — a practice in which foreign nationals use local residents' names to register land ownership, circumventing Indonesia's prohibition on foreign land ownership. This legal loophole has long allowed foreign capital to quietly acquire productive rice fields and develop hidden villas. Kompas reported in April 2026 that nominee schemes have become increasingly widespread, prompting Bali to include explicit penalties in this regulation.

### Both the Nominal Owner and the Foreign Party Face Consequences

The regulation makes clear that both the local resident acting as a nominee and the foreign party using the arrangement can face legal consequences. This dual accountability is intended to remove the financial incentive that has made nominee schemes so prevalent.

## Generous Incentives to Keep Farmers Farming

Recognizing that farmers should not bear the regulatory burden alone, Perda 4/2026 includes a package of meaningful incentives for those who maintain their land in LP2B zones. Benefits include a full 100-percent exemption from Land and Building Tax (PBB), priority access to subsidized fertilizers and improved seed varieties, and government investment in irrigation infrastructure and farm road construction.

The government also plans to permanently designate 87 percent of remaining rice fields as LP2B — locking them into protected status and closing off future loopholes for conversion.

## Debate Over Building Height Limits

The regulation has sparked a broader conversation about how Bali can accommodate development without sacrificing its agricultural land. A senior professor of agriculture at Udayana University has called for a review of Bali's existing 15-meter building height limit. If land conversion is genuinely curtailed, development will concentrate on the island's limited non-agricultural zones, making vertical construction increasingly necessary to meet demand without consuming more rice fields.

Perda Number 4 of 2026 represents one of the most ambitious land protection policies in Bali's history — a legal line drawn in the soil to preserve the island's food sovereignty, cultural landscape, and identity for future generations.
