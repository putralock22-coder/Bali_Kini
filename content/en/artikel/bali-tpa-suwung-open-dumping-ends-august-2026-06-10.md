## The Confusion Around TPA Suwung, Resolved

Bali's largest landfill, TPA Suwung in Denpasar, is not being shut down. Indonesia's Minister of Environment and Head of the Environmental Control Agency (BPLH), Moh Jumhur Hidayat, cleared up the confusion during a working visit to Denpasar on Tuesday, June 9, 2026.

"We are not closing landfills across Indonesia. What we are stopping is open dumping — the collect, transport, dump cycle," Jumhur stated at a community tree-planting and environmental dialogue event at Tukad Bindu, Denpasar.

A widespread misconception had been building that TPA Suwung would close entirely and stop accepting waste. The minister's visit addressed this directly.

## What's Actually Changing: Open Dumping → Controlled Landfill

TPA Suwung will remain in operation — but under an entirely different management model: **controlled sanitary landfill** reinforced with geomembrane lining.

The key difference between the two systems:

- **Open dumping**: Waste is deposited and left completely exposed, untreated — leaching toxins into the ground and releasing greenhouse gases
- **Controlled sanitary landfill**: All incoming waste must first be sorted, then covered with compacted soil and geomembrane every 3–7 days, dramatically reducing environmental contamination

### TPA Suwung: By the Numbers

Bali's largest landfill currently stands **10 stories high** and covers an area of **32 hectares**. Since April 2026, it has ceased accepting organic waste, now only receiving residual material that cannot be recycled or composted.

## The Deadline: August 1, 2026

Starting **August 1, 2026**, Bali will permanently end all open dumping operations. The government is preparing a replacement facility — a **Waste-to-Energy (PSEL) plant in the Benoa area** — that converts high-calorie waste into alternative fuel to replace coal in industrial processes.

During his visit, Minister Jumhur personally inspected the PSEL site to verify operational readiness and the availability of waste transport trucks ahead of the transition deadline.

## 71% of Bali Residents Already Sort Their Waste

One of the most encouraging statistics from the minister's visit: approximately **71 percent of residents** in Denpasar and Badung regency already voluntarily sort their household waste. This grassroots behavioral shift is foundational to the success of Bali's transition away from open dumping.

### No Business in Waste Management

Jumhur also emphasized a key principle: waste management must not become a revenue opportunity for officials. Every program must serve the public interest and protect Bali's environment — and by extension, its reputation as a world-class tourism destination that values cleanliness and sustainability.

Bali's pivot to modern, controlled waste management is being watched across Indonesia as a model for other provinces to follow.
