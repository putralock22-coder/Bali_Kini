## A UNESCO Heritage Site Comes Alive

Spread across the spectacular rice terraces that UNESCO officially recognized as a **World Cultural Heritage Site**, the seventh edition of Jatiluwih Festival unfolded over two full days — June 20 and 21, 2026 — at Desa Wisata Jatiluwih, Penebel District, Tabanan Regency. But this year carried an extra distinction: for the first time in its history, the festival successfully passed a rigorous curation process and was officially listed on the **Karisma Event Nusantara (KEN) 2026** calendar — Indonesia's top 125 national events, recognized by the Ministry of Tourism.

Tabanan Regent I Komang Gede Sanjaya described the achievement as a **"new engine of economic growth"** for a regency long celebrated as Bali's rice bowl.

## Theme: In Balance with Nature, Inspired by Tradition

Jatiluwih Festival VII was anchored by the theme **"In Balance with Nature, Inspired by Tradition"**. This is not merely a marketing tagline. It reflects the very essence of the subak irrigation system — a centuries-old cooperative water management tradition grounded in Tri Hita Karana values: harmony between humanity, nature, and the divine.

The festival was conceived as an authentic platform that brings together diverse stakeholders — farmers, SME operators, traditional artists, cultural custodians, and tourists — in a single, organically vibrant space.

## Sport Tourism: 2,000 Runners Through the Paddy Fields

The headline attraction of this year's festival was the **Bali Tourism Run 2026**, a 5-kilometer race organized in collaboration with the Association of Indonesian Travel Companies (ASITA) Bali. Approximately **2,000 participants** from running communities across Bali and other parts of Indonesia joined the event.

The race route wound through narrow paths flanked by Jatiluwih's stepped rice paddies, with Mount Batukaru providing a dramatic backdrop — making it one of the most visually stunning running experiences in Southeast Asia.

"Sport tourism is a fast-growing trend and Jatiluwih is the perfect stage for it," said Regent Sanjaya.

### Mass Rice Harvest and Traditional Farming Demonstrations

Beyond the run, the festival offered a **mass red rice harvest** — a hands-on experience involving visitors, local schoolchildren, and farmers working side by side in the fields. This participatory harvest moment has become a signature attraction: rare, authentic, and entirely unlike anything available at conventional tourist destinations.

Demonstrations of **traditional Balinese farming tools** added another layer of depth, giving visitors a window into how Bali's ancestors cultivated their land for hundreds of years before mechanization arrived.

The festival grounds also featured a Tabanan SME product exhibition, traditional Balinese performing arts, and various traditional farmers' games that have almost faded from everyday memory.

## Real Economic Impact on the Ground

The festival's economic effects were immediate and measurable. Accommodation occupancy rates across Jatiluwih and surrounding areas surged from an average of **50 percent to full capacity (100 percent)** during the festival days. Visitor traffic to the area climbed sharply, from 50 percent of normal levels to **70–80 percent**.

Local warungs, craftspeople, souvenir vendors, and the full SME chain across Desa Jatiluwih directly felt the economic boost generated by two days of thousands of participants and spectators.

## A Living Heritage

Jatiluwih is not simply a pretty backdrop for tourist photos. It represents **Bali's agrarian civilization** — passed down through generations via the subak system, a communal irrigation framework rooted in mutual cooperation. In 2012, this living cultural landscape was formally inscribed as a **UNESCO World Cultural Heritage Site**.

Jatiluwih Festival exists as an annual affirmation that this heritage is not merely preserved on paper. It is celebrated, reactivated, and introduced to the world through responsible, community-driven tourism that honors the land and the people who work it.
