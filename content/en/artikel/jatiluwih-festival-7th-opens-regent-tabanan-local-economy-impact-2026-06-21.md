The 7th Jatiluwih Festival was officially opened at Jatiluwih Village, Penebel District, Tabanan Regency, on Friday, June 20, 2026. The opening ceremony was led by the Regent of Tabanan, who highlighted the festival's critical role as a driver of the local economy — far more than mere entertainment.

"When many people come to Jatiluwih Village, it certainly impacts the economy, impacts tourism — so this activity is truly a driver of the people's economy," said the Regent of Tabanan in his address.

## Jatiluwih: The World's Best Tourism Village

The setting of the Jatiluwih Festival is extraordinary. The terraced rice paddies of Jatiluwih, part of Bali's legendary **Subak irrigation system**, were recognized by UNESCO as a **World Cultural Heritage Site** in 2012. Beyond that international distinction, the area has also earned recognition as one of the **world's best tourism villages** from various global tourism bodies.

Against this prestigious backdrop, the 7th Jatiluwih Festival ran for two full days, June 20–21, 2026, paired with the **Bali Tourism Run 2026** — a leisure fun run of 5 kilometers winding through the UNESCO subak terraces, targeting 2,000 participants from across Indonesia.

This year also marks a special achievement: the Jatiluwih Festival has been included in the **Karisma Event Nusantara (KEN) 2026** — the Indonesian Ministry of Tourism's program recognizing premier national events. This inclusion dramatically expands the festival's promotional reach across the country.

## Real Economic Benefits for the Local Community

The Regent of Tabanan specifically highlighted the tangible economic benefits flowing to multiple layers of the Jatiluwih community as a result of the festival:

**Accommodation fully booked**: Hotels, guesthouses, and community-owned homestays reported occupancy rates approaching **100 percent**, driven primarily by Bali Tourism Run participants and festival visitors who chose to stay overnight in the village — providing a meaningful income boost for local homestay owners.

**Farmers selling directly to visitors**: Local farmers gained rare direct-selling opportunities for their harvest — especially the **organic red rice** unique to Jatiluwih's subak system — to visiting tourists and festival goers, bypassing intermediaries entirely.

**Local restaurants and UMKM thriving**: Warungs (food stalls), restaurants, and UMKM (small and medium enterprises) around Jatiluwih recorded a significant spike in revenue throughout the festival. Many of these businesses source their raw materials from local farms, meaning the economic impact ripples upstream and strengthens the entire village economy.

## Cultural Performances and Harvest Participation

Beyond the running event, the festival featured a rich program of traditional cultural performances reflecting the deep local wisdom of Tabanan and Bali. Visitors could witness live artistic showcases while engaging directly with subak farming families who have maintained traditional rice cultivation practices for generations.

What made this year's edition especially memorable: the festival coincided perfectly with the **harvest season**. Visitors were invited to participate directly in **rice harvesting** across Jatiluwih's terraced fields — a hands-on, authentic experience rarely available at any other tourism destination in the world.

The festival carried the theme *In Harmony with Nature, Inspired by Tradition*, encapsulating the philosophy of sustainable, community-rooted tourism that has guided the festival since its inception.

## Part of Bali's Bigger Tourism Vision

The 7th Jatiluwih Festival does not stand alone. It forms part of the **Road to 100 Years of Bali Tourism** program building toward Bali's centennial tourism milestone in 2027. The concurrent Bali Tourism Run 2026 is designed to demonstrate that sport tourism and the preservation of culture and nature can advance hand-in-hand — a model Bali is increasingly championing.

With its inclusion in KEN 2026, Jatiluwih is being firmly positioned as a quality tourism destination that does not merely attract visitors, but ensures that the benefits flow directly back to the village community — farmers, vendors, homestay owners, and cultural artists alike.
