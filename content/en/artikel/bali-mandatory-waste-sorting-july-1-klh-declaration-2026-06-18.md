## Bali Opens a New Chapter in Waste Management

Bali is turning the page on its waste crisis. Indonesia's **Ministry of Environment (KLH)** and the **Bali Provincial Government** jointly declared the **Bali 100 Percent Waste Sorting Movement** on June 10, 2026. Starting **July 1, 2026**, every resident and business across the island must sort their waste at the source.

The mandate is sweeping in scope: from **households** in Denpasar's back streets to **five-star hotels** in Nusa Dua, from **small warungs** along Seminyak's beaches to **government offices**, from **traditional markets** to **village community halls** (banjar) — no one is exempt.

## Why Waste Sorting at Source Matters

Source separation is the critical missing link in Bali's waste management chain. When organic, inorganic, and residual waste are mixed together from the start, every downstream step — recycling, composting, energy recovery — becomes dramatically more difficult and costly.

By sorting waste at the point of generation:
- The volume of waste reaching landfills is slashed
- Recyclable materials such as plastic, paper, and glass are preserved for reuse
- Organic waste can be composted and returned to the soil

The effectiveness of Bali's recent shock therapy approach has already proven the concept. When organic waste was banned from TPA Suwung landfill on April 1, 2026, waste volumes in Denpasar and Badung plummeted by **60 percent** within weeks — a remarkable behavioral shift achieved in record time.

## Who Must Comply?

The July 1 mandate covers all sectors without exception:

- **Households** — every family must provide separate bins for different waste categories
- **Hotels and tourist accommodations** — from budget guesthouses to luxury resorts
- **Restaurants and food vendors** — including street food stalls in tourist areas
- **Traditional markets** — market managers are responsible for sorting infrastructure
- **Offices and government agencies** — expected to lead by example
- **Adat (customary) villages** — encouraged to actively monitor and apply traditional sanctions

## Consequences for Non-Compliance

The government is backing the mandate with teeth. The Minister of Environment has advocated for **minor criminal sanctions (tipiring)** against individuals who fail to sort waste or dump it illegally.

Governor Koster is also deepening collaboration with traditional institutions to enforce **customary adat sanctions** at the village and banjar level — a dual-track enforcement approach combining formal legal consequences with deeply ingrained community norms.

### Bali as Indonesia's National Blueprint

Bali's track record of swift, decisive waste policy has earned it the status of **national model** for modern waste management. The July 1 mandatory sorting initiative feeds directly into a larger national roadmap: the complete elimination of open dumping practices across Indonesia, targeted for **August 1, 2026**.

In parallel, construction of the PSEL Denpasar Raya waste-to-energy plant — capable of processing 1,200 tonnes per day — is set to begin July 8, providing the long-term infrastructure backbone to handle Bali's waste at scale.

Bali is showing Indonesia — and the world — that environmental transformation is possible when policy ambition meets community will.
