## Bali Enacts Landmark Law to Protect Agricultural Land

The Bali Provincial Government has formally enacted **Regional Regulation (Perda) Number 4 of 2026 on the Control of Productive Land Conversion and the Prohibition of Nominee Land Ownership**. The regulation is one of the most assertive policies the island has ever adopted to protect its rice paddies and subak irrigation system from the relentless pressure of tourism-driven property development.

The law comes in response to an alarming trend: Bali loses an average of **700 hectares of rice field** every year to conversion for housing, villas, hotels, and tourism infrastructure. Left unchecked, this pace of conversion threatens to erase the agricultural landscape that has underpinned Balinese civilisation for thousands of years within a generation or two.

## Converting Rice Fields to Villas: Now a Criminal Offence

The most striking feature of Perda 4/2026 is its introduction of **criminal penalties** for anyone who converts productive rice paddies into villas, resorts, or commercial developments outside permitted zones. Previously, violations of land conversion rules were handled as administrative infractions, which rarely produced meaningful deterrence.

By attaching criminal liability to illegal conversion, the Bali government has signalled that the era of treating rice field destruction as a minor regulatory infraction is over. Both domestic and foreign investors are now on notice: Bali's sawah are off limits.

## Closing the Nominee Loophole

### A Long-Standing Problem

For years, foreign investors have exploited **nominee arrangements**—using Indonesian citizens' identities to nominally own or control land—as a backdoor method to develop villas and commercial properties on rice paddies that should have been off limits. These arrangements allowed vast tracts of productive farmland to be converted despite existing regulations prohibiting such use.

Perda 4/2026 explicitly outlaws this practice. Nominee-based land transfers are now prohibited, and local governments are required to tighten oversight of all land ownership changes—particularly in subak-controlled zones. The regulation also targets the silent capital flows that have historically driven nominee practices, requiring greater transparency in land transaction documentation.

## Rewards for Farmers Who Keep Their Paddies

Understanding that prohibition without compensation rarely works, Perda 4/2026 pairs its restrictions with a meaningful **incentive package** for farmers who maintain their land within Sustainably Protected Agricultural Land zones (Lahan Pertanian Pangan Berkelanjutan / LP2B):

- **Exemption from Land and Building Tax (PBB)** for registered LP2B paddies
- **Priority access to fertiliser and seed subsidies**
- Participation in organic farming training and development programmes
- Preferential market access for certified organic produce

These incentives are designed to make retaining and cultivating a sawah more financially attractive than selling it to a developer at a windfall price.

## Protecting Subak: A Living UNESCO Heritage

The **subak** system—Bali's ancient communal irrigation network recognised by UNESCO as a World Cultural Heritage site in 2012—forms one of the core justifications for the law. Subak is not merely a historical artifact: it is a living, functioning social and agricultural institution on which tens of thousands of Balinese farming families depend.

When a single paddy in a subak network is converted, the water channels and the social cooperative that manages them can be destabilised, triggering a cascade of further conversions. Perda 4/2026 explicitly positions the protection of subak as a non-negotiable principle of Balinese identity and food sovereignty.

### The Current State of Bali's Rice Fields

Bali currently has approximately **68,000 hectares of rice paddy**—down from 71,000 hectares at the start of Governor Wayan Koster's administration. Of this, roughly **65 percent (around 44,000 hectares)** has already been converted to organic farming under the 2019 Organic Farming Regulation (Perda No. 8/2019). The provincial government targets completing the island-wide organic transition by **2028**.

## A Shield for Bali's Food Sovereignty

Academics and environmental advocates have hailed Perda 4/2026 as a **"fortress for farmers and productive land"** that the island has long needed. The regulation complements earlier legislation and cements Bali's position as a jurisdiction that takes agricultural preservation seriously amid intensifying global tourism and investment pressure.

As awareness of food security and climate change grows internationally, Bali's model of combining subak-based organic farming with strong land-protection regulation is attracting attention as a best-practice approach that could be adopted by other Indonesian provinces—and perhaps developing regions worldwide.
