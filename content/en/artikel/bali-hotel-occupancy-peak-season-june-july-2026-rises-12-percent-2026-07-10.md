Bali's hospitality industry is entering its most anticipated stretch of the year. The Indonesian Hotel and Restaurant Association (PHRI) Bali is projecting a 10 to 12 percent increase in hotel occupancy rates during the June-July 2026 peak season, driven by a strong surge in international visitors who are capitalizing on the favorable exchange rate against the Indonesian rupiah.

## Occupancy Already at 72% by Mid-June

Even before the July peak arrived, data compiled by the Bali Provincial Tourism Office already showed average hotel occupancy reaching 72 percent by mid-June 2026 — outpacing the same period last year and reinforcing the island's continuing recovery trajectory.

PHRI Bali Chairman Tjokorda Oka Artha Ardana Sukawati, widely known as Cok Ace, described the uptick as a strong signal that Bali's tourism market is maturing and gaining even wider recognition among travelers from around the world.

## Foreign Tourists Driving the Surge

This peak season carries a distinctive character compared to previous years: a notably stronger dominance by international visitors. Two key factors are reinforcing each other to produce this effect.

**First**, the weakening rupiah against the US dollar and other major currencies has created a meaningful purchasing power bonus for foreign travelers. Every dollar exchanged yields more rupiah, making accommodation, dining, and activities in Bali feel considerably more affordable for visitors paying in hard currency.

**Second**, the rise in aviation fuel (avtur) prices, which has pushed up airfare to Bali significantly, is weighing more heavily on domestic travelers. International tourists who planned their trips well in advance tend to be better insulated from last-minute fare hikes compared to local travelers who often make spontaneous travel decisions.

## Hotels Holding Room Rates Steady

Despite the surge in demand, most PHRI Bali member hotels have opted not to raise room rates aggressively. This "playing it safe" strategy reflects the still-competitive nature of Bali's hotel market, as well as a desire among operators to protect guest loyalty and maintain Bali's reputation as a destination that offers genuine value for money.

The decision also speaks to the hospitality industry's maturity — Bali's hoteliers are not simply chasing short-term gains, but building a long-term reputation as a destination that consistently delivers an exceptional experience at competitive prices.

## Targeting 95 Percent Occupancy

The optimism within the industry runs deeper than the current 72 percent figure. PHRI Bali has set its sights on reaching occupancy rates as high as 95 percent at the absolute peak of the season, particularly in high-demand areas such as Seminyak, Canggu, Ubud, and Nusa Dua.

If achieved, this would mark the highest occupancy figure in several years and serve as definitive proof that Bali has fully recovered from the pandemic's impact — and that the island can more than hold its own against fierce competition from other Southeast Asian tourism destinations.

## Challenges Ahead

Despite the positive outlook, challenges remain. The significant rise in domestic airfare costs threatens to dampen interest among local travelers, who have historically served as a crucial cushion for Bali's tourism industry during periods when international arrivals were softer. PHRI Bali is calling on the government to prioritize affordable air connectivity to the island, ensuring that tourism growth does not become overly reliant on foreign visitors alone.
