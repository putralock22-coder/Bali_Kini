Bali has launched one of its most aggressive immigration enforcement campaigns in recent memory, targeting foreign nationals who work — or create monetized content — while holding tourist visas. The operation, carried out by **Satgas Dharma Dewata** (Bali's immigration task force), resulted in **62 foreign nationals detained** within just the first three weeks of operations in May 2026.

The violations ranged from working online on tourist visas and overstaying permitted durations, to submitting falsified immigration documents.

## What Counts as Unauthorized Work?

Indonesian immigration authorities have drawn a clear and arguably stricter-than-expected line on what constitutes commercial activity. According to officials, the following are all considered violations when performed by holders of a tourist or visit visa:

- **Sponsored content and brand collaborations** — even if payment comes from an overseas company
- **Portfolio shoots and promotional content** — including content produced in exchange for a free villa stay or complimentary meals
- **Receiving professional or business income** of any kind while in Indonesia
- **Running an online business** from Bali

The critical shift: **whether money changes hands on Indonesian soil is no longer the deciding test.** If a business receives promotional value from a foreigner's content — and the foreigner receives any benefit in return — Indonesian law treats it as commercial activity requiring a proper work visa.

## Hotspot Areas Under Surveillance

Enforcement operations are focused on the island's well-known digital nomad and influencer hubs:

- **Canggu** — coworking cafés and surf-tech scene
- **Ubud** — wellness influencers and creative retreats
- **Seminyak** — lifestyle and fashion content creation
- **Kerobokan** — expat residential zone
- **Uluwatu** — cliff-top villas and surf content

Beyond physical patrols, authorities are now **actively monitoring social media accounts** to identify foreigners posting commercial content while on tourist visas. Reports from local residents have also been used as intelligence in enforcement operations.

## The Legal Path: E33G Remote Worker Visa

For foreigners who want to work remotely from Bali without legal risk, Indonesia offers the **E33G Remote Worker Visa** — a dedicated legal pathway for digital nomads, provided they meet the following conditions:

1. **Work exclusively for companies based outside Indonesia**
2. **Meet the minimum income threshold** set by the Indonesian government
3. **Receive no payments from Indonesian entities**

This visa gives remote workers the legal framework to enjoy Bali's lifestyle while remaining fully compliant with immigration law.

## The Consequences of Getting It Wrong

The penalties for visa violations in Bali are substantial and can have long-term consequences:

- **Administrative fines**
- **Immediate visa cancellation**
- **Detention** at immigration facilities
- **Deportation** to the traveler's home country
- **Re-entry ban** of up to **10 years**
- For repeated or serious violations: **permanent blacklisting** from Indonesia

## Striking the Right Balance

Indonesia maintains that it remains open to global talent and legitimate remote workers who contribute positively to the local economy. The crackdown is not aimed at discouraging digital nomads, but rather at closing legal loopholes that allowed the tourist visa to be routinely misused for commercial purposes.

For anyone planning an extended stay in Bali to work remotely, securing the appropriate visa from the outset — rather than navigating ambiguous grey areas — is now the clear and safest approach.
