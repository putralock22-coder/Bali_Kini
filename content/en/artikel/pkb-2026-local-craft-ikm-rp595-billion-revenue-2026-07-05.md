The Bali Arts Festival (Pesta Kesenian Bali / PKB) has always been the island's greatest annual celebration of culture and artistry. But the 48th edition, PKB XLVIII 2026, is proving it is just as powerful as an economic engine — with data showing that local craft businesses raked in nearly **Rp6 billion in just the first two weeks** of the event.

## The Numbers Tell the Story

Bali's Provincial Department of Industry and Trade (Disperindag) released official figures showing that **124 small and medium craft enterprises (IKM)** participating in the PKB 2026 exhibition recorded a **combined turnover of Rp5,950,675,436** between 13 and 29 June 2026.

That works out to roughly **Rp371 million per day**, shared across artisans selling everything from handwoven textiles to fine jewellery.

"The total revenue from 13 to 29 June 2026 stands at Rp5.950.675.436," confirmed I Ngurah Wiryanatha, Head of Disperindag Bali, citing the figures as proof that PKB delivers tangible economic returns for the island's creative sector.

## Four Categories, 124 Artisans

The 124 participating craft vendors were organised into four product categories:

- **Weaving (Tenun)** — 29 vendors showcasing traditional Balinese textiles including endek, gringsing, and songket fabrics
- **Other Crafts and Products** — 43 vendors, the largest group, covering carvings, basketry, and handmade artisanal goods
- **Fashion (Busana)** — 27 vendors presenting garments incorporating local Balinese motifs and materials
- **Gold and Silver Jewellery** — 19 vendors displaying the island's world-renowned metalworking craftsmanship

Together, these vendors form a living showcase of Bali's craft heritage — and a commercial engine generating real income for hundreds of families behind each stall.

## A Festival That Goes Beyond the Stage

PKB XLVIII runs from **13 June to 11 July 2026**, drawing on the talents of more than **23,936 artists** from across Bali and beyond. The Deputy Minister of Tourism has set an ambitious target: visitor numbers exceeding **1.6 million** over the course of the festival.

The craft sales figures are only part of the economic story. Bali's Department of Cooperatives (Diskop) has been tracking the performance of food and beverage micro-businesses at the festival separately, and that data is equally striking: **UMKM food vendors earned Rp1.88 billion in just the first ten days** of PKB, underscoring the event's reach across multiple sectors of the informal economy.

## Weaving as a Cultural and Commercial Powerhouse

Among all product categories, traditional weaving consistently attracts the strongest interest — both from domestic visitors looking for authentic keepsakes and from international buyers seeking distinctive fabrics to bring home. The craftsmanship embedded in a single piece of Balinese endek can represent dozens of hours of work by skilled weavers, making each purchase far more than a transaction — it is a direct investment in Bali's living cultural heritage.

Government programs encouraging the use of local products in official settings have further boosted demand for Balinese textiles, and PKB provides the ideal platform to connect that demand with the artisans who supply it.

## PKB as a Model for Cultural-Economic Integration

Diskop Bali has described PKB as proof that cultural events and economic development need not be separate tracks — they can reinforce each other. A festival rooted in art and tradition can simultaneously serve as a marketplace, a branding exercise for local goods, and a revenue driver for thousands of small businesses.

As PKB XLVIII heads toward its closing ceremony on 11 July, the economic data emerging from the craft and culinary pavilions suggests that the festival is on track to deliver its most commercially significant edition yet.
