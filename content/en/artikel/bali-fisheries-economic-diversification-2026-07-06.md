The Indonesian government is stepping up efforts to develop Bali's fisheries and marine sector as part of a broader strategy to diversify the island's economy away from its heavy dependence on tourism, which currently accounts for more than **60 percent** of Bali's economic output.

## Untapped Marine Potential

Surrounded by coastline on all sides, Bali possesses enormous natural potential in its maritime resources — yet this potential remains significantly underutilized. The current value of Bali's capture fisheries production stands at **Rp2.88 trillion**, a figure that industry experts believe can grow substantially with the right policy support and investment.

Export data for the **January to May 2026** period already tells a compelling story: fish, crustaceans, and molluscs emerged as **Bali's highest-value export commodity**, capturing a **26.27 percent** share of the island's total cumulative export value. This performance demonstrates that Bali's seafood products have real global market appeal.

## The Risk of Tourism Overdependence

Economists and international financial institutions have long warned about the structural vulnerability of Bali's tourism-heavy economy. The COVID-19 pandemic provided a stark demonstration of this risk: in 2020, Bali's economy contracted by a staggering 9.3 percent — far deeper than the national average — as the tourism industry ground to a near-complete halt.

Even as Bali's tourism sector has recovered strongly, policymakers stress that diversification is not optional but essential to building an economy resilient to global shocks.

## Key Development Strategies

### Spreading Investment Beyond the South

One critical challenge is that approximately **88 percent** of investment in Bali is concentrated in just three southern districts: Denpasar, Badung, and Gianyar. The government is pushing to redirect investment attention toward coastal districts with strong fisheries potential, including **Jembrana, Karangasem, Klungkung,** and **Buleleng**.

### Integrated Maritime Tourism

Bank Indonesia's Bali Regional Office is actively advocating for the development of **marine ecosystem-based tourism** in Jembrana Regency (West Bali). This approach integrates fisheries with sustainable ocean tourism, giving local fishing communities a dual income stream — from their catch and from eco-tourism activities.

### Ministry of Marine Affairs Support

The Ministry of Marine Affairs and Fisheries (KKP) has committed to supporting the Bali Provincial Government in building an integrated maritime economy. Key programs include empowering local fishermen, modernizing fishing equipment, and developing a competitive seafood supply chain.

## Bali's Economy Grows 5.58% in Q1 2026

Despite the structural challenges, Bali's overall economy remained on a positive trajectory. Statistics Indonesia (BPS) recorded **5.58 percent growth** in Q1 2026, supported by Galungan and Kuningan religious holidays and rising tourism activity. Bank Indonesia projects full-year 2026 growth of **5.4 to 5.9 percent** for the island.

The push into fisheries is expected not only to strengthen economic resilience but also to generate new employment opportunities for communities outside the hotel and restaurant sector, which has long dominated Bali's workforce.
