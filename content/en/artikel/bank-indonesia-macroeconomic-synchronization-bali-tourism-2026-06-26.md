Bank Indonesia's (BI) Bali Representative Office has called for strategic **macroeconomic synchronisation** to protect the island's tourism sector from structural vulnerabilities — even as economic growth figures remain encouraging. The central bank's analysis paints a nuanced picture: Bali is thriving on paper, but faces systemic risks that could hollow out that success if left unaddressed.

## Growth Projections for 2026

Bank Indonesia projects Bali's economy to expand at **5.0 to 5.8 percent** in 2026 — comfortably above national growth forecasts. The tourism sector, the backbone of Bali's economy, is expected to contribute approximately **2.9 percentage points** to that growth figure.

In the first quarter of 2026, BPS (Indonesia's Central Statistics Agency) recorded economic growth of **5.58 percent year-on-year**, even as the accommodation and food services sector contracted due to the seasonal low period. International visitor arrivals during the same quarter reached **1.47 million**, down approximately 11 percent from the holiday-heavy fourth quarter of 2025 — a predictable seasonal pattern rather than a structural decline.

## The Tourism-Led Inflation Paradox

Bank Indonesia's most pointed concern is the risk of **tourism-led inflation** — a phenomenon where a surge in high-spending foreign visitors drives up the cost of property, rentals, food, and services to levels that are unaffordable for local residents and domestic tourists alike.

The paradox is stark: Bali risks becoming "too expensive" for Indonesians while remaining "cheap" for international visitors whose purchasing power is anchored in stronger currencies. This dynamic — already visible in areas like Canggu and Seminyak — could accelerate if tourism investment and development continue to outpace regulatory oversight.

The cost of flights to Bali has been identified as a key "physical barrier" that has already pushed the island beyond the reach of many Indonesian middle-class families who might otherwise choose it as their holiday destination.

## Bank Indonesia's Strategic Response

To counteract these risks, Bank Indonesia has outlined a set of economic strategies that require coordinated action from government, the private sector, and local communities:

### Destination Diversification
Development must spread beyond the saturated southern triangle of Canggu, Seminyak, Ubud, and Uluwatu. BI is advocating for investment in under-explored kabupaten (regencies) such as Karangasem, Buleleng, and Jembrana, each possessing significant natural and cultural assets that remain largely untapped.

### Strengthening Local Supply Chains
Tourism businesses are encouraged to source products and services locally — from food and beverages to handicrafts and skilled labour — ensuring that a larger share of tourist spending flows directly into Balinese households rather than being captured by non-local corporations.

### MSME Financing and Development
Expanding micro, small and medium enterprise (MSME) access to affordable credit is central to BI's inclusive growth agenda for Bali. Strengthening the MSME layer of the tourism economy creates a more resilient and socially equitable foundation for the sector.

### Digital Payment Acceleration
Efficient digital payment infrastructure benefits both tourists (who expect seamless cashless transactions) and local vendors (who gain efficiency, transparency, and access to formal financial services). BI is actively working to deepen digital payment penetration across Bali's tourism ecosystem.

## Inflation Remains Controlled

Despite the inflationary risks flagged, BI is confident that Bali's headline inflation will remain within the target band of **2.5 ± 1 percent** throughout 2026. This is underpinned by smooth food supply logistics, adequate stock of key commodities, and strong coordination through Bali's Regional Inflation Control Team (TPID).

Keeping inflation stable is a prerequisite for Bali to remain competitive as a quality tourism destination — affordable enough for Indonesian visitors while being sustainable for its residents.

## The Bigger Picture

Bank Indonesia's push for macroeconomic synchronisation reflects a mature understanding of what sustainable tourism actually requires. It is not enough for visitor numbers to grow — the economic benefits must reach the full breadth of Balinese society, the environment must not be degraded, and the island's extraordinary cultural identity must be preserved.

Bali's success story will only hold if the foundations — a stable economy, a thriving local community, and a protected natural and cultural landscape — remain intact for generations to come.
