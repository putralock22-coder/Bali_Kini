Bali took a landmark step in its battle against waste on Wednesday, June 10, 2026. At a ceremony in Denpasar, Environment Minister Moh. Jumhur Hidayat, Bali Governor Wayan Koster, and all regents and mayors across the island jointly declared the **"Bali Towards 100% Waste Sorting"** movement—a comprehensive commitment to transform how Bali's nine regencies and one city manage their daily waste.

## A Unified Declaration

The declaration marks a collective pledge from local governments to ensure continuous improvement in waste management, supported by community-wide participation in sorting waste at the source. In a moment of unified commitment, the leaders proclaimed together: *"We call on all people of Bali—let us achieve 100% waste sorting together, simultaneously, for a Bali that is clean, healthy, beautiful, and sustainable."*

The ceremony was attended by all regional leaders in Bali, representing a rare show of cross-regional coordination on an environmental issue that has plagued the island for years.

## Current Progress and Firm Deadlines

Bali has made measurable progress in waste sorting behavior. As of mid-2026, several regencies and cities report that **76% of residents are already sorting their waste**—a genuine shift in daily habits that reflects growing environmental awareness among the Balinese population.

However, the government has set two binding deadlines to close the remaining gap:

- **July 1, 2026**: All Bali residents must begin sorting waste simultaneously at the source—starting from their own households.
- **August 1, 2026**: All waste disposal sites (TPA) still operating under the **open dumping** method will be permanently closed across Bali, in line with a national policy taking effect simultaneously across all of Indonesia.

## Two Strategic Pillars

Governor Koster has structured the Bali Clean Waste (Bali Bersih Sampah) program around two main pillars. The first is **source-based waste management**, which pushes households, schools, communities, and businesses to separate organic from non-organic waste at the point of generation. The second is the **tightening of single-use plastic regulations**, which continues to reduce the volume of plastic waste contaminating Bali's land, rivers, and coastal waters.

## All Regencies Commit

Karangasem Deputy Regent Pandu Prapanca Lagosa was among the regional leaders present, affirming his regency's full readiness to implement the agreed policies. *"This is not merely a program, but a collective movement to protect nature in Karangasem and Bali for future generations,"* he said, pledging to strengthen coordination with villages, schools, communities, and businesses to make waste sorting a daily habit rather than an occasional effort.

## Long-Overdue Action

Bali's waste crisis has been building for decades. Annual waste generation on the island reaches millions of tons, with Denpasar and Badung as the largest contributors. Without a massive, consistent shift in behavior across all sectors of society, Bali's natural beauty—the very foundation of its world-class tourism industry—risks further degradation.

Today's declaration signals that Bali is serious about protecting its environment not just as a tourism destination, but as a living island with a genuine commitment to sustainability for generations to come.
