Indonesia's Ministry of Tourism (Kemenpar) has formally designated Penglipuran Tourism Village in Bangli Regency, Bali, as the national reference model for tourism village development across the archipelago. The designation was made official during a working visit by Commission VII of the House of Representatives (DPR RI) to Penglipuran Village on Thursday, July 9, 2026 — a milestone that cements the traditional Balinese village's reputation as one of Indonesia's finest examples of community-based sustainable tourism.

## What Makes Penglipuran Stand Out

According to Hariyanto, Senior Expert for Tourism and Creative Economy at the Ministry of Tourism, Penglipuran's success stems from its ability to harmonize economic growth with cultural preservation and local wisdom in a way few destinations have managed to replicate.

"Penglipuran has proven that tourism villages don't have to sacrifice cultural identity for economic gain. The two can coexist and, in fact, reinforce each other," Hariyanto explained during the visit.

Penglipuran is celebrated for several distinctive features: its immaculately arranged traditional architecture, a longstanding prohibition on motorized vehicles along the main village path, an actively preserved bamboo forest, and residents who remain deeply committed to ancestral customs and ceremonies. Together, these elements create an authentic cultural experience that stands apart from Bali's mass-tourism circuits.

## Commission VII Champions Storytelling and Creative Economy

Deputy Chair of Commission VII, Chusnunia Chalim, used the visit to advocate for a broader strategic shift in how Indonesia develops its tourism villages — one that centers on storytelling and creative economy development as competitive advantages in a modern tourism market.

"Tourism villages can no longer rely solely on natural beauty and culture. Today's visitors seek meaningful experiences and stories they can carry with them," said Chusnunia.

### Community Inclusion as the Core Principle

Commission VII particularly highlighted the critical importance of involving local communities across the entire tourism value chain — from accommodation and food to handicrafts, guiding, and cultural performances. The Penglipuran model, where economic benefits flow directly to village residents, was held up as the ideal standard for sustainable tourism that other regions should aspire to replicate.

This approach aligns with the broader national push for inclusive tourism: ensuring that the economic rewards of visitor spending reach the people who maintain the cultural and natural assets that make destinations attractive in the first place.

## ANTARA to Amplify Penglipuran's Story Nationally

State news agency LKBN ANTARA announced its readiness to actively promote Penglipuran as an inspiration for tourism village development across Indonesia. Media support from the country's largest state-owned news agency is expected to extend the reach of Penglipuran's success story to both national and international audiences, potentially attracting more visitors and investors to community-based tourism ventures beyond Bali.

### Toward a Regenerative Tourism Village of World Class

Penglipuran's ambitions extend beyond national recognition. The village is actively working toward becoming a **world-class regenerative tourism destination** — a model that goes beyond mere sustainability to actively restore and improve its social, cultural, and environmental systems over time.

The concept of regenerative tourism represents the next frontier beyond "sustainable" travel: rather than simply minimizing harm, it seeks to leave communities and ecosystems better than it found them. Penglipuran, with its deep-rooted traditions and community cohesion, is seen as well-positioned to lead this transition.

## A Blueprint for 6,000 Tourism Villages

With more than 6,000 registered tourism villages across Indonesia, the potential for replication is enormous — and largely unrealized. By holding up Penglipuran as a concrete, proven blueprint, the Ministry of Tourism hopes to accelerate quality development at other villages that have the raw ingredients for success but lack a clear direction.

The Kemenpar's move also signals a deliberate strategic pivot in Indonesian tourism policy: away from pure arrival numbers and toward quality, community impact, and long-term cultural resilience. In that shift, the quiet lanes of Penglipuran village in the Bangli highlands have become a guiding star.
