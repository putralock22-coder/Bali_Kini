Bali is already one of the world's most visited destinations. Now the Indonesian government and Bank Indonesia want to add a new dimension to the island's appeal: a fully-fledged **International Financial Centre (IFC)**, anchored in a dedicated Special Economic Zone (KEK), that could reposition Bali as a serious hub for global finance — and in doing so, unlock an entirely new category of high-value tourism.

## BI Bali: The Finance KEK Can Supercharge Business Travel

Bank Indonesia's Bali Regional Office has come out firmly in support of the proposed International Financial KEK, with a clear message for the tourism sector: this is not just an economic development story — it is a tourism opportunity.

The Deputy Director of Bank Indonesia Bali explained that designating the island as a financial KEK would catalyse the growth of **business leisure** — a hybrid travel segment where visitors combine professional obligations with leisure experiences. These travellers typically spend significantly more per day than conventional tourists, stay longer, and seek premium accommodation, dining, and activities.

"The financial KEK can boost business leisure by combining tourism and business to attract quality visitors who spend more and stay longer in Bali," the BI official stated. For an island government committed to shifting from high-volume to high-value tourism, this is a highly compelling argument.

## Kura Kura Bali: The Most Likely Home for Bali's IFC

The frontrunner location for the International Financial KEK is **KEK Kura Kura Bali**, located on Pulau Serangan in Denpasar — a 498-hectare zone developed by PT Bali Turtle Island Development (BTID). The zone sits just 10 minutes from Ngurah Rai International Airport, a factor that makes it uniquely attractive for internationally mobile financial professionals.

PT BTID has put forward its **Knowledge District** concept as the strategic centrepiece — an integrated ecosystem designed to bring together financial institutions, education providers, talent development, and innovation infrastructure on a single campus-style development.

By the first quarter of 2026, KEK Kura Kura Bali had already recorded **Rp1.62 trillion in investment realisation** and was providing employment to **2,146 workers** — figures that suggest strong momentum even before the financial KEK designation is formally confirmed.

## Indonesia Aims for Three International Financial Centres

The ambition is national in scope. Coordinating Minister for Economic Affairs Airlangga Hartarto has stated that Indonesia is preparing to establish **three International Financial Centres**, and Bali is positioned as one of the three candidates. The government is currently finalising the regulatory framework and supporting infrastructure needed to make the IFC operational.

If realised, this would place Bali alongside an exclusive group of regional financial hubs — a category currently dominated by Singapore, Hong Kong, and Kuala Lumpur. Bali's combination of natural beauty, cultural richness, and world-class hospitality infrastructure gives it a competitive edge that no purpose-built financial district can easily replicate.

## The MICE Multiplier

The most direct near-term impact of the financial KEK would be on Bali's **MICE sector** — Meetings, Incentives, Conferences, and Exhibitions. MICE visitors consistently rank among the highest-spending segments in global tourism: average daily expenditure for a MICE delegate is typically three to five times that of a leisure traveller.

A Bali-based international financial centre would naturally draw high-profile conferences, investment forums, central bank summits, and fintech gatherings that currently take place in other financial hubs. Each event would bring hundreds or thousands of well-resourced delegates to the island — spending money not only in conference venues and five-star hotels, but in restaurants, spas, craft markets, and cultural tours.

### A Note on Sustainability

Governor Wayan Koster has expressed broad support for the IFC project, while stressing the importance of **legal certainty** and ensuring that the economic benefits flow to ordinary Balinese — not merely to foreign investors and large corporations. Local civil society groups have echoed these concerns, pointing to Bali's history of rapid development that has sometimes outpaced the island's social and environmental carrying capacity.

Striking the right balance — between international financial ambition and Bali's irreplaceable cultural identity — will be the defining challenge as the International Financial Centre project moves from planning into reality.
