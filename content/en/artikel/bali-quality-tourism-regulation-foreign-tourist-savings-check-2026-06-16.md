Bali is drafting what would be its most ambitious tourism management policy in history: a systematic filter for who gets to visit. Under Governor Wayan Koster, the provincial government is preparing a **Regional Regulation (Perda) on Quality Tourism Implementation** — a law that, once passed, would fundamentally change how Bali receives foreign visitors.

At the center of the debate: **the savings accounts of foreign tourists will be checked**.

## The Shift from Quantity to Quality

For two decades, Bali measured tourism success almost entirely by a single metric: how many millions arrived. Annual targets were set, chased, and celebrated when exceeded.

Governor Koster is inverting that logic.

The consistent message from the provincial administration is that Bali doesn't need volume for its own sake — it needs visitors who actually contribute to the local economy. A tourist who spends their entire trip in a budget hostel, eating at the cheapest warungs, and claiming free beach access generates far less economic value than one who stays in locally-owned accommodation, eats at restaurants, hires guides, and buys handcrafted goods.

## What the Regulation Will Screen

Based on information released by Governor Koster, the Quality Tourism Regulation will assess foreign visitors across several dimensions:

### Financial Capacity
Foreign tourists will be required to demonstrate financial sufficiency through their **bank statements — specifically, savings activity over the preceding three months**. Koster has publicly addressed this: *"One of the things we look at is how much money they have in their savings account over the last three months."*

The rationale is practical: ensure visitors have sufficient funds to cover accommodation, activities, and living expenses during their time in Bali, without risk of becoming a social burden.

### Length of Stay
Duration of visit will also factor into the evaluation. Visitors planning unusually short trips or those with visa categories inconsistent with declared purposes will face stricter scrutiny.

### Planned Activities
The types of activities a visitor intends to carry out during their stay will be evaluated. This targets a persistent problem: foreigners who enter on tourist visas but quietly work, teach English without permits, or operate micro-businesses while presenting themselves as vacationers.

## Not Without Precedent

Bali would not be alone in implementing visitor filtering mechanisms. Several premium destinations worldwide already use comparable approaches:

- **Bhutan** charges a mandatory daily fee per tourist, effectively pricing out budget travelers
- **The Maldives** applies protective levies to safeguard its fragile island ecosystems
- **The Galápagos Islands** restricts both visitor numbers and permitted activities

The difference with Bali's approach is its use of financial verification rather than flat entry fees — a mechanism more flexible in theory, but more complex to implement and more diplomatically sensitive.

## Unresolved Implementation Questions

Despite the clarity of intent, several significant questions remain unanswered:

**The technical challenge:** How will savings account verification actually work at arrival checkpoints? Does Indonesia's immigration system have the infrastructure for real-time financial screening of incoming tourists?

**The diplomatic dimension:** The policy could generate friction with high-volume source countries if perceived as discriminatory or excessively intrusive.

**The threshold question:** Governor Koster has not yet disclosed what minimum savings balance would be considered sufficient. Without this number, both travelers and the tourism industry are left planning around an uncertainty.

## The Bigger Signal

Whatever the final text of the regulation looks like, the drafting process itself sends an unmistakable message: Bali is done defining its success by arrival numbers alone.

After years of headline targets and passenger count records, the island is attempting to redefine what a desirable visitor actually means. The draft regulation will be submitted to Bali's provincial legislature (DPRD) for deliberation. Industry stakeholders, traditional communities, and international tourism observers will be watching closely as the specifics take shape.
