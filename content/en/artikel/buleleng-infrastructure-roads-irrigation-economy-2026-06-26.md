The Buleleng Regency Government through its Public Works and Spatial Planning Office (PUPR) is ramping up strategic infrastructure development in 2026, with Rp63 billion earmarked specifically for road and bridge repairs. The investment comes in response to a significant infrastructure deficit in North Bali, where approximately 300 kilometers of regency roads remain damaged and in need of repair.

## A Daunting Scale: 300 Kilometers of Damaged Roads

The scale of Buleleng's road deterioration is striking. Of the regency's total road network, roughly 300 kilometers range from lightly potholed to severely damaged. With current budget capacity, however, the PUPR office can only address around 30 kilometers per year — meaning at current pace, full rehabilitation would take a decade, absent major new funding.

Despite this constraint, the government has moved to prioritize the most economically critical stretches. Among them is the road section in Lemukih village, near the Menara Turyapada area, where ongoing repairs are essential for connectivity to communities in Buleleng's highland interior.

## Two Priority Bridges: Banyuasri and Tejakula

Bridge rehabilitation is equally urgent. The Banyuasri and Tejakula bridges have been designated as priority repair sites for 2026. Both structures are essential links in Buleleng's transport network, facilitating the movement of agricultural produce, fish, and daily goods between coastal and inland communities.

Their deterioration has real economic consequences: delays in transporting fresh catch from Tejakula fishermen or highland farm produce to market translate directly into income losses for thousands of families dependent on these supply chains.

## Plastic Asphalt: Innovation Meets Environmental Solution

One of Buleleng's more innovative responses to the infrastructure challenge is the adoption of plastic asphalt technology — road surfacing that incorporates recycled plastic waste as a binding and strengthening agent. The approach addresses two challenges simultaneously: road durability and plastic waste management.

The method has already been piloted on several road sections, including in Bengkala village and the Pura Segara Rupek area, with early results showing improved road quality and longevity. The regency plans to scale the application across more road projects as the program matures.

## Rehabilitating 35 Irrigation Networks

Beyond roads, Buleleng has launched rehabilitation work on 35 irrigation network sections distributed across the regency. Functional irrigation is fundamental to the agricultural communities that form the backbone of North Bali's economy — particularly in the rice and horticulture belts of the interior.

Supporting this effort is the Tamblang Dam project in Buleleng, which is being developed to strengthen the irrigation system for North Bali while simultaneously providing potential drinking water supply for approximately 300,000 residents.

## Infrastructure as the Engine of Economic Growth

Local government officials have emphasized that the infrastructure investment is not merely about physical construction — it is about unlocking economic potential that has long been constrained by poor connectivity. Better roads open markets to smallholder farmers, reduce transport costs for businesses, and create conditions for investment and job creation.

Buleleng, as Bali's northernmost and largest regency by area, has historically lagged behind the tourism-driven economic centers of South Bali. Addressing the infrastructure gap is seen as the most direct lever for narrowing this development disparity and delivering tangible improvements in quality of life for the regency's residents.

With Rp63 billion deployed this year and a multi-year plan in place, Buleleng is making a deliberate bet that physical infrastructure is the foundation from which broader prosperity must be built.
