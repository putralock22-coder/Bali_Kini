Bali has made history as the first region in Indonesia to break ground on a Waste-to-Energy (WtE) processing facility, known locally as PSEL (*Pengolahan Sampah menjadi Energi Listrik*). But the ambitions don't stop at the Island of the Gods. Indonesia's Environment Minister Moh. Jumhur Hidayat has confirmed that this cutting-edge technology will be replicated across the entire archipelago.

## Bali's PSEL: Processing 1,500 Tons of Waste Daily

The PSEL facility being built across the Denpasar–Badung area is designed to process up to **1,500 tonnes of waste per day**. Construction, which began in July 2026, is expected to be completed by the end of 2027, with commercial operations commencing in the **first half of 2028**. Built with European-standard WtE technology through a partnership with Indonesia's sovereign wealth fund Danantara, the project carries a total investment value of **Rp3 trillion (approximately USD 180 million)**.

"Bali has started — now it's time for other regions to follow," said Minister Jumhur at the groundbreaking ceremony.

## National Rollout: 34 Agglomerations, 60–70 Regencies

The Environment Ministry has mapped out **34 urban agglomerations** encompassing 60–70 regencies and cities across Indonesia as the next priority sites for PSEL development. Danantara CEO Rosan Roeslani announced that **eight additional PSEL facilities** would soon begin construction simultaneously in various provinces.

Despite this ambitious rollout, the true scale of demand is even larger. Minister Jumhur acknowledged that approximately **480 regencies and cities** throughout Indonesia still require this type of waste management infrastructure, highlighting the urgency of scaling up the program.

## Bali as a National Benchmark

For years, Bali's waste crisis — particularly in the densely populated Denpasar and Badung areas — drew international criticism and threatened the island's reputation as a world-class tourist destination. The PSEL project represents a paradigm shift: rather than managing waste as a burden, Bali is now transforming it into a resource.

### Tourism Impact

Governor Wayan Koster emphasized that the PSEL project would significantly boost Bali's standing among environmentally conscious international travelers. As sustainability becomes an increasingly important factor in travel decisions, a cleaner Bali could attract higher-quality visitors and command premium pricing in the tourism market.

## European-Standard Technology

Bali's PSEL adopts the same **waste-to-energy incineration technology** widely used in Germany, the Netherlands, Denmark, and Switzerland. The process involves burning sorted, non-recyclable waste at high temperatures to generate steam, which drives turbines to produce electricity — all while minimizing harmful emissions through advanced filtration systems.

Researchers involved in the project's feasibility study describe WtE technology as an **integrated solution**: it simultaneously reduces landfill pressure, generates renewable energy, and eliminates the need for traditional open-dump sites that are common sources of pollution and greenhouse gas emissions.

## A Cleaner Bali by December 2026

The groundbreaking of the PSEL facility is part of Bali's broader zero-waste ambition, targeting a **complete resolution of the island's waste problem before the end of 2026**. Complementary measures include:

- **Mandatory waste sorting** for all Bali residents, effective July 1, 2026
- **Decommissioning** of the aging Suwung landfill's open-dumping operations
- **Community-scale composting** through the BRIN-developed Lahsamor technology
- **Village-level enforcement** through traditional adat councils (*desa pakraman*)

With construction underway and a national expansion plan in motion, Bali's PSEL journey is becoming a template for Indonesia's broader ambition to transform its waste management system — and for the island itself, a long-overdue answer to one of tourism's most persistent challenges.
