Bali Governor Wayan Koster has called for the agricultural sector to receive equal footing alongside tourism in Bali's development agenda. Speaking at a meeting with the East Indonesia Agricultural Faculty Communication Forum in Denpasar on July 2, 2026, Koster made clear that the current relationship between farming and tourism is structurally unfair to Bali's farmers.

## Farmers Are Not Just Landscape Keepers

Koster highlighted a glaring inequality: the iconic terraced rice fields that draw millions of tourists to Bali each year — generating enormous revenue for the tourism industry — often deliver little financial return to the farmers who cultivate them.

"Farmers must be positioned as the primary actors in agro-tourism development. They cannot continue to be mere guardians of scenery enjoyed by tourists without receiving a proportionate share of the economic benefits," Koster stated firmly.

His remarks address a reality visible across Bali's most photographed agricultural landscapes, from Tegalalang's famous terraces to the UNESCO-listed Jatiluwih rice fields, where farmers earn modest incomes despite their land sitting at the center of thriving tourist economies.

## Two Key Regulations to Protect Farmland

Governor Koster has backed his words with concrete regulatory action through two significant pieces of legislation:

### Organic Agriculture Regional Law
**Bali Provincial Regulation No. 8 of 2019** on the Organic Agriculture System established the legal foundation for transforming Bali's agricultural practices toward environmentally sustainable methods. The regulation encourages farmers to transition from chemical-intensive farming to organic systems that command premium prices in both domestic and international markets.

### Productive Farmland Protection Law
**Bali Provincial Regulation No. 4 of 2026** on the Control of Productive Farmland Conversion directly targets one of Bali's most pressing threats: the unchecked conversion of rice paddies and agricultural land into villas, hotels, and commercial developments. By placing legal restrictions on farmland conversion, the regulation seeks to safeguard Bali's food security and preserve its agricultural identity.

## Bali's Farming Model Goes Global

Before his meeting with agricultural academics, Koster represented Bali at the **Global Policy Dialogue on Sustainable Agriculture** in London on June 24, 2026. The high-level forum brought together policymakers from multiple nations to address climate resilience, food security, and just rural transitions.

At the event, Koster presented the **Subak system** — Bali's traditional communal irrigation network recognized by UNESCO as a World Cultural Heritage site — as a globally relevant model for sustainable, climate-adaptive agriculture.

"The Subak is not merely an irrigation technique. It is a philosophy of life that balances human needs, nature, and spirituality. This is what we want to share with the world," Koster told the forum.

## The Road Toward Agriculture-Tourism Integration

Koster is actively promoting a model of **genuine agriculture-tourism integration** in which farmers become active business partners rather than passive backdrop providers. Practical steps being championed by the Bali provincial government include:

- Developing **community-based agro-tourism** programs that channel direct revenue to farming households
- Pursuing organic product certification to access premium export markets
- Strengthening Subak institutions as both agricultural managers and tourism destination operators
- Training young farmers to market and package their products with cultural value narratives

## Farmer Welfare Remains Under Pressure

Despite the ambitious policy agenda, current economic data reveals the challenge ahead. Bali's **Farmer Exchange Rate (NTP)** for June 2026 stood at **104.47** — a decline of 1.35 percent from the previous month — indicating weakening purchasing power as agricultural commodity prices fall while production costs rise.

This economic pressure underscores the urgency of Koster's reform agenda, which must go beyond regulatory frameworks to deliver tangible improvements in farmer income, market access, and price guarantees.

If successfully realized, Bali's model of dignified agriculture-tourism integration could serve as an inspiring template not only for Indonesia's other nature-and-culture-based tourism destinations, but for agrarian societies worldwide grappling with the tension between heritage and commercialization.
