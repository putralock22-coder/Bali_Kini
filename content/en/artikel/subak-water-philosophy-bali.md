On a quiet morning in the highlands of Tabanan, an elderly farmer walks along the narrow dykes of his rice field. He is not simply checking his crop — he is fulfilling a duty to his community, to nature, and to God. The system that governs his life has existed for more than a thousand years. It is called **Subak**.

## More Than Just an Irrigation System

Subak is often simplified as "Bali's traditional irrigation system." But that is like calling gamelan music merely a "set of instruments." Subak is a **social-spiritual system** that governs the relationship between humanity, nature, and the divine as a single, harmonious whole.

In practice, Subak is a community organisation of farmers who collectively manage water distribution for their rice fields. Every member — called *krama subak* — holds equal rights and responsibilities. Water is allocated fairly. Planting schedules and rice varieties are decided together through deliberation.

No one may take more water than their share. No plot of land may be left untended. In Subak, the common good always takes precedence over individual interest.

## Tri Hita Karana: The Three Causes of Happiness

The soul of Subak is the philosophy of **Tri Hita Karana** — three harmonious relationships that form the foundation of Balinese life:

1. **Parahyangan** — Harmony between humanity and God
   At every Subak rice plot, there is always a *pura ulun carik* — a shrine dedicated to Dewi Sri, goddess of rice and prosperity. Before planting, there is ceremony. After harvest, there is gratitude. The agricultural cycle is a spiritual cycle.

2. **Pawongan** — Harmony among people
   All decisions within Subak are made collectively. Disputes are resolved through dialogue at the *bale subak* (meeting hall). Mutual cooperation is not just a value — it is daily practice.

3. **Palemahan** — Harmony between humanity and nature
   Subak teaches that nature is not a resource to be exploited, but a life partner to be respected. Crop rotation, natural drainage, and efficient water use are evidence of ecological wisdom practised for centuries before the word "sustainability" entered the development lexicon.

## World Recognition: UNESCO 2012

On **June 29, 2012**, the world officially acknowledged what the Balinese had known for centuries: Subak is an extraordinary civilisational heritage. UNESCO inscribed "The Cultural Landscape of Bali Province: the Subak System as a Manifestation of the Tri Hita Karana Philosophy" as a **World Heritage Site** at its 36th session in Saint Petersburg, Russia.

The inscription covered four Subak landscapes across Bali: Pura Taman Ayun, Pura Ulun Danu Batur, the Pakerisan River Watershed, and the Catur Angga Batukaru Subak area.

## The Rice Terraces the World Fell in Love With

The most visible result of the Subak system is the **Tegalalang terraces** in Ubud and the **Jatiluwih rice fields** in Tabanan. These are not simply beautiful landscapes for photographs — they are the result of sophisticated hydrological engineering that enables paddy farming on steep hillsides with optimal water management.

At Jatiluwih, an active Subak system irrigates more than **600 hectares** of terraced fields. Water flows from springs at the foot of Mount Batukaru through channels built and maintained over centuries, cascading level by level from one paddy to the next — not a drop wasted.

## Subak in the Modern Era: Surviving or Fading?

The greatest threat to Subak today is not from nature, but from economics. Many rice fields in southern Bali have been converted into villas, hotels, and housing estates. Young people who once inherited their parents' fields are increasingly choosing to work in tourism.

Yet in many villages, Subak remains alive — not out of obligation, but because communities understand that it is their identity. When the rice fields survive, the culture and customs around them survive too.

## What Subak Teaches the World

In an increasingly individualistic world, Subak offers a simple yet profound lesson: **the most precious natural resources — water, land, air — cannot be managed alone**. They must be guarded together, shared fairly, and treated with respect.

This evening, as you enjoy a cup of Balinese coffee or a plate of nasi campur, remember that somewhere on a terrace in Tabanan or Gianyar, a farmer is tending his field — carrying on a tradition older than the civilisation of many modern nations.

Subak is not merely a relic of the past. It is a mirror for the future.
