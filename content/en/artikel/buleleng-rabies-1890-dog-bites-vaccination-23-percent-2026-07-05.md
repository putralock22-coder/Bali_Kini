Rabies remains a serious and unresolved public health challenge across Bali. The latest data from Buleleng Regency's Agriculture, Food Security, and Fisheries Agency (PKPP) reveals that from **January to June 23, 2026**, **1,890 cases of bites by rabies-transmitting animals (HPR)** were recorded in the regency alone — while the vaccination program has reached only a fraction of the animal population.

## Vaccination Coverage Stuck at 23.91 Percent

Of an estimated **68,748 dogs** across Buleleng Regency, only **16,438 animals** — roughly **23.91 percent** — had received rabies vaccination by end of June 2026. This figure falls far short of the coverage threshold required to establish herd immunity and break the chain of rabies transmission.

Buleleng PKPP Head **Gede Melandrat** stressed that disease control cannot rest on government efforts alone. Active community participation — especially from pet owners — is critical to the program's success.

"Routine vaccination of dogs, cats, and monkeys is the single most important step in reducing the spread of the rabies virus. We urge residents to bring their animals to the PKPP Office or the Agricultural Extension Centers (BPP) across all nine sub-districts," Melandrat said as quoted by Kompas on July 1, 2026.

## Denpasar Shows a More Controlled Picture

The situation in **Denpasar**, Bali's provincial capital, paints a more encouraging picture. By mid-2026, **39,074 dogs** had been vaccinated in the city, with only **4 dogs** testing positive for rabies — a sharp drop from the 24 positive cases recorded throughout all of 2025.

Denpasar's city government is targeting **92.7 percent vaccination coverage** of its estimated dog population of **84,369 animals** for 2026. The strategy combines scheduled vaccination drives at service posts with proactive door-to-door outreach to reach animals that haven't been brought in by owners.

## Bali's Goal: Rabies-Free by 2028

The Bali Provincial Government, working with the Ministry of Agriculture, has set an ambitious target: **making Bali rabies-free by 2028** — three years ahead of the national deadline. The program involves mass vaccination campaigns, free sterilization of stray dogs, and public education on immediate post-bite response.

The Ministry has already held free vaccination and sterilization events in Denpasar as part of this accelerated effort.

## What to Do After an Animal Bite

Health authorities emphasize the critical importance of immediate action after any bite from an animal that may carry rabies:

1. **Wash the wound immediately** with running water and soap for at least 15 minutes
2. **Go to a health center or hospital** to receive Anti-Rabies Vaccine (VAR)
3. **Do not wait for symptoms** — rabies is almost always fatal if treatment doesn't begin before symptoms appear
4. **Report the biting animal** to local PKPP officers to support epidemiological investigation

## Buleleng's Ongoing Vulnerability

The low vaccination coverage in Buleleng reflects the challenge of reaching a large geographic area with a substantial stray dog population. The regency's high bite case count means community exposure risk remains significant throughout 2026.

Pet owners in Buleleng can access **free rabies vaccination services** at the **Buleleng PKPP Office** or at **Agricultural Extension Centers (BPP)** in all nine sub-districts. Vaccinating pets doesn't just protect the animals — it protects families and entire communities from one of the world's most dangerous infectious diseases.
