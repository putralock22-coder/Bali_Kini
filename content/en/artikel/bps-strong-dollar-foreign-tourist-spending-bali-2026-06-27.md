New data from the **Central Bureau of Statistics (BPS) of Bali Province** reveals a notable shift in foreign tourist spending patterns on the island. According to the latest figures, international visitors spent an average of **Rp2.11 million per night** in Bali during 2025 — an increase of Rp60,000 compared to 2024's Rp2.05 million per night. However, the story behind the numbers is more nuanced than a simple uptick in tourist generosity.

## Currency, Not Consumer Behavior

BPS Bali's head clarified that the apparent rise in spending is primarily the result of a weakening rupiah rather than tourists voluntarily opening their wallets wider. The agency uses the average JISDOR (Jakarta Interbank Spot Dollar Rate) published by Bank Indonesia to calculate rupiah-equivalent spending:

- **2024**: Rp15,850 per USD
- **2025**: Rp16,478 per USD

When the same amount of foreign currency buys more rupiah, nominal spending figures in local currency inflate — even if the actual dollar amount spent by tourists remains largely unchanged. As the BPS chief noted, *"Foreign tourists actually benefit from this — their money goes further, giving them access to better facilities and services at a lower effective cost."*

## Above the National Average

Despite the caveats, Bali's per-night tourist spending figure of Rp2.11 million remains **above the national average of Rp2.02 million**, reinforcing the island's status as Indonesia's premier premium tourism destination. Visitors to Bali are consistently willing to pay more than those visiting other Indonesian regions — a reflection of the perceived quality and uniqueness of the Bali experience.

### Shorter Stays

One less flattering data point: the **average length of stay** for international tourists in Bali dipped to **12 days in 2025**, down from 13 days in 2024. While still a relatively long visit by global standards, the decline hints at possible shifts in traveler behavior — perhaps shorter, more targeted holiday trips — that the industry should monitor.

## The Rupiah in Freefall: What It Means for Tourism

The exchange rate story became even more dramatic entering mid-2026. By early June 2026, the rupiah had plunged to a record low of **Rp18,039 per US dollar** according to Bank Indonesia's mid-rate. For inbound foreign tourists, this represents an extraordinary purchasing power advantage: Bali has, in effect, become significantly cheaper for anyone arriving with dollars, euros, or yen.

Luxury resorts, world-class spa treatments, cooking classes, surf lessons, temple tours, and fine dining — all of which were already competitively priced — now represent even greater value in foreign currency terms. Industry players are cautiously optimistic that the weaker rupiah could trigger another surge in international arrivals in the second half of 2026.

## Bali's Tourism Earns Rp176 Trillion in Forex

The macroeconomic backdrop underscores just how central tourism is to Bali's economy. Throughout 2025, Bali's tourism sector contributed an estimated **Rp176 trillion in foreign exchange earnings** — a staggering figure that cements the island's role as Indonesia's top forex-generating destination.

### The Risk of Currency Dependency

Economists and tourism analysts have raised concerns about over-reliance on exchange rate advantages to attract and retain foreign visitors. When the rupiah eventually stabilizes or strengthens, the apparent "bargain" of visiting Bali will diminish. At that point, the island must compete on the strength of its product — its culture, service quality, infrastructure, and sustainability credentials.

This is why Bali's current strategic pivot toward **quality tourism over mass tourism** is not just philosophically appealing but economically essential. By investing in higher-value experiences, unique cultural programming, and sustainable hospitality, Bali can remain attractive even when currency dynamics stop working in its favor.

The lesson from the BPS data is clear: nominal spending figures can mask real challenges. Bali's long-term tourism prosperity depends on building an ecosystem that draws visitors for what the island offers, not merely what the exchange rate makes it appear to cost.
