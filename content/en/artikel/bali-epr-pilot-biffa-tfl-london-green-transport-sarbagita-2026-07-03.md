Behind Governor Bali I Wayan Koster's headline-making presence at London Climate Action Week 2026 lay a series of quiet but consequential meetings that could reshape how Bali handles its two most pressing urban challenges: waste and traffic. During a working visit to London on June 21–22, 2026, Koster sat down directly with three major partners: **BIFFA** (England's largest waste management company), **PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC)**, and **Transport for London (TfL)** — the body that runs one of the world's most sophisticated integrated transport networks.

## Bali Set to Become Indonesia's First EPR Pilot

The single most important outcome of these meetings was the plan to make Bali the **national pilot region for Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)** implementation in Indonesia.

EPR is an environmental policy requiring producers to take responsibility for the full lifecycle of the products they bring to market — from design through post-consumer waste, including the collection and recycling of packaging. In developed countries, EPR has proven highly effective at reducing plastic waste volumes and driving the transition to a circular economy.

Indonesia's Minister of Environment has confirmed that a Presidential Regulation on EPR is in the final stages of drafting, and that **Bali has been selected as the first region to implement it**.

Governor Koster signalled Bali's readiness. "We are preparing a Regional Regulation Bill (Ranperda) on Producer Waste Responsibility that will provide the legal foundation for EPR implementation in Bali," he said, as reported by multiple media outlets.

## Partnership with BIFFA: World-Class Waste Management Know-How

The meeting with **BIFFA** — the UK's largest waste industry giant, handling waste from millions of households and businesses every day — opens the door to technology transfer and expertise sharing for Bali.

BIFFA will share its experience in designing efficient and sustainable waste collection, sorting, and recycling systems. These systems are expected to be adapted for Bali's specific conditions, including the challenge of plastic waste entering the ocean and single-use packaging from thousands of hospitality businesses.

This collaboration builds on Bali's existing single-use plastic ban, in place since 2018 — but now set to be reinforced by an EPR framework that legally binds producers to fund the recycling of their packaging.

## Partnering TfL for Sarbagita's Green Transport Transformation

On the transportation side, Governor Koster invited **PwC** to help design an integrated, environmentally-friendly transportation system for the **Sarbagita metropolitan area** — encompassing Denpasar, Badung, Gianyar, and Tabanan.

Transport for London (TfL), renowned for its electric bus fleet, underground rail network, and integrated cycling infrastructure, shared London's governance model for integrated urban transport — an invaluable reference for Sarbagita's planners. Bali received detailed inputs on financing mechanisms, infrastructure ownership models, and urban mobility development strategies.

In the longer term, this partnership is expected to push the development of electric-powered public transportation along Sarbagita's main corridors — reducing dependence on private vehicles that have long been the primary source of congestion and air pollution across southern Bali.

## Why This Matters for Bali

Bali is simultaneously wrestling with two existential pressures: **waste** and **gridlock**. Neither is merely an environmental issue — both pose direct and growing threats to the island's global tourism reputation.

Partnering with BIFFA and Transport for London signals that Bali is serious about world-class solutions to world-class problems. Piecemeal fixes are no longer sufficient — Bali needs systems designed for a large-scale tourism city and region.

If EPR is successfully implemented, producers marketing products in Bali will be required to fund the recycling of their own packaging. This creates an entirely new revenue stream for Bali's waste management — reducing near-total reliance on the regional government budget.

And if Sarbagita's green transport system takes shape, the face of mobility across Bali could change dramatically — shifting from a sea of private vehicles toward a clean, comfortable, and integrated public transit network on par with leading cities around the world.
