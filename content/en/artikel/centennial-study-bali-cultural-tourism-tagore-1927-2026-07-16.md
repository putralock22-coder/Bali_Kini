Ahead of the centenary of Balinese cultural tourism, the Indonesian Institute of the Arts (ISI) Bali and Bali's Regional Research and Innovation Agency (BRIDA) held a Focus Group Discussion titled "A Centennial Study of Balinese Cultural Tourism (1927-2027)" on Monday, July 13, 2026, at the Kirtya Sabha Mahottama Auditorium, Citta Kelangen Building, ISI Bali. The academic forum reconstructed the history of Bali's tourism development since Rabindranath Tagore's landmark 1927 visit, while charting policy direction for the island's cultural tourism heading into its second century.

## Four Phases of Bali's Tourism Journey

The study presented at the forum identifies four developmental phases in Balinese cultural tourism, based on colonial archives, academic works, and travel documentation triangulated with oral accounts from the speakers.

The first, the embryonic period (1908-1927), was marked by the colonial travel agency Vereeniging Toeristenverkeer — established in 1908 — promoting Bali to European tourists and laying the island's earliest tourism infrastructure. The second phase (1927-1939), the cultural internationalization period, was marked by the arrival of world figures including German painter Walter Spies, Indian Nobel laureate Rabindranath Tagore, and Mexican artist-anthropologist Miguel Covarrubias, who together cemented Bali's global reputation as a center of art and culture.

The third phase (1950-1974) was a period of post-independence national development, marked by the construction of the Hotel Bali Beach under President Sukarno's initiative. The fourth phase (1974-present) is the institutionalization period, marked by the official adoption of the "Cultural Tourism" paradigm integrating art, culture, humanity, and nature — the framework still guiding Bali's tourism policy today.

## Tracing Tagore's Footsteps and Cok Ace's Remarks

Cultural observer and lontar manuscript expert Sugi Lanus, one of the forum's speakers, presented a paper titled "The Dawn of Bali Tourism: Examining the Early Foundations of Physical and Cultural Infrastructure of Bali Tourism from Rabindranath Tagore's Perspective (1927)." He explained that Tagore visited Bali to study its culture, meeting with Balinese nobility across several regencies over August-September 1927.

Former Bali Deputy Governor (2018-2023) and PHRI Bali chairman Tjokorda Oka Artha Ardhana Sukawati, known as Cok Ace, also spoke, underscoring the significance of 1927 as the year Bali gained global recognition. "1927 marked Bali's global recognition through figures like Walter Spies, establishing our centennial framework," he said.

Tourism practitioner Ida Bagus Ngurah Wijaya rounded out the panel, offering an industry perspective on how this historical legacy remains relevant to Bali's present-day tourism challenges.

## A Warning Against Reducing Culture to Commodity

Amid optimism about the coming centennial, several academics at the forum voiced concern over the commercialization of Balinese culture, warning it risks stripping away the island's sacred and spiritual dimensions.

"Balinese culture must not be sold cheaply until it loses its sacredness and spirituality within society," said Dr. Ketut Wica during the discussion. Prof. Made Supartha Utama echoed the concern, cautioning that public and industry attention should not focus solely on the behavioral or ceremonial aspects of culture — such as the ngaben cremation ritual — without understanding the underlying philosophical values.

## Charting the Path to a Centennial

The joint ISI Bali-BRIDA study is designed as a scholarly foundation for shaping Bali's tourism policy going forward, with completion targeted ahead of the cultural tourism centennial in 2027. Its findings are expected to serve as an official reference for the provincial government in crafting policy that keeps culture as the primary basis of tourism, rather than prioritizing short-term economic interests.

The forum comes shortly after the 48th Bali Arts Festival (PKB) 2026 closed with 1.82 million visitors over a month-long run, underscoring that culture remains both Bali's central draw and its central management challenge amid the accelerating commercialization of modern tourist destinations.
