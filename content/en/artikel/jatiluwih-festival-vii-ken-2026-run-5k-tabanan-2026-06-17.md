Exciting news from Tabanan: **Jatiluwih Festival VII 2026** has officially been included in the **Karisma Event Nusantara (KEN) 2026** — Indonesia's Ministry of Tourism's curated calendar of premier national events. It is the first time this festival, held at the heart of a UNESCO World Heritage subak landscape, has achieved this national recognition.

Scheduled for **June 20–21, 2026** at Desa Wisata Jatiluwih, Penebel, Tabanan Regency, Bali, this year's edition is the most ambitious yet. Alongside traditional Balinese performances and UMKM showcases, the festival introduces something entirely new: the inaugural **Bali Tourism Run 5K** — a race through the iconic terraced rice fields of Jatiluwih.

## What KEN Means

Karisma Event Nusantara is the Ministry of Tourism's selective recognition program for high-quality regional events that deserve national-level promotion support. Being listed in KEN opens doors to far wider visitor reach, including international audiences, and signals that Jatiluwih is ready to compete as a world-class cultural and sport tourism destination.

## A Two-Day Experience

### Saturday, June 20 — Culture, Craft, and the Harvest

Day one is designed for immersive cultural experience. Visitors can enjoy traditional Balinese performing arts set against the backdrop of 227 hectares of terraced paddy fields, browse exhibitions from **40 local UMKM businesses**, taste distinctive Tabanan cuisine, and attend Subak educational sessions.

The most memorable activity: a **hands-on rice harvesting experience**. Guests are invited into the fields to harvest paddy the traditional way — the kind of authentic rural encounter that luxury resort-style tourism rarely provides.

Among the products showcased: **Jatiluwih red rice**, a local heirloom variety renowned across Indonesia, and **Jatiluwih highland coffee**, grown on the slopes of Mount Batukaru.

### Sunday, June 21 — Bali Tourism Run 5K

Day two centers on sport: the inaugural **Bali Tourism Run 5K**, organized in partnership with **ASITA (Indonesian Travel Agents Association)** to mark 100 Years of Bali Tourism. The event targets **2,000 runners** from communities across Bali and beyond.

The 5-kilometer route winds through the terraced rice fields — a landscape that UNESCO recognized as a World Cultural Heritage in 2012 for its extraordinary integration of agricultural engineering, spiritual philosophy, and ecological sustainability. Running here, with fresh mountain air and panoramic views of Mount Batukaru, is unlike any urban road race.

The total prize pool is **Rp 70 million (approximately USD 4,300)**. Every participant also receives a **Rp 25,000 shopping voucher** redeemable at UMKM stalls — a smart mechanism to ensure the sporting event directly benefits local vendors and artisans.

## The Subak: More Than an Irrigation System

The Subak system that sustains Jatiluwih's terraced fields is not merely a feat of engineering. It embodies the Balinese philosophy of **Tri Hita Karana** — harmony among humans and the divine, among humans and fellow humans, and among humans and nature.

The festival dedicates significant programming to Subak education: helping visitors understand how this centuries-old cooperative irrigation system has kept the fields productive while maintaining biodiversity and preventing soil erosion. It is a lesson in sustainability that contemporary agriculture is only beginning to rediscover.

## Festival Theme: Balance with Nature

Jatiluwih Festival VII 2026 adopts the theme **"In Balance with Nature, Inspired by Tradition"**. At a time when Bali faces serious overtourism pressures in its southern resort areas, Jatiluwih offers a model worth studying: a destination that grows precisely because it protects — rather than exploits — its natural and cultural assets.

This kind of tourism generates respect, repeat visits, and long-term sustainability. It is, arguably, exactly what Bali needs more of.
