## A Bold Choice: Capping Visitors Instead of Chasing Numbers

Penglipuran Village in Bangli Regency, Bali — consistently ranked among the world's cleanest and most beautiful villages — has made a decision that flies in the face of conventional tourism logic: it is **deliberately limiting the number of visitors and introducing an open-close entry system**. The motivation is not a lack of interest from tourists. On the contrary, it is precisely because visitor numbers have grown dangerously out of control.

Penglipuran Village Manager **I Wayan Sumiarsa** explains that the policy is a direct response to conditions on the ground that have become unsustainable. Over recent months, daily visitor numbers at Penglipuran have surged to **more than 3,000 people per day** — well above what the village can comfortably absorb.

## When Popularity Becomes a Problem

The village's **sustainable carrying capacity is estimated at 1,500 to 2,000 visitors per day**. This figure ensures that guests, residents, and the natural environment can co-exist without one degrading the other. When that threshold is repeatedly exceeded, the consequences become visible quickly:

- **Overcrowding and congestion** along the village's narrow traditional lanes
- **Waste accumulation** that exceeds the capacity of local waste management
- **Environmental damage** to the iconic Petung bamboo groves and green open spaces
- **Disruption to residents' daily life** and adat (customary) practices

Penglipuran is not just a tourist attraction — it is a living village where families still follow centuries-old traditions. Overtourism threatens not just the scenery, but the social fabric of the community itself.

## The Solution: Visitor Packages and Timed Entry

Starting **January 2026**, village management officially introduced a **structured visitor package system**. This isn't simply a quota on numbers — it redesigns the entire visitor experience to be more organized, meaningful, and sustainable.

Under the new system:
- Tourists purchase **time-bound visit packages** that structure their movement through the village
- **Peak-hour crowding** is redistributed across the day through controlled entry slots
- The **open-close mechanism** activates when the daily capacity is reached, temporarily closing the entrance until enough visitors have completed their tour and exited
- With fewer crowds, **authentic interaction with residents** becomes possible again

The result is that while fewer people may enter each day, each visitor goes home with a richer, more genuine experience of Balinese village life.

## Tri Hita Karana: The Philosophical Foundation

Every decision at Penglipuran is guided by the philosophy of **Tri Hita Karana** — the Balinese principle of maintaining harmony between humans and the divine, humans and each other, and humans and nature. For village management, this philosophy is not an abstract concept but a practical framework for decision-making.

"Revenue is certainly important, but the sustainability of our environment and adat traditions remains our top priority," the management affirmed.

This value system positions Penglipuran's visitor cap not as a limitation, but as a commitment to the long-term health of the destination — ensuring it remains genuinely beautiful and culturally intact for future generations.

## A Model for Bali's Tourism Transformation

Penglipuran's approach aligns with Bali's broader strategic shift from **mass tourism toward quality tourism**. As overtourism continues to be a global challenge, Penglipuran's willingness to reduce its visitor targets offers a compelling counter-narrative: the best destinations are not the most crowded ones, but the most thoughtfully managed.

In a region where tourism pressure can erode culture and environment with alarming speed, Penglipuran stands as proof that saying "enough" — and meaning it — is the most sustainable choice a destination can make.
