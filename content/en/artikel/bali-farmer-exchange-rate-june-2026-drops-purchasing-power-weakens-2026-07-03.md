Bali's Statistics Agency (BPS Provinsi Bali) released new data on Friday (3/7/2026) showing the province's **Farmer Exchange Rate (NTP)** for June 2026 stood at **104.47**, a **1.35 percent decline** from the previous month. The figure signals weakening purchasing power among Bali's farming communities and raises important questions for agricultural policy on the Island of the Gods.

## What Is the Farmer Exchange Rate?

The Farmer Exchange Rate (NTP — *Nilai Tukar Petani*) measures the relative welfare of farmers by comparing the **prices they receive** for their agricultural output against the **prices they pay** for both production inputs and household consumption needs.

An NTP above 100 means farmers are in a relatively favorable position — earning more from their products than they spend. When the index dips toward or below 100, farmers face financial pressure. At 104.47, Bali's farmers remain above the break-even threshold, but the 1.35% monthly drop points to a **deteriorating trend** that warrants attention.

## What's Driving the Decline?

Two concurrent forces pushed Bali's NTP lower in June 2026:

**Falling agricultural commodity prices**
Several horticultural commodities saw significant price corrections. Red cayenne pepper prices dropped by **7.65 percent**, large red chili fell **2.63 percent**, and shallots declined **1.45 percent**. These drops compressed revenue on the farmers' income side.

**Rising household and production costs**
On the expenditure side, the prices farmers pay for everyday goods and inputs moved upward. Medium-grade rice prices rose **0.55 percent**, beef increased **0.52 percent**, and garlic climbed **0.09 percent**. Only broiler chicken prices offered relief, falling **0.73 percent**.

The combination — lower selling prices and higher spending — squeezed the NTP downward.

## Bali vs. National Performance

At the national level, BPS recorded Indonesia's NTP at **127.65** in June 2026, edging down just **0.06 percent** from May. The wide gap between Bali's 104.47 and the national 127.65 reflects structural differences in Bali's agricultural sector.

Bali's farmers predominantly work on **smaller plots**, grow more price-volatile horticultural crops, and face a higher cost of living driven by the tourism economy. These factors make Balinese farmers more vulnerable to commodity price swings compared to their counterparts in rice-surplus provinces like East Java or South Sulawesi.

## Real-World Impact

A falling NTP translates directly into household financial stress for farming families. With shrinking income and rising expenses, farmers may cut back on production inputs — fertilizer, quality seeds, hired labor — potentially reducing yields in the next growing season.

Longer-term, persistent NTP pressure accelerates the shift away from farming. In regencies like Tabanan and Karangasem, many farming families already supplement their income through service sector and tourism-related jobs. A sustained NTP decline could further erode Bali's remaining agricultural land as economic calculations increasingly favor other land uses.

## What Needs to Happen

Agricultural authorities in Bali are expected to act on the BPS data through several channels: commodity price stabilization programs, better market access for local farmers, well-targeted input subsidies, and support for high-value agricultural diversification — such as organic produce catering to the growing number of health-conscious tourists.

Bali's NTP, while still above 100, remains considerably lower than the national average. Bridging that gap requires sustained policy attention to the island's agricultural base — the foundation that supports both local communities and Bali's broader cultural landscape.
