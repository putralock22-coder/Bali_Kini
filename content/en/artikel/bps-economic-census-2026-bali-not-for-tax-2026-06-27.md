Every ten years, Indonesia conducts a nationwide **Economic Census** to build a comprehensive portrait of the country's business landscape. The 2026 edition is now underway—and in Bali, it comes with a unique set of challenges that highlight just how complex the island's economy has become.

## The Challenge: Bali's Uniquely Diverse Economy

The head of BPS (Statistics Indonesia) Bali Province has acknowledged that the island's extraordinary diversity of business types presents the biggest hurdle for accurate data collection. Unlike most Indonesian provinces, Bali hosts an unusually compressed spectrum of economic activity:

- **Large enterprises**: five-star hotels, international resorts, multinational hospitality companies
- **Micro-businesses**: street food vendors, home-based craft producers, neighborhood warungs
- **Seasonal businesses**: pop-up operations tied to Bali's festival calendar or tourist seasons
- **Formal and informal businesses**: often blending into each other without clear legal boundaries
- **Digital economy players**: e-commerce sellers, content creators, digital nomads, and remote workers who have made Bali their base

The rapid emergence of new business forms—particularly in the creative and digital economy—makes it difficult for census enumerators to track and categorize them accurately.

## Progress: 19.94 Percent and Counting

Field data collection runs from **June 8 through August 31, 2026**, with enumerators conducting door-to-door surveys of business premises across all of Bali's nine regencies and one city.

As of the latest reporting period, the province-wide completion rate stands at **19.94 percent**. **Buleleng Regency** is leading the pace, benefiting from relatively accessible terrain and a high rate of community cooperation.

## Setting the Record Straight: This Is Not a Tax Audit

Perhaps the most important message BPS Bali wants to convey is this: **census data will not be used to assess, adjust, or investigate anyone's tax obligations**. Period.

The fear that honest disclosure could trigger a tax inquiry has caused some business owners to underreport or avoid census enumerators. BPS officials are countering this directly, explaining that census data is protected by law and used exclusively for national development planning purposes.

The agency also explained why the 2026 questionnaire is more detailed than previous editions: it's designed to capture the digital economy dimensions that simply didn't exist when the last census was conducted in 2016. More questions don't mean more scrutiny—they mean better data for smarter policy.

## Why This Matters for Bali's Future

Accurate census data has real consequences for Bali. The results will inform government decisions on UMKM support programs, infrastructure investment priorities, and economic development strategies for the next decade.

For a province whose economy is heavily concentrated in tourism, good census data could actually help make the case for diversification—identifying non-tourism sectors with growth potential that could reduce Bali's vulnerability to global disruptions like pandemics, geopolitical tensions, or natural disasters.

BPS Bali is urging all business owners—large and small, formal and informal—to give census enumerators accurate information. As the agency puts it: the quality of tomorrow's policies depends on the honesty of today's data.
