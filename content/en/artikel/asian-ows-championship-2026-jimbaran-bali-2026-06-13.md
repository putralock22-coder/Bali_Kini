Jimbaran Bay in Badung Regency became the stage for one of Asia's most prestigious open-water swimming competitions starting Saturday (June 13, 2026). The 12th Asian Open Water Swimming (OWS) Championship 2026 officially opened today, bringing together 78 athletes from 18 countries to compete in the crystal-clear waters of Jimbaran Bay over three days, running through June 15.

## A Growing Field of Nations

The championship originally attracted 17 participating countries, but last-minute additions pushed the count to 18 after Bahrain confirmed its entry with one athlete and two officials. The total delegation across all nations reached 127 people — 78 competing swimmers and 49 supporting officials and coaches.

The competition features three main categories: the 5-kilometer individual race, the 10-kilometer individual race, and the 4x1,500-meter relay. Alongside the elite competition, organizers also hosted an **open series** category for amateur swimmers, sports community members, and the general public, drawing approximately 300 additional participants.

## Indonesia's Six-Strong Contingent

The host country sent six athletes under the banner of PB Akuatik Indonesia. The team consists of Made Nadya and Izzy Dwi Faiva in the women's category, and Andy Fauzan, Alexander Adrian, Akbar, and Sang Arka in the men's category.

Among the standouts is **Izzy Dwifaiva Hefrisyanthi**, who had just completed nearly a full year of intensive training at a camp in France before returning to Indonesia. This championship serves as her first major international test after that European training stint — a real-world measure of how much she has grown during her time abroad.

Rather than chasing medals, PB Akuatik Indonesia's primary goal for this tournament is exposure and competitive experience. Open water swimming presents very different challenges from pool racing, and international experience in real sea conditions is invaluable for the athletes' long-term development.

## Jimbaran Bay: A World-Class Venue

The choice of Jimbaran Bay as the host venue reflects Bali's growing capacity to stage international sporting events. The bay offers relatively calm waters, excellent underwater visibility, and robust surrounding tourism infrastructure that can absorb the influx of athletes, officials, and spectators with ease. Event organizers reported preparations reaching 99 percent completion before competition day — a testament to the meticulous planning behind the scenes.

### Economic and Tourism Impact

Beyond the athletic dimension, the championship delivers tangible economic benefits to Bali. Hundreds of athletes, coaches, and officials from across Asia filled hotels, restaurants, and transport services in Jimbaran and the broader Badung area throughout their stay. The event is estimated to have attracted hundreds of additional tourists who came specifically to watch the open-water races.

The 12th Asian OWS Championship also reinforces Bali's identity as a **sport tourism destination**, aligning with the Indonesian government's policy of using sports events to drive regional economic growth. Bali is increasingly positioning itself not just as a beach and cultural destination, but as a dynamic host of world-class competitive events that attract international visitors year-round.

With the competition running through June 15, all eyes will be on Jimbaran Bay as swimmers from across Asia battle it out in one of the continent's most scenic open-water arenas.
