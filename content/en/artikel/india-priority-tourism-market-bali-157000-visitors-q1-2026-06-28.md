Bali is making a strategic bet on India. As the island's tourism authorities execute a deliberate pivot away from dependence on traditional long-haul markets in Europe and North America, India has emerged as one of the most important priority markets for Balinese tourism in 2026 — and the early numbers back up the optimism.

According to data from Statistics Indonesia (BPS), the number of Indian visitors to Bali reached **157,100** in the January-to-March 2026 period, representing growth of **3.90 percent** year-on-year. Approximately 78.92 percent — some 42,460 tourists — arrived via I Gusti Ngurah Rai International Airport in Badung, with the remainder arriving through sea ports and land border crossings.

## Government Takes the Message to India's Cities

To accelerate the momentum, Indonesia's Ministry of Tourism and Creative Economy dispatched a trade delegation on a **Sales Mission to Ahmedabad and Hyderabad** from June 9 to 11, 2026.

The choice of cities was strategic. **Ahmedabad**, the commercial hub of Gujarat, is home to a rapidly expanding upper-middle-class population with strong purchasing power and a growing appetite for international leisure travel. **Hyderabad**, meanwhile, has established itself as India's Silicon Valley — a city of young technology professionals with disposable income and a preference for experiential, premium travel.

During the Sales Mission, the Indonesian delegation presented Bali as a high-quality premium destination, emphasising its unique blend of cultural depth, spiritual heritage, culinary diversity, and natural beauty.

## Why India Makes Sense for Bali

The renewed focus on India is underpinned by powerful structural factors:

### An Exploding Middle Class

India's population now exceeds 1.4 billion, with a middle class that analysts project will add hundreds of millions of new members within the current decade. An increasing share of these newly affluent Indians are choosing international travel as a lifestyle aspiration — and South and Southeast Asia sit squarely in their sightlines.

### Improving Air Connectivity

Multiple carriers have expanded or are planning new direct routes between major Indian cities (Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru, Chennai) and Bali's I Gusti Ngurah Rai Airport. At roughly 5–6 hours of flight time, Bali is meaningfully closer to India than European or American alternatives — making it a compelling choice for shorter holiday windows.

### Cultural and Spiritual Resonance

Bali holds a distinctive pull for Indian travellers, particularly Hindus. The island's magnificent temples, daily religious ceremonies, and Hindu iconography — from the Ramayana carvings of Prambanan echoed in Balinese dance to the offerings placed at every doorstep — create a sense of spiritual familiarity wrapped in exotic beauty. For many Indian visitors, Bali offers a Hindu experience that is both recognisable and revelatory.

### The Wellness Factor

India is the birthplace of yoga, and Bali is ranked among the world's top wellness destinations. This combination creates a natural marketing synergy: the Bali wellness-and-yoga ecosystem speaks directly to the values of health-conscious Indian travellers, who often seek destinations where spiritual and physical renewal can coexist.

## Strategic Diversification Away from Western Markets

The shift toward India reflects a deliberate recalibration of Bali's tourism strategy. For years, Bali's visitor mix was dominated by Australians, Europeans, and Americans. These markets remain important, but global geopolitical instability, high long-haul airfares, and currency fluctuations have exposed the risks of over-reliance on distant source markets.

The new strategic framework targets **mid-haul markets** — India, China, South Korea, Japan, and Southeast Asia — where flight distances are shorter, growth potential is larger, and economic conditions are more aligned with Bali's push for quality over quantity.

> "Indian visitors show consistent growth trends. They tend to stay longer and their spending patterns are attractive for our tourism ecosystem," said a Bali Tourism Office official.

## 2026 Target: 6.63 Million International Arrivals

Indonesia's Ministry of Tourism has set a national target of **6.63 million international tourist arrivals** for Bali in 2026. With India contributing 157,100 visitors in just three months — and with more promotional activity, flight expansion, and positive word-of-mouth expected to sustain momentum — India's share of the annual total is projected to be meaningfully higher than in previous years.

The emphasis on India also aligns with Governor Koster's **quality tourism** policy, which prioritises visitors who contribute more per capita to Bali's economy while placing less strain on the island's infrastructure. Indian travellers, particularly those in the premium segment targeted by the Sales Mission, fit this profile well.

## Room to Grow

Despite the positive trajectory, 157,100 visitors in a quarter remains a small fraction of India's vast outbound tourism market, which runs to tens of millions of international trips each year. Bali's market penetration in India is still relatively modest compared with destinations like Thailand, Singapore, or even the Maldives.

That means the upside is significant. With the right air connectivity, targeted marketing, and a product offering that resonates with Indian tastes and values, Bali has the potential to dramatically increase its Indian visitor numbers in the years ahead — making India a structural pillar of the island's tourism economy rather than a niche source market.

Currently, Australia, China, and India trade places among the top three sources of international visitors to Bali. As India's outbound travel market continues to mature, that competition for the top spot is set to intensify.
