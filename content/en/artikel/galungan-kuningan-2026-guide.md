On **17 June 2026**, Bali will transform. Thousands of tall bamboo poles — each one intricately decorated with woven coconut leaves, tropical fruit and hand-crafted offerings — will appear overnight along every road on the island. The scent of incense will drift from household shrines. Women in elaborate kebaya blouses and men in white shirts and checked sarongs will move in ceremonial processions toward village temples. Galungan has arrived.

For visitors fortunate enough to be in Bali during this period, it is arguably the single most visually and spiritually immersive experience the island offers.

## What Is Galungan?

Galungan is the most sacred festival in the Balinese Hindu calendar, occurring every **210 days** (one Pawukon cycle). It commemorates the **victory of Dharma (righteousness) over Adharma (evil)** — a cosmic triumph celebrated across ten days, concluding with **Kuningan**.

During this period, Balinese Hindus believe the spirits of deceased ancestors descend to earth to visit their families. Homes and temples are decorated, elaborate offerings are prepared, and the living and the dead share a symbolic reunion before the ancestors return on Kuningan.

**2026 Dates:**
- **Galungan:** Wednesday, 17 June 2026
- **Kuningan:** Saturday, 27 June 2026

## The Penjor: Bali's Most Iconic Decoration

The **penjor** is the towering bamboo pole you will see lining every street, arching gracefully outward over the road. No two are identical.

Each penjor is hand-crafted by the household that erects it, typically in the days before Galungan. The decorations carry specific spiritual symbolism:
- The **curved bamboo pole** represents the sacred mountain Gunung Agung
- **Coconut leaves** woven into geometric patterns represent Bali's agricultural abundance
- **Offerings tied at the tip** express gratitude to Ida Sang Hyang Widhi (the supreme deity)
- **A small shrine at the base** receives daily incense and flower offerings throughout the festival

Walking or cycling down a rural Balinese road on Galungan day — with penjors swaying gently on both sides for kilometre after kilometre — is an experience that defies easy description.

## Day-by-Day: What Happens When

### The Days Before Galungan
Preparation begins three days prior. Markets overflow with palm leaves, flowers, fruit and young bamboo. Families gather to make **jajan** (traditional sweets) and **lawar** (a ceremonial mixed salad of minced meat, vegetables, grated coconut and spices). The smell of cooking and incense begins to permeate neighbourhoods.

### Galungan Day (17 June)
- **Pre-dawn:** Household shrines are prepared with fresh offerings
- **Morning:** Families dress in ceremonial attire and pray at home shrines, then proceed to village temples
- **Midday:** Community feasting — lawar, babi guling (spit-roast pig) and rice feature prominently
- **Evening:** Gamelan music and occasional dance performances in village banjar halls

### The Ten Days Between
Daily offerings continue at home shrines. Village-level prayers happen at specific intervals. For visitors, this is an excellent time to observe everyday Balinese religious life — far less crowded than the main days.

### Kuningan Day (27 June)
Kuningan is the farewell. Ancestors' spirits return to their realm at midday, so prayers are completed before 12:00. The mood is simultaneously celebratory and bittersweet. **Yellow rice (nasi kuning)** — symbolising prosperity and the golden light of spiritual transition — features prominently in offerings.

## Best Places to Witness Galungan

**Village roads anywhere in Bali** — The penjors are island-wide; you do not need to go anywhere specific. Simply renting a scooter and driving through residential areas in Ubud, Gianyar, Tabanan or any rural district delivers the full effect.

**Ubud's main street (Jalan Raya Ubud)** — The palace area hosts ceremonial processions and traditional dance performances on Galungan morning. Arrive by 08:00 for the best position.

**Pura Besakih** — Bali's Mother Temple on the slopes of Gunung Agung sees its most elaborate ceremonies during Galungan. Significant crowds but unmatched spiritual atmosphere.

**Your accommodation's neighbourhood** — If staying in a homestay or small guesthouse in a residential area, your hosts will almost certainly invite you to observe or participate in family prayers. This is the most intimate experience available to visitors.

## What to Wear

Visiting temples or joining processions requires:
- A **sarong (kain)** covering the legs to the ankle
- A **sash (selendang)** tied around the waist
- A reasonably modest upper garment (shoulders covered)

Sarongs and sashes are available to borrow at all major temples, usually free of charge. Purchasing your own at a local market (Rp30,000–80,000) is a respectful gesture.

## Practical Considerations

**Shops and restaurants:** Most tourist-facing businesses remain open throughout Galungan. Some local warungs may close on the main day.

**Traffic:** Ceremonial processions cause localised road closures in villages. Build extra time into any travel plans on 17 June.

**Photography:** Street processions and penjors are freely photographable. For temple interiors and close-up ceremony photography, always ask first — and read the atmosphere. When in doubt, put the camera down and simply watch.

**Accommodation:** Galungan is Bali's peak domestic travel period. Book accommodation months in advance if travelling during this window.

## The Spiritual Logic

A common question from visitors: why do the Balinese celebrate so elaborately for ancestors they cannot see?

The Balinese concept of **Tri Hita Karana** — harmony between humans and God, humans and other humans, and humans and nature — provides the framework. Galungan is not merely a festival; it is an affirmation that the invisible world is as real and present as the visible one. The elaborate offerings, the specific timing, the collective participation of entire villages — these are not performances for tourists. They are the operating system of Balinese civilization, running without interruption for centuries.

Witnessing it, if you approach it with genuine curiosity and respect, is a privilege.
