Bali once again stepped into the national spotlight as a model province for environmental governance. On Wednesday (8/7/2026), the iconic **Lapangan Puputan Margarana Niti Mandala** square in Denpasar hosted the **National Waste Sorting Alert Rally** (*Apel Siaga Pilah Sampah*), attended by Bali Governor Wayan Koster alongside Deputy Coordinating Minister for Food Affairs, Zulkifli Hasan. The event also served as a prelude to the groundbreaking of Bali's first Waste-to-Energy (PSEL) facility.

## A National Call to Action

The rally was not merely a symbolic gathering. Governor Koster issued a clear directive to all Balinese residents and stakeholders: tackle waste at its source.

"Don't wait until waste becomes an overwhelming problem. Sort it now — at home, at the source," Koster declared in his address to thousands of participants gathered at the square.

Deputy Coordinating Minister Zulkifli Hasan reinforced this message, stating that **source-based sorting is the cornerstone of effective waste management**. When waste is sorted at the point of generation, downstream processes — composting, recycling, and energy recovery through PSEL — become far more efficient and economically viable.

## Ten Villages Honored for Waste Sorting Excellence

A highlight of the rally was the recognition of **ten villages and urban wards** across Bali that had demonstrated outstanding commitment to implementing household and community-level waste sorting systems. Among the recognized communities:

- **Sanur Kauh Village** and **Tegal Kerta Ward** (City of Denpasar)
- **Kutuh Village** and **Gulingan Village** (Badung Regency)
- Six additional villages from other regencies across Bali

Each of the ten honored communities received **Lahsamor units** — a compact household organic waste processor developed by Indonesia's National Research and Innovation Agency (BRIN).

## Lahsamor: BRIN's Innovation for Household Organics

Lahsamor (*Lahan Sumber Organik*) is a small-scale organic waste processing device designed for household and community use. It leverages natural decomposing bacteria to rapidly convert food scraps, vegetable peels, and other organic matter into high-quality compost within days rather than months.

Deputy Coordinating Minister Zulkifli described Lahsamor as the perfect complement to the large-scale PSEL facility. "PSEL handles non-recyclable waste that can't be composted. Lahsamor handles household organics. Together, they close the loop," he explained.

## Bali Targets Full Waste Resolution by End of 2026

The Bali Provincial Government has set an ambitious target: **completely resolve the island's waste problem before December 2026**. The multi-pronged strategy includes:

1. **Mandatory waste sorting** effective July 1, 2026 for all households and businesses
2. **PSEL Denpasar–Badung** facility operational by early 2028
3. **Termination of open-dumping** at the outdated Suwung landfill
4. **Lahsamor distribution** to high-performing villages as performance incentives
5. **Adat council enforcement** through traditional village laws (*awig-awig*)

### Preventing TPA Fires

The rally also carried an urgent safety dimension. The Coordinating Ministry for Food Affairs warned that unmanaged, unsorted waste at landfill sites poses a severe fire risk during the dry season — a problem that has caused large-scale fires at TPA (*Tempat Pemrosesan Akhir*, or Final Processing Sites) across Indonesia. By sorting waste at source, the volume of organic material sent to landfills drops dramatically, significantly reducing fire risk.

## The Power of Adat in Waste Management

What makes Bali's approach to waste sorting truly distinctive is the central role of **traditional village governance** (*desa adat*). Unlike purely administrative enforcement, Bali's adat councils use customary law with deep cultural authority to ensure community compliance. Residents respond not just to regulations but to a sense of shared responsibility toward their natural and spiritual environment.

This community-embedded approach has proven far more effective than top-down policy alone, and it's precisely why the national government is looking to Bali as a replicable model for the rest of Indonesia.

Bali's Apel Siaga Pilah Sampah demonstrated that with the right mix of leadership, community culture, and technological innovation, a sustainable waste management revolution is not just possible — it's already well underway.
