Bali's most ambitious infrastructure project — the **Bali Urban Subway** — has become a source of growing frustration and scrutiny in 2026. A grand groundbreaking ceremony was held in September 2024, but nearly two years later, the site remains eerily quiet. No heavy machinery, no workers, no visible progress. Bali's parliament is now pushing hard for transparency.

## Why Bali Desperately Needs Mass Transit

Southern Bali's traffic gridlock is no longer just an inconvenience — it's an existential threat to the island's tourism economy. The Kuta–Seminyak–Canggu corridor, once the beating heart of Bali's nightlife and beach scene, is increasingly choked with vehicles. Nusa Dua's MICE district and Jimbaran's seafood restaurants face similar pressure.

Without a mass transit solution, Ngurah Rai International Airport risks being cut off from its surrounding resort areas by chronic congestion, as annual passenger numbers continue to climb.

## The Plan: Four Phases, $20 Billion, Connecting the Island

The Bali Urban Subway project, managed by PT Bali Infrastruktur Prima (BIP), is designed in four phases:

| Phase | Route | Distance |
|-------|-------|----------|
| 1 | Airport → Kuta Central Parking → Seminyak → Berawa → Cemagi | ~16 km |
| 2 | Airport → Jimbaran → Udayana University → Nusa Dua | ~13.5 km |
| 3 | Kuta Central Parking → Sesetan → Renon → Sanur | TBD |
| 4 | Renon → Sukawati → Ubud | TBD |

The total investment across all four phases is estimated at **US$ 20 billion** — one of the largest infrastructure investments in Indonesian history. Phases 1 and 2 alone would require **US$ 10.8 billion**.

The full Phase 1 and 2 system is targeted to be operational by end of 2031, with the airport-to-Kuta Sentral Parkir stretch expected to complete by Q2 2028.

## What Went Wrong?

Following the September 2024 groundbreaking, construction was expected to begin in earnest. But by early 2026, **no physical work had started**.

The Head of Bali's Transportation Agency (*Dishub*) declined to elaborate on the project's status, saying only that the agency was "waiting for an official submission from the developer." His now-infamous quote — *"There's not much I can tell you"* — became a viral symbol of official evasiveness around the delayed megaproject.

**Nyoman Suyasa**, Chair of Commission III at Bali's Provincial Parliament (DPRD), confirmed that as of February 2026, the legislative body had received no official progress report from the operator. Suyasa announced plans to urgently meet with the Governor and the Transportation Agency to demand clarity.

## Governor Koster: "It Will Start in 2026"

Governor I Wayan Koster has maintained that the subway project is designed to launch in 2026, with third-party parties handling construction. He noted that feasibility studies for Phase 1 are complete, while Phases 3 and 4 remain in the study stage.

But months into 2026 with no visible activity, Koster's assurances have done little to calm critics.

## Meanwhile, Smaller Solutions Move Forward

While the subway remains stuck in limbo, two smaller-scale transport solutions are gaining traction:

- **E-Shuttle Buses in Sanur:** Community-operated electric shuttle services, managed by local village cooperatives, are providing a green transport option for the Sanur area
- **Jimbaran Underpass:** The long-discussed underpass near the McDonald's intersection — a notorious congestion point at the entrance to the Bukit Peninsula — is scheduled to break ground by late 2026

These projects, though modest in scale, signal that local solutions can move faster than megaprojects mired in investor uncertainty.

## What's at Stake

The Bali Urban Subway is more than an infrastructure project. It's a statement about whether Bali can evolve from an island overdependent on private vehicles into a modern, sustainable destination with world-class public transit. As traffic worsens each peak season, the clock is ticking.
