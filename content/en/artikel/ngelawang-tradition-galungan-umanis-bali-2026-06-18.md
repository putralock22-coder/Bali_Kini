Across Denpasar and villages throughout Bali, **Umanis Galungan** on Thursday (18/6/2026) was marked by a distinctly Balinese spectacle: groups of young people in elaborate Barong Bangkung costumes moving from house to house in the ancient ritual known as **Ngelawang**. Part procession, part exorcism, and entirely captivating — this tradition remains one of the most authentic expressions of Balinese Hinduism that visitors can witness firsthand.

## The Meaning Behind the Name

The word **Ngelawang** derives from *lawang*, the Balinese word for **door** — and the ritual is exactly what the name implies. The sacred barong figure enters the doorway of each home, bringing with it spiritual protection, the neutralisation of negative energies, and blessings for all who live within.

Far from being merely ceremonial, Ngelawang is understood by its practitioners as a genuine form of **spiritual cleansing** for the entire community. The procession sweeps through the village's energy field, door by door, resetting it in alignment with the higher forces celebrated during the Galungan holiday.

## The Barong Bangkung: Guardian in the Form of a Wild Boar

The barong featured in Ngelawang is not the more internationally recognizable **Barong Ket** (the lion-like king of spirits). Instead, it is the **Barong Bangkung** — a mythological protector embodied in the form of a wild boar.

In Balinese, *bangkung* means **sow** (female pig), while *bangkal* refers to a boar. Far from being an animal of ill omen, the Bangkung is revered as a powerful guardian deity capable of confronting and repelling demonic forces. Its painted mask with tusks and layered ceremonial cloth creates an imposing yet sacred presence that commands both reverence and delight — especially from children who line the lanes to watch.

## Youth at the Center of a Living Tradition

Each Ngelawang troupe typically comprises **8 to 15 young people**, all from the local *banjar* (community ward). Two of them perform as **barong dancers**, supporting the full costume structure, while the rest serve as **gamelan musicians** — playing portable instruments carried as the group moves through the neighbourhood.

The fact that this tradition is carried primarily by children and teenagers is significant. It is how each generation learns to inhabit and own their cultural heritage — not in a classroom, but in the streets of their own village, in full ceremonial regalia, before the eyes of their community.

## Once Every Six Months

Ngelawang is a rare occasion. The tradition is performed **only during the period between Galungan and Kuningan** — a ten-day window that occurs every 210 days (6 months) according to the Balinese Pawukon calendar. This makes Umanis Galungan one of only two opportunities each year to witness this living ritual.

For travellers in Bali on June 18, 2026, wandering into the lanes and alleyways of any traditional village — particularly in Denpasar, Gianyar, Ubud, or Bangli — offers a strong chance of encountering a Ngelawang procession in full swing.

## An Unrepeatable Cultural Encounter

Unlike staged performances in hotels or cultural parks, Ngelawang is unscripted and spontaneous. There is no ticket, no stage, no set time. You simply walk, listen for the sound of gamelan growing closer, and wait as a troupe of costumed teenagers rounds the corner with their towering Barong Bangkung — bringing a ritual as old as the village itself to your doorstep.

That is what makes Bali's Umanis Galungan one of the most magical days to be on the island.
