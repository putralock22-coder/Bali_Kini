Bali's provincial government has moved to formally close a number of low- and medium-low-risk Indonesian Standard Business Classification (KBLI) codes to foreign direct investment (PMA). The step follows five years of data showing that nearly half of the tens of thousands of PMA companies registered in Bali are suspected of exploiting small-business permits mainly to obtain residency, rather than genuinely investing capital and running a business.

## It Started With an Urgent Letter From Governor Koster

The policy traces back to an urgent-classified letter Bali Governor Wayan Koster sent to the Ministry of Investment/BKPM on January 28, 2026, requesting the closure of PT PMA registrations for nine low-risk KBLI codes in Bali, including a ban on foreign companies registering through virtual office addresses.

Bali's Investment and One-Stop Integrated Service Office (DPMPTSP) followed up with a formal request to the Ministry on February 9, 2026. The proposed list was narrowed to seven KBLI codes covering motorcycle and car rentals, retail trade, photography services, travel agencies, and real estate — with restaurants/cafes, entertainment, and fitness centers added in a later draft.

"We have already submitted correspondence regarding several planned closures," said DPMPTSP Bali head I Ketut Sukra Negara, confirming the move to local media.

## Only One of Seven Codes Approved So Far

As of mid-July 2026, only one of the seven proposed KBLI codes has received formal approval from the Ministry of Investment: code 70209 for management consulting services, which was officially closed to new foreign-company registrations in late February 2026.

The remaining six codes — real estate (68111), travel agencies (79110), car/motorcycle rental (77100), restaurants and cafes (56100), entertainment and recreation (93290), and fitness centers (93130) — remain under ministry review. The Online Single Submission (OSS) system reportedly began automatically rejecting some applications from Bali-based business addresses across broader low-risk categories between March and May 2026, even though the definitive legal framework has not yet been fully finalized.

Bali stands to become the first Indonesian province to impose this kind of restriction on low-risk foreign investment — a precedent being closely watched by investment consultants and foreign business operators across the island.

## The Data Behind the Policy: 19,262 PMA Registrations, Nearly Half Low-Risk

The policy's rationale rests on data that has alarmed Bali's policymakers. Between 2021 and 2025, Bali recorded 19,262 PT PMA company registrations — roughly 40 percent of all PMA registrations nationwide, a share far exceeding Bali's proportion of Indonesia's land area or population.

Of that total, about 47.55 percent were classified as low-risk business activities requiring only a Business Identification Number (NIB) with no additional permits. Provincial authorities concluded that most of these companies functioned primarily as an "easy entry door" to obtain investor residency permits (KITAS), without genuine business activity or substantial capital investment behind them.

Meanwhile, Bali's total realized investment in 2025 reached Rp42.81 trillion, or 94 percent of target, with foreign investment contributing Rp25.60 trillion — up 5.7 percent year-on-year, with Australian investors dominating, concentrated mostly in real estate.

## Protecting Local Small Businesses

For Bali's small and medium enterprises, the policy is both welcome relief and a signal of local government backing. Sectors such as vehicle rental, cafes, and photography services have long been cited as easy targets for small-capital foreign investors to move into, squeezing out local entrepreneurs and domestic investment (PMDN) in the same segments.

Investment observers acknowledge the policy risks depressing Bali's overall foreign investment figures, since low-risk projects are numerous even if individually small in value. But DPMPTSP insists the long-term goal is to raise the quality of foreign investment entering Bali — not just chase volume — while closing loopholes for nominee schemes and illegal business practices that have tarnished the island's investment climate.

Going forward, business operators and investors alike are watching for the Ministry of Investment/BKPM's final decision on the six still-pending KBLI codes, which will determine how far this restriction is ultimately applied on the ground.
