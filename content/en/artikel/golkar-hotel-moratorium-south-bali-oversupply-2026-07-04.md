Development pressure in South Bali has reached a critical point. The Golkar Party faction in Bali's Regional House of Representatives (DPRD) has formally proposed a moratorium on new hotel and villa construction across Bali's southern region, particularly in Badung Regency and surrounding areas. The proposal was delivered during a faction opinion session as a direct response to the region's increasingly imbalanced tourism landscape.

## South Bali Facing Severe Oversupply

According to the Golkar faction, South Bali is grappling with serious accommodation oversupply. The number of hotel rooms and villas continues to climb while tourist demand does not always keep pace. This has led to destructive price competition among accommodation providers, ultimately damaging the entire industry's health.

Popular areas such as Seminyak, Kuta, Canggu, and Jimbaran have reached saturation. Productive agricultural land, green open spaces, and coastal buffer zones continue to shrink as hotels and villas replace them.

## Congestion and Land Conversion Taking Their Toll

Beyond business concerns, unchecked development has fueled severe traffic congestion along South Bali's main corridors. Roads like Jalan Ngurah Rai, Sunset Road, and the route to Uluwatu regularly experience long gridlock, especially during holiday seasons. This harms local residents and degrades the tourist experience alike.

Golkar lawmakers stressed that converting rice fields and farmland into hotel and villa complexes violates Bali's spatial planning principles, which mandate a balance between nature, culture, and tourism.

## Redirect Investment to Northern and Eastern Bali

The proposed moratorium is not intended to stop investment in Bali altogether. Golkar is pushing to redirect hotel and accommodation development toward regions that still need economic growth — North Bali (Buleleng), East Bali (Karangasem), and West Bali (Jembrana).

This aligns with the provincial government's equitable development goals. Bank Indonesia's Bali office has already mapped tourism clusters based on regional potential — maritime tourism in West Bali, wellness and agro-spiritual tourism in North Bali, and cultural heritage tourism in Klungkung and Bangli.

### Broad Support for the Moratorium

The proposal has drawn support from various stakeholders. Six Bali regents previously agreed to stop promoting new hotel and restaurant construction in their respective areas — Tabanan, Jembrana, Buleleng, Bangli, Karangasem, and Klungkung. Extending similar restrictions to South Bali is seen as a logical next step.

Academics and tourism observers also back a comprehensive evaluation of South Bali's development. They argue that Bali's tourism focus must shift from maximizing accommodation supply to delivering sustainable, high-quality travel experiences.

## What Comes Next

DPRD Bali is expected to formalize Golkar's proposal into an official recommendation to the Bali Provincial Government. A thorough review of the Regional Spatial Plan (RTRW) is among the concrete steps proposed, along with stricter building permit enforcement in saturated zones.

Governor Wayan Koster has previously signaled the need to reorient Bali's tourism development toward a more even geographic distribution. The political momentum behind this moratorium proposal may finally translate that vision into binding policy.
