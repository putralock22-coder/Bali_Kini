Bali took a landmark step in tackling one of its most persistent environmental challenges on Wednesday, June 10, 2026. **Environment Minister Moh Jumhur Hidayat**, together with **Governor of Bali Wayan Koster** and the regents and mayors of all nine regencies and one city across Bali, officially declared a movement toward **Bali 100 Percent Waste Sorting** in Denpasar.

## A Unified Declaration by All Regional Leaders

For the first time, every head of local government in Bali stood together to commit to a single, unified waste management goal: ensuring that all residents sort their waste at the source — before it ever reaches a collection point or landfill.

In his declaration, Minister Jumhur issued a direct call to the people of Bali:

> "Let us together realize Bali 100 percent sorting waste — simultaneously, for a Bali that is clean, healthy, beautiful, and sustainable."

Governor Koster underscored the urgency, warning that Bali's reputation as a world-class tourist destination remains at risk as long as waste management remains inadequate. Images of garbage-strewn beaches have repeatedly gone viral internationally, threatening the island's premium tourism brand.

## Two Hard Deadlines

The declaration comes with two firm and non-negotiable deadlines:

- **July 1, 2026**: All Bali residents must begin sorting waste simultaneously at the source — households, businesses, and public facilities.
- **August 1, 2026**: Bali will officially and permanently **close all landfills operating under the open dumping system**, in line with a national push to end open waste disposal across Indonesia.

Minister Jumhur stressed that open dumping does not just pollute the environment — it damages Bali's international image and undermines the island's claim to sustainable tourism leadership.

## Current State of Waste Sorting in Bali

Government data reveals a mixed picture. Across Bali's regencies and cities, current household waste sorting rates range from **40 to 76 percent**. Badung Regency and Denpasar City lead the pack at 76 percent, while several other areas still lag significantly behind.

The government's immediate target is to reach **70 percent waste sorting across all of Bali by the end of August 2026**, as a stepping stone toward the 100 percent goal.

## Infrastructure to Match the Commitment

The government recognizes that without adequate infrastructure, even the most committed residents cannot effectively sort and dispose of waste. Minister Jumhur pledged to accelerate the rollout of:

- **TPS3R** (Reduce, Reuse, Recycle Waste Processing Sites) at the village and ward level
- **TPST** (Integrated Waste Processing Sites) for larger-scale processing
- **Community waste banks** where residents can deposit sorted recyclables

With this infrastructure in place, sorted waste will be processed at the nearest facility, leaving only true residue to be sent to landfills — dramatically reducing the overall volume of waste.

## Tukad Bindu: A Symbol of What's Possible

Minister Jumhur's visit to Bali also included a stop at **Tukad Bindu** in Denpasar, a river that was once clogged with garbage and flanked by dingy concrete walls. Thanks to community-driven revitalization, it has been transformed into a vibrant, lush public space. The minister used Tukad Bindu as proof that genuine change is possible when government and community work together with purpose.

## Why This Matters for Bali's Future

Bali's waste crisis has long been a paradox: one of the world's most beloved travel destinations, struggling with the very environmental conditions that threaten what makes it special. Southern beaches, including Kuta and Seminyak, are regularly hit by waves of plastic waste during the wet season, and global media coverage has repeatedly put Bali's environmental management under scrutiny.

This declaration signals a shift from reactive cleanup campaigns to a structural, behavioral revolution — one that begins in every kitchen and backyard across the island.

Whether the July and August deadlines will be met remains to be seen. But the political will shown by the unprecedented alignment of Bali's provincial and regional leaders suggests that this time, the commitment runs deeper than words.
