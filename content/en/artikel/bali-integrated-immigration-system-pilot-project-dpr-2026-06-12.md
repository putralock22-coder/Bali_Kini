A senior Indonesian lawmaker has called for Bali to be designated as the national testbed for an integrated digital immigration management system, citing the island's massive international footprint and growing concerns over transnational crime. The proposal, put forward by **Rieke Diah Pitaloka**, a member of Commission XIII at the House of Representatives (DPR), represents a significant shift in how Indonesia envisions border governance at its most globally visible destination.

## The Case for Bali as a Model

Rieke's argument centers on Bali's unparalleled role as Indonesia's primary international gateway. No other region in the country handles the sheer volume of foreign movement that flows through Ngurah Rai International Airport and the island's seaports.

"Bali must become the model for modern immigration governance — one that is built on One Data Indonesia, digital government systems, transparency, accountability, and the protection of national sovereignty," Rieke stated in a formal session.

## The Numbers Behind the Urgency

The scale of Bali's international activity makes the case clearly:

- **6.9 million** foreign tourists entered Bali in 2025
- **Over 15 million** international border crossings recorded in a single year
- **53,428** immigration residence permit documents issued
- **Nearly 28,000** passports processed across various categories

These figures position Bali as Indonesia's highest-intensity point for foreign workforce activity, foreign investment flows, and general international mobility — and simultaneously its most exposed point if oversight systems are inadequate.

## Four Pillars of the Proposed System

### Pillar 1: Integration into One Data Indonesia

All immigration data from Bali would be consolidated into the national **One Data Indonesia** (*Satu Data Indonesia*) architecture and the **National Digital Government System** (SPD). This would enable authorized agencies to verify the immigration status of any foreign national in real time — eliminating information silos between ministries and local authorities.

### Pillar 2: Cross-Institutional Interoperability

The system is designed to build data-sharing pathways between all relevant ministries and agencies, regional governments, village administrations (*desa dinas*), and even **traditional Balinese village institutions** (*desa adat*). The inclusion of desa adat is notable — it reflects Bali's unique governance structure in which indigenous community bodies play a significant role in local administration.

### Pillar 3: A Clear Legal Framework

Rieke emphasized that the system requires dedicated legislation providing the legal basis for data integration and cross-ministerial coordination in national immigration governance. Without clear regulatory authority, she argues, the system cannot be enforced effectively.

### Pillar 4: Digital Forensic Audits

The proposal includes a comprehensive investigative audit — covering both conventional investigation and digital forensics — of all immigration document issuances, including:
- Visas across all categories
- Limited Stay Permits (KITAS) and Permanent Stay Permits (KITAP)
- Investor residence permits and work permits
- Foreign national sponsorship arrangements
- Foreign Direct Investment (PMA) company records

## Rising Concerns Over Transnational Crime

Separately, MPR member **Marinus Gea** highlighted growing evidence of Bali being exploited as an entry point for transnational criminal networks. Criminal syndicates involved in online fraud, narcotics trafficking, human trafficking, and other cross-border crimes have reportedly been taking advantage of gaps in Bali's immigration administration.

The DPR has urged Bali's Directorate of Immigration to significantly intensify its enforcement activities, particularly regarding foreign nationals who overstay visas, work illegally, or use investment permits as covers for illicit activities.

## A Balancing Act: Openness and Sovereignty

The proposed integrated system attempts to resolve a long-standing tension in Bali's governance: the need to remain **open and welcoming** as a world-class tourism destination, while simultaneously functioning as a **secure and sovereign** national border.

The current patchwork of disconnected databases, manual verification processes, and limited inter-agency communication has made it difficult to track foreign nationals comprehensively — particularly those who enter as tourists but transition into residents, workers, or investors.

## Replication Potential Across Indonesia

If successfully implemented, Bali's integrated immigration model could serve as the blueprint for other emerging international destinations across the archipelago — including Labuan Bajo (East Nusa Tenggara), Mandalika (Lombok), and Likupang (North Sulawesi), all of which are being developed as premium tourism zones.

The digitization of immigration governance, advocates argue, is not just a security measure — it is an essential foundation for sustainable and responsible international tourism development in the 21st century.
