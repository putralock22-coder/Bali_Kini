A troubling social phenomenon is unfolding across Bali: an increasing number of local residents are being forced to rent boarding house rooms — even in their own ancestral villages. Land prices have surged to between **Rp350 million and Rp1 billion per are** (100 square meters), placing property ownership completely out of reach for most Balinese workers.

## A Widening Gap Between Wages and Property

The disparity between earnings and property values in Bali has grown to alarming proportions. Bali's **Provincial Minimum Wage (UMP)** currently stands at just **Rp3.2 million per month** — a figure dramatically disconnected from skyrocketing land prices driven by decades of tourism-led investment.

The Head of Bali's Manpower and Energy Resources Agency has noted that the ideal UMP should be around **Rp5 million per month** for residents to meet basic living standards. But even at that higher figure, land ownership remains an essentially unattainable goal for salaried workers in today's Bali property market.

## Renting in Your Own Village

What makes this crisis particularly striking is that it has spread far beyond Bali's urban centers. Even in traditional Balinese village communities (*desa adat*), some residents are now renting rooms in boarding houses within their own ancestral villages — pushed out of family homes that can no longer accommodate growing generations, with no realistic prospect of acquiring land nearby.

This situation has prompted warnings that Balinese people risk becoming **"guests in their own island"** — a phrase describing how local communities are being displaced from their own land by the relentless advance of outside investment and the distorting effects of mass tourism on the property market.

The phenomenon threatens not just individual families but the broader social fabric and cultural continuity of Balinese society, which has long been organized around close-knit village community life.

## Parliament Pushes for Apartment Buildings

**I Nyoman Parta**, a member of Indonesia's Parliament (DPR RI) Commission III, has called for serious debate about building **affordable apartment complexes (rusun)** in Bali as a practical housing alternative for locals who can no longer compete in the open property market.

The rusun model has demonstrated success in major Indonesian cities, helping working families access decent housing in land-scarce urban environments. However, implementation in Bali carries unique complexities. Local cultural values and customary law (*adat*) frameworks — including rules governing communal land ownership (*druwe desa*) within traditional villages — must be carefully integrated into any policy design to ensure the solution is culturally appropriate and community-supported.

## Tourism's Double-Edged Impact

The explosion in Bali property prices is inextricably linked to the island's tourism boom. Waves of foreign and domestic investment flooding into the accommodation sector — villas, boutique hotels, resorts, and short-term rental properties — have dramatically inflated land values, particularly in sought-after areas like Seminyak, Canggu, Ubud, Sanur, and Nusa Dua.

This demand pressure has rippled outward, pushing up land costs across the entire island — including in regions previously considered too remote to attract significant investor interest.

## Need for Protective Policy

Policymakers face mounting pressure to formulate concrete regulations protecting local residents' housing rights. Proposals under discussion include restrictions on foreign property ownership, dedicated housing subsidy programs targeting Balinese community members, and the designation of land zones reserved exclusively for local residents.

Without meaningful policy intervention, the marginalization of Bali's own people from their ancestral land is expected to deepen — a development that would have profound and lasting consequences for the island's unique culture and social identity.
