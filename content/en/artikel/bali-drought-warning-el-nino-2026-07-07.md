Bali's **Regional Disaster Management Agency (BPBD)** and Indonesia's **Meteorology, Climatology, and Geophysics Agency (BMKG)** have jointly issued an early warning about the potential spread of dry-season impacts across the Island of the Gods, following the intensification of the **El Nino** phenomenon detected since early July 2026. The warning covers drought risk, clean water shortages, and an elevated risk of forest and land fires.

## El Nino Index Crosses Into Strong Territory

According to data released for the period of July 1–10, 2026, the **El Nino Southern Oscillation (ENSO) index** has reached **+1.61**, staying above the normal threshold for seven consecutive ten-day periods. BMKG estimates a **98 percent probability that El Nino will strengthen into the "strong" category**, indicating a very high level of certainty that Bali will face a dry season considerably drier than its historical climatological average.

## Peak Dry Season Expected in August-September

BMKG projects that most of Bali will hit peak dry conditions between **August and September 2026**. During this period, rainfall is expected to fall well below normal, raising the risk of agricultural drought, shrinking spring water discharge, and reduced raw water supply for both households and traditional subak irrigation systems across the island's regencies.

## Rising Risk of Forest and Land Fires

Beyond drought, BPBD Bali has also warned of an increased likelihood of forest and land fire reports as vegetation dries out in hillside and protected forest areas. Regions historically prone to seasonal fires, such as North and East Bali, have been urged to strengthen preparedness, including deploying early firefighting equipment and conducting regular patrols in high-risk zones.

## Local Government Mitigation Measures

In response to the warning, BPBD Bali has coordinated with regency and city governments to prepare a range of mitigation steps, including the distribution of clean water tanks to areas at risk of water crises, remapping of drought-prone zones, and public education campaigns on water conservation during the prolonged dry season.

Communities, particularly farmers and tourism operators dependent on stable water supply—such as villa and resort managers—are advised to begin independent mitigation efforts, such as rainwater harvesting and efficient water use, to reduce the impact of the extreme dry season expected to last through the end of the third quarter of 2026.

## Cross-Sector Coordination Seen as Key

BMKG's national office has also called for cross-sector preparedness nationwide in facing the impacts of the 2026 El Nino, given the phenomenon's potential to affect agriculture, food security, and water resource management across Indonesia, including Bali. Coordination among BPBD, BMKG, agricultural agencies, and local water utilities is considered crucial to prevent drought impacts from spreading to other vital sectors on the island.
