Bali once again proved its standing as a global meeting point — not only for leisure, but for ideas. Over three days from July 10 to 12, 2026, Universitas Hindu Indonesia (UNHI) in Denpasar hosted the **Global Hindu Business Conference (GHBC) 2026**, bringing together hundreds of delegates from eight countries to forge a new model of international business rooted in ancient values.

## Dharmanomics: Ethics as Economic Architecture

The conference rallied around a single galvanizing concept: **"Towards Dharmanomics: Designing the Future."** Dharmanomics is a framework that positions Dharma — Hindu principles of righteousness, duty, and cosmic order — as the ethical foundation for economic activity.

In practical terms, Dharmanomics advocates for business models that prioritize sustainability, collective welfare, and moral accountability alongside profit. The concept challenges purely extractive economic thinking by arguing that long-term prosperity is impossible without harmony between human activity and the natural and social environment.

## A Truly Global Gathering: 300 Delegates, 8 Countries

The 2026 edition drew approximately **300 delegates** representing **India, Malaysia, Singapore, Nepal, Mauritius, Australia, the United Arab Emirates (Dubai)**, and Indonesia. Participants spanned a wide spectrum of sectors: renewable energy, electric vehicles, logistics, information technology, quality assurance, wellness, and tourism.

Over three days, delegates engaged in keynote addresses, panel discussions, business networking and matching sessions, product exhibitions, and field visits designed to translate forum conversations into concrete investment and partnership opportunities.

## Koster Positions Bali as Dharma-Economy Hub

Governor Wayan Koster signaled the provincial government's full backing for GHBC 2026 during a **Gala Dinner at Jaya Sabha, Denpasar**, on the opening night of July 10. His message was clear: Bali is not merely a picturesque backdrop for international conferences — it is an active participant in the global conversation about sustainable, values-driven economic development.

Koster pointed to Bali's unique combination of a living Hindu tradition, a creative economy, and an internationally recognized tourism brand as powerful assets for anchoring Dharmanomics in practice. "Bali is a real-world example of how spiritual values and economic progress can walk hand in hand," he said.

## Wellness Tourism at the Heart of the Agenda

Indonesia's Deputy Minister of Tourism, **Ni Luh Puspa**, offered a warm endorsement of GHBC 2026, drawing particular attention to its relevance for the development of **wellness tourism** — one of the fastest-growing and highest-yielding segments of global travel.

Ni Luh Puspa noted that Bali's biodiversity, cultural traditions, healing practices, and genuine hospitality combine to offer wellness experiences that cannot be replicated elsewhere. Bali's potential as a world-class wellness tourism hub, she argued, aligns naturally with the Dharmanomics philosophy of well-being as an economic driver.

## Economic Impact: Layers of Benefit for Bali

The immediate economic impact of GHBC 2026 is felt across Denpasar's hospitality and services sector, as 300 international delegates spend several days on the island. But the organizers and provincial government are eyeing far larger long-term returns.

GHBC 2026 is expected to seed new investment pipelines in sectors ranging from green energy to wellness retreats — industries that align with Bali's strategic goal of diversifying beyond mass tourism. The MICE (Meetings, Incentives, Conferences, and Exhibitions) sector also benefits directly, as events of GHBC's scale and international profile reinforce Bali's reputation as Southeast Asia's premier conference destination.

With global interest in ethical business models growing, forums like GHBC could position Bali not just as a place to visit, but as a place where the future of conscious capitalism is being written.
