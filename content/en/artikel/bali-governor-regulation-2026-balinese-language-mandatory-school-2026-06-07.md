Bali Governor **Wayan Koster** has officially enacted **Governor Regulation (Pergub) No. 7 of 2026** on the Implementation of Local Content Subjects. This landmark policy represents a concrete step in preserving Bali's cultural identity amid the ever-accelerating tide of modernization, while reaffirming the provincial government's commitment to safeguarding the island's cultural heritage across generations.

## Two Independent Subjects: A New Breakthrough in Balinese Education

The most significant change introduced by this regulation is the **separation of local content into two mandatory independent subjects** to be taught at all levels of formal education across Bali:

1. **Balinese Language** — covering Balinese language, script, and literature
2. **Balinese Local Wisdom** — incorporating the values of Sad Kerthi, customary practices, and the Vision of Bali's 100-Year Development Plan

Previously, both subjects were merged into a single local content block that often received minimal time in school schedules. With this separation, both fields now receive a more proportional and structured allocation, with a standing equal to that of national core subjects.

Governor Koster describes this policy as a **"Cross-Generational Cultural Fortress"** — a genuine commitment from the provincial government to ensure Bali's younger generations do not lose their roots in ancestral language and culture amid the forces of globalization.

## Balinese as the Official Medium of Instruction

One of the most important provisions in Regulation No. 7/2026 is the mandate to use **Balinese language as the medium of instruction** in both subjects. This positions Balinese not merely as textbook material to be memorized, but as an **active communication tool** within the daily learning process.

This policy has a dual impact: students not only learn Balinese vocabulary or grammar theoretically, but also become accustomed to using it in real-world classroom contexts.

## A Structured Learning Roadmap from Elementary to High School

The regulation provides a detailed, tiered curriculum structure with clear continuity from primary through upper secondary education:

### Elementary School (SD/MI)
- **Grades 1–2**: Introduction to Balinese Local Wisdom through engaging thematic learning
- **Grades 3–6**: Systematic Balinese language instruction — script, vocabulary, conversation, and foundational literature

### Junior High School (SMP/MTs)
- **Grades 7–8**: Continuation and deepening of Balinese language study
- **Grade 9**: Focused strengthening of Balinese Local Wisdom — customary values, philosophy, and social norms

### Senior High School (SMA/SMK/MA)
- **Grades 10–11**: Advanced Balinese language
- **Grade 12**: Comprehensive in-depth study of Balinese Local Wisdom before entering adult life

## Opening the Door for Traditional Communities

Perhaps the most progressive aspect of this regulation is the **official recognition of Bali's traditional educational institutions**. The regulation formally opens the door for:

- **Pasraman** — Hindu-based learning centers
- **Sekaa** — traditional arts and customary groups
- **Sanggar** — dance, music, and craft studios
- **Customary community organizations** of all kinds

These institutions can now officially participate in formal Balinese cultural education. This means the wisdom that has long lived only within adat (customary) communities can now enter the national education system **without losing its authenticity**.

## The Urgency Amid Modernization Pressures

Field observations and data indicate that the use of Balinese language among younger generations is declining. In many urban families — particularly in Denpasar, Badung, and Gianyar — daily communication has shifted entirely to Indonesian, with some moving further toward English for professional and social purposes.

This trend prompted the Bali provincial government to act through the most reliable channel for long-term change: **the formal education system**. While similar policies existed before, implementation was often weak due to a lack of a clear curriculum framework and adequate time allocation. Regulation No. 7 of 2026 addresses this with concrete solutions: a structured curriculum, an official medium of instruction, and formal involvement of customary communities.

## Bali: Culture as Foundation, Not Just Attraction

Bali consistently places culture at the **foundation of its development**, not merely as a tourist attraction. In the Vision of Bali's 100-Year Development Plan championed by Governor Koster, cultural identity is a competitive asset that sets Bali apart from other destinations around the world.

The concept of **Sad Kerthi** — Bali's six pillars of sustainability encompassing Atma Kerthi (spirit), Segara Kerthi (sea), Wana Kerthi (forest), Danu Kerthi (lake/water), Jana Kerthi (humanity), and Jagat Kerthi (universe) — forms the core of the mandated Local Wisdom curriculum.

Through Regulation No. 7 of 2026, Bali is not simply preserving a language and traditions. The government is building a system to ensure that Bali's noble values continue to live, grow, and remain relevant in the lives of future generations — a long-term cultural investment of immeasurable worth.
