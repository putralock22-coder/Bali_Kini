The long-running issue of foreign nationals conducting unlicensed business activities in Bali has reached a new flashpoint. On **Wednesday, June 11, 2026**, the **Indonesian Real Estate Broker Association (AREBI) Bali Chapter** formally lodged a complaint with the **Civil Service Police Unit (Satpol PP) of Bali Province**, demanding stronger enforcement against the proliferating practice of illegal property brokerage by foreign nationals operating across the island.

## A Formal Meeting, A Serious Complaint

The AREBI Bali delegation — led by Chairman **Michael Hikma Gunawan** and accompanied by Secretary **I.A Diana Krisnayanthi**, Treasurer **Himwan Pratama**, and Head of Membership and Organization **Oka Dharmayasa** — was received directly by **Bali Province Satpol PP Chief Dewa Nyoman Rai Dharmadi**.

The meeting was substantive. AREBI laid out detailed field findings illustrating the scale and nature of the violations. The Satpol PP Chief responded positively, acknowledging that the spread of unlicensed property brokers is a **shared concern** with the potential to harm consumers and undermine Bali's healthy investment environment.

## Hotspots: Canggu, Bingin, Sanur, Buleleng

According to AREBI Bali's findings, illegal foreign property broker activity is most concentrated in Bali's fastest-growing tourism corridors:

- **Canggu** — the epicenter of digital nomad and expat life in Bali
- **Bingin** — an increasingly exclusive enclave of high-end villas and surf tourism
- **Sanur** — a mature resort area in eastern Bali with steadily rising property values
- **Buleleng** — several districts in North Bali that have caught the eye of foreign investors

In these areas, foreign nationals frequently operate under the guise of "property consultants" or "facilitators" — all while holding **tourist visas** that legally bar any form of commercial activity.

## The Nominee Scheme: Local Citizens at Risk

One of the most common and troubling modus operandi is the **nominee scheme**, in which a foreign national uses the legal identity of an Indonesian citizen — typically a Balinese local — as the registered owner of property or business, while retaining effective control and financial benefit themselves.

This arrangement is not only illegal; it exposes the Indonesian nationals who "lend" their names to serious legal liability. Should a dispute arise, those locals may face legal consequences despite having no genuine stake in the transaction.

## Two Regulations Draw a Clear Line

The legal foundation for action is unambiguous. Two key regulations prohibit these practices:

- **Ministerial Regulation on Trade (Permendag) No. 33 of 2025** on Business Licensing explicitly states that the profession of property agent or broker **may only be practiced by Indonesian citizens**.
- **Bali Provincial Regulation No. 4 of 2026** on Control of Productive Land Conversion and Prohibition of Land Title Transfer via Nominees specifically outlaws the use of Indonesian citizens' names to mask foreign ownership.

Together, these regulations give enforcement agencies both the mandate and the tools to act.

## AREBI's Position: Consumer Protection and Market Integrity

For AREBI Bali, this complaint is not primarily about protecting the livelihoods of licensed brokers — it is about **protecting consumers and preserving market integrity**. Unlicensed brokers who operate outside formal professional standards expose both Indonesian and well-intentioned foreign buyers to potential fraud, misrepresentation, and legal complications.

There is also a fiscal dimension. Foreign brokers who transact outside official channels typically repatriate their commissions without paying Indonesian taxes, depriving the local economy of revenue that should flow to communities and public services.

AREBI Bali's decision to go directly to Satpol PP signals that Bali's legitimate property industry is pushing for the state to enforce rules that are already on the books — a decisive step toward a fairer and more transparent market.
