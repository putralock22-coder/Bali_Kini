Bali Governor Wayan Koster has called for an accelerated push to register the island's unique cultural and tourism assets as Geographical Indication Intellectual Property (HKI-IG). The call came during a high-level meeting with Indonesian House of Representatives member Yasonna Laoly in Denpasar on Friday, June 19, 2026, which focused on strategies to legally protect Bali's most distinctive products and tourism offerings in the global marketplace.

## Only 15 GIs — Bali Can Do More

Bali currently holds 15 registered Geographical Indications (GIs) — a legal designation that protects products tied to a specific geographic origin with qualities, reputation, or characteristics attributable to that location. While this represents meaningful progress, Governor Koster emphasized that the number falls far short of reflecting the full breadth of Bali's cultural and agricultural wealth.

Among the GIs that received certification in 2025 were:
- **Gula Dawan Klungkung** — a specialty palm sugar from Klungkung Regency
- **Tenun Cepuk Tanglad Nusa Penida** — a traditional woven fabric from Nusa Penida
- **Lukisan Batuan Gianyar Bali** — paintings from the renowned artistic village of Batuan in Gianyar
- **Kopi Robusta Lemukih Buleleng** — a robusta coffee variety from Buleleng's highland areas

Currently in the registration pipeline for 2026 are **Batu Pulaki Banyupoh Buleleng**, a distinctive natural stone, and **Tenun Songket Gelgel**, a ceremonial woven textile with deep roots in Balinese royal tradition.

## IP as an Economic Weapon for SMEs

Governor Koster framed intellectual property protection not as a bureaucratic formality, but as a dual-purpose economic tool — both a shield and a sword for Bali's small and medium industries (IKM) and micro, small, and medium enterprises (UMKM) competing in global markets.

By registering products as GIs, local producers gain legal protection against counterfeiting and unauthorized use of product names by competitors, including those from overseas. This means that iconic Balinese products like Kintamani coffee, endek fabric, or Celuk silver crafts can be marketed internationally with certified authenticity.

## Tourism Built on Geographical Identity

DPR member Yasonna Laoly advocated for a new approach: tourism products themselves — not just agricultural or craft goods — can be registered as Geographical Indications. Destinations with unique geographic and cultural identities, such as the Subak irrigation system of Jatiluwih, the traditional village of Penglipuran, or the Kintamani highland coffee landscape, could potentially receive GI status, further strengthening their global appeal and market positioning.

This approach aligns with Bali's broader tourism policy shift from mass-volume to quality-focused visitation — attracting travelers genuinely interested in Bali's authentic cultural offerings rather than generic beach tourism.

## 5,889 IP Applications Filed in First Half of 2026

The intellectual property surge in Bali is backed by numbers. In the first six months of 2026 alone, a total of **5,889 intellectual property applications** were filed across the island, comprising:
- **1,504 trademark applications**
- **24 patent applications**
- Additional applications spanning copyrights, industrial designs, and other IP categories

This figure reflects a significant rise in awareness among Bali's artisans, entrepreneurs, and cultural practitioners about the value of formally protecting their work. The Bali Provincial Government's facilitation programs — simplifying access to IP registration services for IKM and UMKM operators — are credited as a key driver of this growth.

## The Road Ahead

Bali's provincial government plans to substantially expand its roster of registered Geographical Indications over the coming years. A systematic mapping exercise is underway to identify products from across Bali's nine regencies and one city that possess the geographic distinctiveness and quality credentials needed for GI registration.

This initiative holds the potential to open new export channels, command premium prices in international markets, and strengthen the economic resilience of thousands of small producers who form the backbone of Bali's cultural economy.
