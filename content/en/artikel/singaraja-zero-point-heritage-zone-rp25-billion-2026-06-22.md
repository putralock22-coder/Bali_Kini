Singaraja — the administrative capital of Buleleng Regency on Bali's northern coast — is undergoing a striking transformation. The local government is in the final stages of restoring the **Titik Nol (Zero Point) area** of Singaraja City, with an investment of **Rp25 billion**, in a bid to revive the city's remarkable colonial history and position it as a heritage tourism icon for North Bali.

## A City That Was Once the Capital of the East

Few visitors to Bali know this history: before Denpasar became the island's economic and cultural center, **Singaraja was the seat of power** for an area far larger than Bali alone. During the Dutch colonial period and the early years of Indonesian independence, Singaraja served as the capital of the **Sunda Kecil Province** (the Lesser Sunda Islands) — a vast administrative region that encompassed modern-day Bali, West Nusa Tenggara (NTB), and East Nusa Tenggara (NTT).

That history lives on in the streets and buildings surrounding the Zero Point — the symbolic heart of the old city, where the Regent's Office, the Regional Representative Council (DPRD), and the iconic **Singa Ambara Raja statue** still stand, weathered but dignified.

## Rp25 Billion for a New-Old Identity

The groundbreaking ceremony for the project was held on **February 20, 2026**, at the Singa Ambara Raja statue plaza. The Rp25 billion budget — sourced from **Special Financial Assistance (BKK) provided by Badung Regency** — funds a comprehensive restoration program:

- Restoring the Regent's Office to its original Dutch colonial appearance
- Widening pedestrian walkways and sidewalks
- Creating public spaces that echo the character of a historic trading city
- Installing artistic lighting that highlights colonial-era architecture

Contract realization reached approximately **Rp24 billion**, and the project is on track for completion by **July 2026**.

## Golden Hour at the Zero Point

Even before construction is fully complete, the Zero Point area has already become one of Singaraja's most beloved gathering spots — particularly in the **late afternoon and evening** when construction crews have stopped for the day. Residents stroll along the newly expanded footpaths, exercise against a backdrop of colonial facades, and stop for photos with the historic buildings and the warm evening sky.

The atmosphere draws comparisons to **Yogyakarta's Zero Kilometer district** — but with a quieter, more intimate character that suits North Bali's personality perfectly.

## Bupati Sutjidra's Vision for North Bali Tourism

Buleleng Regent Nyoman Sutjidra frames the project as more than just urban beautification. He envisions the Zero Point heritage zone as a new anchor for tourism in North Bali, with a planned **heritage walking trail** connecting the restored district to the old Singaraja harbor, the Chinese quarter, and other colonial-era landmarks scattered across the city.

"We want the identity of Singaraja as a historic city to come alive again, so it can be enjoyed and learned from by both local residents and visitors," said Bupati Sutjidra.

## North Bali's Moment to Shine

For years, Bali's northern coast has played second fiddle to the glamour of the south. The restoration of the Singaraja Zero Point sends a clear signal: North Bali is investing in depth — in history, architecture, and identity — rather than competing with Kuta and Seminyak on their own terms.

For travelers seeking a more reflective, historically rich experience in Bali, Singaraja's revived heritage district may soon become an unmissable stop on the island's emerging cultural tourism circuit.
