A total of 16 long-tailed macaques (*Macaca fascicularis*) have been voluntarily surrendered by Bali residents to the Natural Resources Conservation Agency (BKSDA) of Bali since late 2025 through mid-June 2026. This positive development is a direct result of a gubernatorial circular prohibiting residents from keeping the primates without official permits.

## Governor's Circular Proves a Turning Point

Bali Governor Wayan Koster issued Circular Letter Number 19 of 2025, formally prohibiting Bali residents from keeping long-tailed macaques. This makes Bali the only province in Indonesia to have enacted such a regulation—a bold step that sets the island apart in wildlife governance.

BKSDA Bali Head Ratna Hendratmoko stated that the circular has been a genuine turning point. Residents are now far more aware of the risks associated with keeping wild primates, and many are proactively surrendering their animals to authorities.

## Why the Ban Was Necessary

Three primary reasons underpinned the decision to restrict ownership of long-tailed macaques:

### Rabies and Zoonotic Disease Risk

Although not classified as a protected species, long-tailed macaques can transmit rabies and other zoonotic diseases. The risk is particularly acute for children who frequently have close physical contact with the animals.

### Exploitation for Entertainment

Many captive macaques end up being exploited for street performances or social media content. These conditions subject the animals to chronic stress and fail to meet even basic animal welfare standards.

### Ecosystem Balance

In the wild, long-tailed macaques play a vital role in maintaining the balance of Bali's forest ecosystems. Illegal trade and private ownership deplete wild populations and disrupt natural habitats.

## Rehabilitation and Reintroduction Plan

Of the 16 macaques surrendered so far:

- **6 individuals** are currently undergoing rehabilitation at the BKSDA Bali facility
- **10 individuals** have been temporarily placed with the Indonesian Animal Network (JSI)

All animals will undergo thorough health and behavioral assessments before being released back into their natural habitat. BKSDA emphasizes that reintroduction will only proceed once each primate is physically and psychologically ready.

## An Option for Those Who Want to Keep Them

BKSDA Bali has not completely closed the door for residents who genuinely wish to keep long-tailed macaques. Those interested must obtain official permits, sign declarations that they will not display the animals publicly, and demonstrate commitment to proper animal welfare standards.

Residents who still have long-tailed macaques are encouraged to contact their nearest BKSDA office for a safe, penalty-free handover process.

## A Sign of Growing Environmental Awareness

The voluntary mass surrender of wild animals reflects a broader shift in environmental consciousness among Bali's residents. Amid increasing pressure from mass tourism and development, wildlife protection measures like this one contribute to preserving the natural balance that makes Bali unique in the first place.
