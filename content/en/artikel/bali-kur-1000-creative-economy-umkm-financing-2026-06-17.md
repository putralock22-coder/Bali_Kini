A significant shift in how Indonesia finances its creative industries unfolded in Bali in May 2026. The government, in partnership with BRI (Bank Rakyat Indonesia), conducted a mass loan signing ceremony for 1,000 creative economy SMEs (UMKM Ekraf) in Bali as part of the Creative Economy SMEs and Outstanding Entrepreneurship Fair for Bali Province. The event was more than a financial transaction — it marked a new chapter in Indonesia's approach to unlocking capital for its most culturally vibrant sector.

## Intellectual Property as Collateral: A Game-Changer

The most groundbreaking element of this program is the formal recognition of Intellectual Property Rights (HKI — Hak Kekayaan Intelektual) as legitimate collateral for government-backed KUR (Kredit Usaha Rakyat) loans. For years, creative entrepreneurs — batik makers, digital designers, musicians, photographers, and traditional craftspeople — faced near-impossible barriers when seeking formal bank loans, as they lacked the physical assets (land, vehicles, machinery) that conventional lending requires.

Under this new framework, a copyright registration, a trademark certificate, or a patent can serve as the basis for accessing credit. This is a fundamental reimagining of what counts as economic value in a knowledge and creativity-driven economy.

## BRI's Rp65.95 Trillion National KUR Portfolio

The scale of the broader program underlines the government's commitment. BRI had disbursed Rp65.95 trillion (approximately USD 4.1 billion) in KUR loans to around 1.3 million borrowers across Indonesia through April 2026. Of Indonesia's 21 recognized creative industry subsectors, 13 are now formally eligible for KUR financing — including culinary arts, fashion, crafts, music, film and video, photography, visual communication design, and digital content creation.

### Bali as a Creative Economy Flagship

Bali was selected as a priority province for this initiative not by coincidence. The island is home to tens of thousands of artisans, craftspeople, designers, performing artists, and creative entrepreneurs whose work has found international markets. Yet many have historically relied on personal savings or informal loans because the formal banking system did not accommodate their asset profiles. This program directly addresses that structural gap.

## Digitalization and Market Expansion

The KUR mass signing was embedded in a broader initiative to accelerate the digitalization of creative businesses. Participants were encouraged to adopt digital marketing tools — from e-commerce platforms to social media — to reach customers beyond the tourists who physically visit Bali. With global online marketplaces now accessible from a Ubud studio or a Klungkung workshop, the geographic constraints that once limited Balinese artisans' market reach are rapidly dissolving.

## A Multi-Ministry Collaboration

The program brought together the Ministry of Creative Economy, the Ministry of SMEs, BRI, the Bali Provincial Government, and local universities — a coalition reflecting the view that sustainable SME growth requires more than just capital. Technical assistance, HKI registration support, digital literacy training, and market access facilitation are all components of the broader ecosystem being built around this initiative.

## Wider Economic Impact

By unlocking financing for Bali's creative sector, the government expects a multiplier effect: more businesses scaling up, more employment in creative industries, and more high-value products reaching export markets. Bali, long celebrated as a tourism destination, is steadily building its identity as a creative economy powerhouse — one where culture is not just preserved, but economically productive.
