As geopolitical conflicts continue to roil global financial markets — and with the Indonesian rupiah under sustained pressure against the US dollar — Bali's tourism sector has demonstrated remarkable resilience. Bamsoet (Bambang Soesatyo), Chairman of the Indonesian People's Consultative Assembly (MPR RI), has pointed to Bali's steady visitor numbers as proof that the island holds an irreplaceable position on the world tourism stage.

## Foreign Tourists Keep Coming

According to Bamsoet, international tourist arrivals at I Gusti Ngurah Rai International Airport in Denpasar remained solid at approximately 20,355 international passengers per day as of early June 2026. This figure signals that global uncertainty — including escalating conflicts in the Middle East and geopolitical tensions elsewhere — has not meaningfully deterred travelers from choosing Bali.

"Bali is a world destination that is very hard to replace," Bamsoet said, adding that the island's unique cultural identity, natural beauty, and spiritual depth give it a competitive advantage that transcends temporary geopolitical headwinds.

## A Track Record of Recovery

Bamsoet highlighted the tested resilience of Bali's tourism industry, which has survived multiple waves of crisis over recent decades. The island endured the Asian financial crisis of the late 1990s, the devastating Bali bombings in 2002 and 2005, volcanic eruptions at Mount Agung in 2017–2019, and most severely, the COVID-19 pandemic that brought international tourism to a near-complete halt for nearly two years.

Each time, the industry rebuilt itself — often emerging stronger and more diversified than before.

"Bali's tourism operators are highly adaptive. When one market slows down, they pivot quickly to other growing markets," Bamsoet observed.

## The Silver Lining of a Weaker Rupiah

Counterintuitively, the weakening rupiah carries a positive effect for inbound international tourism. When the rupiah depreciates against major currencies, the cost of holidaying in Indonesia becomes relatively cheaper for foreign visitors paying in dollars, euros, or British pounds.

Accommodation, dining, transport, and tourist attractions in Bali all effectively become more affordable when priced in hard currency. This dynamic can encourage longer stays and higher per-visitor spending — translating into greater foreign exchange earnings for Indonesia, with Bali as the primary beneficiary.

## Keeping Competitive in Southeast Asia

Bamsoet called on both the government and private sector to continue strengthening Bali's competitive advantages. Thailand, Vietnam, the Maldives, and Japan are all competing aggressively for the same pool of international tourists — making it essential for Bali to maintain consistent quality across accommodation, hospitality, cultural experiences, and infrastructure.

With a proven track record of weathering crises and an industry built on adaptability, Bali's tourism sector appears well-positioned to navigate ongoing global turbulence and sustain its long-term growth path through the remainder of 2026 and beyond.
