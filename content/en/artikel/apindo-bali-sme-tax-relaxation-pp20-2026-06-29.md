The Indonesian Employers Association (Apindo) in Bali has warmly welcomed a new central government regulation that relaxes the Final Income Tax (PPh Final) rate for small and medium enterprises (SMEs). Government Regulation No. 20 of 2026 — commonly referred to as PP 20/2026 — is seen as a significant step forward in supporting SME growth across the island.

## The Key Change: No More Time Limit

The most impactful change introduced by PP 20/2026 is the removal of the time restriction on the 0.5% PPh Final facility. Under the previous regulation, individual taxpayers who qualified as SMEs could only benefit from the reduced 0.5% rate for a maximum of seven years, after which they would face a jump to the standard income tax rate.

Under the new regulation, the 0.5% rate applies **indefinitely** — as long as the business continues to meet the revenue threshold of Rp4.8 billion (approximately USD 295,000) per year. This permanent status gives small business owners far greater certainty in their long-term financial planning.

## Apindo Bali Chairman Welcomes the Policy

**I Nengah Nurlaba**, Chairman of Apindo Bali, expressed his appreciation for the policy shift, highlighting its practical benefit for SME operators across the province.

> "This policy increases the opportunity for SMEs to move up in class. It gives businesses more working capital that can be reinvested directly into growing the enterprise," said Nurlaba.

He added that Apindo Bali hopes the regulation will also encourage more informal business operators to register and enter the formal tax system, given that the rate is now stable and affordable over the long term.

## Who Qualifies?

While the relaxation is broadly positive, PP 20/2026 also tightens the definition of eligible taxpayers. The 0.5% PPh Final rate now specifically applies to:

- **Individual taxpayers** with annual turnover below Rp4.8 billion
- **Single-person limited liability companies (PT Perorangan)** established by a single individual
- **Cooperatives** meeting the stipulated revenue requirements

Business entities structured as partnerships or firms no longer fall under the new facility. This narrowing of scope is partly intended to prevent the common practice of artificially splitting businesses to stay below the revenue threshold and avoid higher tax obligations.

## What This Means for Bali's SME Ecosystem

For Bali's vast and diverse SME community — ranging from souvenir makers in Ubud and warung owners in Seminyak to small villa operators in Canggu and home industry producers in rural Tabanan — this policy change is a meaningful relief.

The combination of a low flat rate and indefinite validity reduces both the tax burden and the administrative complexity that often discourages small operators from formalizing their businesses. With greater clarity on their tax obligations, SME owners can redirect their attention to what matters most: improving their products, expanding their markets, and creating more local jobs.

## SMEs as the Foundation of Bali's Economy

SMEs are not peripheral to Bali's economy — they are its backbone. They form the supply chain that keeps the tourism industry running, providing everything from handcrafted goods and local food to small-scale hospitality services. During periods when large resorts and hotels face downturns, it is often the SME sector that keeps local purchasing power alive.

Tax policies that support SMEs therefore have a multiplier effect on the broader economy. Apindo Bali is calling on all eligible businesses to promptly seek information on how to apply for and take advantage of this facility before the end of the fiscal year.
