Indonesia's Vice Minister of Tourism, **Ni Luh Enik Ermawati**, has issued a strong call to action for Bali's hospitality sector: hotels, restaurants, and cafes must take direct responsibility for their waste — or risk undermining the very reputation that makes Bali the world's most coveted tropical destination.

The message was delivered at a high-level coordination meeting on **improving the role of the HOREKA sector** (Hotels, Restaurants, Cafes) **in waste management**, held at Hotel The Meru Sanur on June 9, 2026. The meeting was jointly organized by the Ministry of Environment and the Ministry of Tourism.

## The Uncomfortable Numbers

The statistics presented at the meeting painted a stark picture:

- **Only 22.6%** of HOREKA businesses in Bali independently process their organic waste
- Bali ranks **8th nationally** in total waste generation — a troubling figure for an island that markets itself as a pristine tropical paradise
- Tens of thousands of hotel rooms, restaurants, warungs, and beach clubs generate massive daily volumes of food waste, single-use packaging, and kitchen scraps

The gap between Bali's green image and its waste reality is not lost on the Vice Minister:

> *"Poor waste management directly translates to negative tourist reviews, declining destination ratings, and ultimately reduced foreign exchange earnings from tourism — which currently reach Rp176 trillion annually."*

## Three Pathways for the Hotel and Restaurant Sector

The Ministry of Environment presented three practical options for HOREKA operators to come into compliance:

1. **Third-party partnerships:** Contract with government-verified waste management companies
2. **Free technical assistance:** Government-provided guidance for operators who want to build in-house waste processing capability
3. **Collective management schemes:** Neighboring hotels and restaurants in the same district pool resources to share waste processing infrastructure, reducing individual costs

The third option is seen as particularly viable for smaller guesthouses and warungs that lack the capital to invest individually but together could manage a shared composting or biogas facility.

## Five Policy Recommendations from Kemenpar

The Ministry of Tourism proposed five strategic steps to accelerate compliance across the sector:

1. Clearer timelines for evaluation and enforcement
2. Expanded outreach, socialization, and on-the-ground technical support
3. Better alignment between national and local regulations
4. Prioritizing education and capacity-building before punitive enforcement
5. Strengthening the supervisory role of district and city governments

## The Broader Context: Bali's Zero-Waste Ambition

This HOREKA push is part of a larger, historic policy shift in Bali's waste management landscape. On June 10, 2026, the Minister of Environment together with Governor I Wayan Koster formally declared Bali a **national pilot for 100% household waste sorting**, with mandatory separation of waste at the source required from **July 1, 2026** — covering households, traditional villages (*banjar*), markets, hotels, and offices alike.

The earlier shock therapy measure — banning organic waste from the Suwung landfill starting April 2026 — already cut waste volumes at the facility by 60% in just weeks. Now, the government wants to replicate that success across the entire commercial tourism sector.

## Why This Matters for Bali's Global Standing

Bali was recently named one of Tripadvisor's best destinations in the world and continues to attract millions of international visitors annually. But sustainability concerns are increasingly influencing traveler decisions, especially among environmentally conscious visitors from Europe, Australia, and North America.

A Bali that fails to manage its own commercial waste sends a damaging signal — one that no amount of marketing can fully offset. The Vice Minister's message is clear: being a world-class tourism destination requires world-class environmental stewardship, and that starts in every hotel kitchen and restaurant stockroom on the island.
