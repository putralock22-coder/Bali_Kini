Bali's immigration authorities deported **342 foreign nationals** during the first half of 2026, according to data released by the Directorate General of Immigration. The figure reflects a sweeping crackdown across all immigration offices on the island as authorities move decisively against those who exploit Indonesia's visa system.

## What Violations Were Found?

Immigration data for the January–June 2026 period identifies the following categories of violations that led to deportations:

- **Overstay** — remaining in Indonesia beyond the expiry of a valid visa or residence permit
- **Visa misuse** — using tourist visas to engage in professional or commercial activities
- **Working without authorization** — conducting paid work without the required work permit or appropriate visa
- **Fictitious investment** — exploiting loopholes by fabricating investment activity to obtain longer residence permits
- **Violations of public order and local customs** — behavior deemed disruptive to Bali's social and cultural norms

Fictitious investment cases drew particular attention, as this scheme involves foreign nationals falsely claiming to invest in Bali to secure extended stay permissions without genuinely committing capital to the local economy.

## Coordinated Operations Across All Bali Immigration Offices

The enforcement effort involved a coordinated sweep by every immigration authority operating in Bali:

- **Class I Special Immigration Office, TPI Ngurah Rai**
- **Class I Immigration Office, TPI Denpasar**
- **Class II Immigration Office, TPI Singaraja**
- **Class III Non-TPI Immigration Office, Tabanan**
- **Class III Non-TPI Immigration Office, Klungkung**
- **Immigration Detention Center (Rudenim), Denpasar**

Each unit conducted tactical operations, moving through residential areas, villas, cafés, co-working spaces, and other locations commonly used by foreign nationals. Those found in violation faced **deportation and a re-entry ban** — meaning they are not only expelled from Indonesia but are prohibited from returning for a set period.

## The Broader Context: Bali's Tourism Quality Shift

These deportations come as Bali works to reposition itself from a mass tourism model toward quality, sustainable tourism. A central challenge has been the proliferation of foreign nationals who enter on tourist visas but work remotely as digital nomads — technically a violation of Indonesian visa regulations, regardless of where their employer is based.

Bali and national immigration authorities have repeatedly made their position clear: foreign visitors are warmly welcomed, but must comply with the visa conditions under which they enter. Working, operating a business, or engaging in commercial activity without the appropriate permit is a serious legal violation that will be enforced.

## Accelerating Enforcement

The pace of enforcement has been intensifying. By May 2026, 334 foreign nationals had already been sanctioned in just the first four months of the year — suggesting a sharp acceleration in the final two months of the first half. This trajectory indicates that Bali's immigration apparatus is not merely reacting to individual cases but pursuing a systematic policy of enforcement.

The crackdown is part of a long-term strategy to ensure Bali remains a safe, orderly destination governed by the rule of law — while also protecting local economic opportunities from unfair competition with unregistered foreign workers.
