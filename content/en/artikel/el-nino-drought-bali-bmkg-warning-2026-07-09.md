Bali is bracing for serious drought conditions as the El Niño phenomenon continues to intensify. Indonesia's Meteorology, Climatology, and Geophysics Agency (BMKG), in coordination with Bali's Regional Disaster Management Agency (BPBD), has issued formal early warnings in early July 2026, urging residents, farmers, and local authorities to take immediate precautionary steps ahead of what could be the island's most prolonged dry season in years.

## El Niño Index Hits Alarming Levels

The latest BMKG data shows the El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO) index has climbed to **+1.61** — a figure that has remained above normal thresholds for seven consecutive 10-day periods. As a result, the probability of El Niño strengthening into the "strong" category has now reached **98 percent**.

For an island that depends heavily on agriculture and tourism, the implications are significant. A prolonged and intense drought could simultaneously disrupt crop yields, water availability at resorts and hotels, and the natural beauty that draws millions of visitors to Bali each year.

## Northern Bali Placed on Drought Alert

Bali's northern region, particularly Gerokgak District in Buleleng Regency, has been officially designated as being in a **drought alert** (Siaga Kekeringan) status. The designation follows a drastic drop in atmospheric humidity levels recorded across the area — one of Bali's most historically vulnerable zones during El Niño events.

BMKG's national early-warning bulletin for July 2026 lists Bali among the regions requiring heightened vigilance, with specific subdistricts flagged at either the alert or caution level.

### Peak Dry Season Expected in August and September

While current conditions are already a cause for concern, most of Bali's territory is projected to reach the peak of the dry season only in August and September 2026. This means the most critical period is still weeks away, and the window for effective preparation is narrowing rapidly.

BPBD Bali projects that the prolonged dry season could extend well into late September — significantly longer than the typical annual pattern.

## Unusual Spike in Building Fires

One early and visible consequence has already emerged: a notable increase in fire incidents affecting civilian buildings across Bali during this dry season. BPBD Bali has recorded an anomalous rise in fires at houses, shops, and traditional markets — a pattern not seen in previous years and closely linked to the extremely dry air conditions.

Although no official reports of community water shortages have been filed as of early July, authorities are urging proactive caution rather than reactive response.

### Recommended Precautions

BPBD Bali and BMKG are advising a range of mitigation steps:

- Significantly reduce daily water consumption
- Avoid burning waste or clearing land by fire during the dry period
- Check electrical installations and gas appliances regularly to reduce fire risk
- Farmers in rain-fed fields are advised to adjust planting schedules or delay new plantings

## The Broader Stakes for Bali

This El Niño alert arrives at a critical moment for Bali, which is in the midst of a strong post-pandemic tourism recovery. Projections for 2026 point to over 16 million tourist arrivals. Any disruption to water supplies — whether affecting rice terraces that attract eco-tourists or hotel pools that serve beach guests — could carry real economic costs.

BMKG is calling on local governments and communities to use the remaining lead time before the peak dry season to invest in water storage infrastructure, community water reserves, and heightened fire-prevention awareness, so that the island's people and its tourism landscape can weather what may be a challenging few months ahead.
