**Bank Indonesia (BI) Bali Representative Office** and the **Regional Inflation Control Team (TPID)** have confirmed that their coordinated intervention strategy has successfully kept Bali's price pressures within safe bounds. Bali's year-on-year inflation rate for May 2026 was recorded at **2.99 percent** — still comfortably within Indonesia's national inflation target range of 1.5 to 3.5 percent.

## The Four Pillars of the 4K Strategy

The cornerstone of Bali's inflation management success is the consistent, cross-sector implementation of the **4K strategy** — a framework built around four interconnected pillars:

1. **Keterjangkauan Harga (Price Affordability)** — ensuring that essential commodity prices remain accessible for all segments of society, especially before major religious holidays and school holiday periods
2. **Ketersediaan Pasokan (Supply Availability)** — guaranteeing sufficient food stocks across all parts of Bali, including remote areas
3. **Kelancaran Distribusi (Distribution Smoothness)** — clearing bottlenecks so goods flow from producers to consumers without disruption
4. **Komunikasi Efektif (Effective Communication)** — disseminating price and market condition information transparently to prevent panic buying

### On-the-Ground Execution

The 4K strategy is translated into concrete actions that involve all eight regencies and cities across Bali. **Market operations (operasi pasar)** serve as the primary tool, conducted on a regular schedule according to the **3T principle: right time, right location, right target**.

Intensive market operations are deployed especially ahead of major religious celebrations — including Galungan, Kuningan, and Nyepi — and during school holiday seasons when household consumption demand surges sharply. Operation sites are selected strategically at locations historically prone to sudden price spikes.

## Strengthened Cross-Sector Synergy

The effectiveness of the 4K strategy does not rest on BI and TPID alone. **Village-Owned Enterprises (BUMDes)**, regional food companies (Perumda pangan), and cooperatives serve as key partners in ensuring supply chains function smoothly down to the village level.

**Inter-regional cooperation** has also been intensified. Since Bali imports a substantial portion of its food supply from outside the island — primarily from Java and Lombok — cross-provincial coordination is essential to guarantee smooth delivery of strategic commodities including rice, chili peppers, shallots, and cooking oil.

### SPHP Distribution Monitoring

One of the key instruments being optimized is **SPHP (Food Supply and Price Stabilization)** — the government program ensuring availability of medium-grade rice at controlled prices. BI and TPID have tightened monitoring of SPHP distribution acceleration to prevent delivery delays that could trigger retail price spikes.

## Watchfulness for Future Pressures

Despite May 2026 inflation remaining under control, BI Bali is not lowering its guard against potential pressure in the months ahead. **July 2026 and October 2026** have been identified as periods requiring extra vigilance.

July 2026 represents the peak international tourist season, bringing a surge of foreign visitors that historically drives up demand — and prices — in accommodation, food and beverage, and transportation segments. October 2026, meanwhile, tends to coincide with major religious celebrations that boost domestic consumption.

### Annual Inflation Target

Bank Indonesia remains broadly optimistic that national inflation in 2026 can be maintained within a range of **2.5 percent plus or minus 1 percent** — consistent with the government's established target. For Bali specifically, the BI-TPID coordination framework that has proven effective in recent months is expected to keep regional inflation broadly in line with the national benchmark.

## Strengthening Local Food Production

Beyond short-term market interventions, the medium-term strategy of BI and TPID also encompasses **strengthening local agricultural production** within Bali itself. The island has latent capacity to produce rice, vegetables, and fruits that, if optimized, could meaningfully reduce dependence on supplies from the mainland.

This dimension of the strategy includes farmer group development, facilitating access to agricultural financing, and supporting adoption of modern farming technology. Greater self-sufficiency in food — even if 100 percent cannot be achieved — is viewed as a critical buffer against inflation shocks triggered by disruptions to cross-island distribution chains.

Looking ahead, BI Bali plans to expand the scope of real-time price monitoring through the digitalization of traditional market reporting systems, enabling faster and more precisely targeted price interventions whenever needed.
