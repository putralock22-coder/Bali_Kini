In a powerful show of environmental commitment, Bali Governor **Wayan Koster** and Indonesia's **Minister of Environment Mohammad Jumhur Hidayat** jointly led a large-scale mangrove planting event at the **Mangrove Arboretum Park in Pedungan, South Denpasar**, on Wednesday, June 10, 2026.

## From 18 Hectares of Damage to Near-Recovery

The restoration effort targets a coastal area that had experienced serious ecological deterioration. Governor Koster disclosed that approximately **18 hectares** of mangrove forest had been damaged over the years — a result of land conversion and infrastructure encroachment along the coastline.

Rehabilitation work began around **2019–2020**, and the progress is encouraging. Of the original 18 hectares of damage, only **2 hectares** remain unplanted as of this event. During the June 10 activity, participants planted **3,000 mangrove seedlings**, bringing the recovery effort closer to its final goal.

## A Cross-Sector Collaboration

What made this event stand out was the breadth of participation. The planting activity was not a government affair alone — it drew volunteers from across society, including:

- Central and regional government officials
- **Indonesian National Armed Forces (TNI)** and **National Police (Polri)** personnel
- Students from schools and universities across Bali
- Environmental communities and civil society organizations

The direct involvement of the Environment Minister — traveling from Jakarta to Denpasar for this occasion — underscored the national significance of Bali's mangrove ecosystem.

## Why Mangroves Matter: Three Vital Functions

Minister Jumhur Hidayat was emphatic that mangrove planting must be seen as **meaningful conservation action**, not ceremonial symbolism. Healthy mangrove forests serve three critical ecological roles:

1. **Coastal protection** — acting as natural buffers against erosion, storms, and sea-level rise
2. **Carbon sequestration** — mangroves are among the most effective carbon sinks on earth
3. **Marine habitat** — nursery grounds for fish, shrimp, and other coastal species essential to local livelihoods and biodiversity

For Bali — a world-renowned tourism destination — maintaining healthy coastlines is both an environmental imperative and an economic one.

## Denpasar Mayor: Collaboration Is Key

Denpasar's Mayor also stressed the importance of **multi-stakeholder collaboration** in coastal management. Civil society, environmental groups, and youth are recognized as equally vital players alongside government policy in protecting coastal ecosystems over the long term.

The Mangrove Arboretum Park in Pedungan has served as a key conservation and education zone, doubling as an eco-tourism site where visitors can learn about the importance of mangroves firsthand.

## Looking Ahead: World Mangrove Day, July 28

The June 10 event is just one chapter in a larger story. The national government has announced plans for a **significantly larger mangrove planting event in Bali** to coincide with **World Mangrove Day on July 28, 2026**. That event will involve multiple ministries, labor unions, NGOs, and community organizations from across Indonesia.

Bali has been selected as one of the primary venues — a recognition of the island's role as a model for balancing tourism with environmental stewardship.

As the remaining 2 hectares move toward full restoration, Bali's mangrove forests are well on their way to functioning once again as thriving coastal ecosystems — protecting the shoreline, absorbing carbon, and welcoming the marine life that makes these waters so extraordinary.
