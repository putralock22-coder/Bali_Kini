The Association of Indonesian Travel Agencies (ASITA) Bali has placed **village tourism** at the center of its product diversification strategy for the island's tourism sector. Rather than chasing visitor numbers, the association is focused on increasing length of stay and per-visitor spending — hallmarks of quality tourism that benefit local communities more directly.

## Village Tourism as Community Economic Engine

ASITA Bali Chairman **I Putu Winastra** underscored the strategic importance of village tourism within Bali's broader tourism ecosystem. In his view, the strength of village tourism directly translates to the strength of local community economies.

"Strong village tourism means a strong community economy," he said. Each village tourism destination offers its own unique character — authentic cultural experiences, traditional crafts, and living heritage — that cannot be replicated by conventional urban tourism packages.

The core objective of the diversification strategy is clear: **maximize tourist spending**, not merely optimize visitor arrivals. By encouraging tourists to explore Bali's diverse village destinations, their stays naturally lengthen and their spending expands across more of the island's communities.

## Foreign Tourist Spending on an Upward Trend

Data from Indonesia's Central Statistics Agency (BPS) reflects an encouraging trend in foreign visitor spending in Bali. In 2025, the average spend by international tourists reached **IDR 2.11 million per night** — up IDR 60,000 from the 2024 figure of IDR 2.05 million per night.

While part of the increase is attributed to the strengthening US dollar exchange rate, the upward trajectory is nonetheless a positive signal. ASITA Bali is targeting continued growth in this figure as its product and market diversification efforts gain momentum.

## Eight Tourism Trends Defining Bali in 2026

ASITA Bali has released a forward-looking map of the **eight dominant tourism trends** expected to shape Bali's market through 2026:

1. **Cultural Tourism** — immersive experiences centered on Balinese Hindu traditions and rituals
2. **Wellness & Healing Premium** — spas, meditation retreats, and holistic recovery programs
3. **Village Tourism** — authentic rural experiences including rice farming, traditional crafts, and local cuisine
4. **Eco Tour** — responsible, ecologically conscious travel experiences
5. **Digital Nomad** — long-stay packages catering to remote workers based in Bali
6. **Family Private Travel** — exclusive, customized itineraries for family groups
7. **Green Mobility** — tourism travel powered by electric vehicles
8. **Adventure Curated** — bespoke sunrise treks, diving expeditions, and guided photography tours

## Expanding into New Markets

Alongside product diversification, ASITA Bali is pursuing **market source diversification**. Established markets including Australia, China, and India remain priorities, but the association is also turning attention to **Central Asia** as an under-tapped growth segment.

This market expansion is particularly timely. The competitive landscape for regional tourism has never been more intense, with destinations like Koh Samui and Phu Quoc aggressively courting international travelers. Reaching new visitor segments is essential for maintaining stable, long-term growth.

### 2026 Visitor Targets

Bali and its tourism stakeholders have set a target of **6.6 million international visitor arrivals** for 2026, with a clear emphasis on spending quality over volume. From January through April 2026, a total of **2,019,892 foreign tourists** arrived directly in Bali — a marginal 1.11 percent decline compared to the same period in 2025.

ASITA Bali remains optimistic that the annual target is achievable, provided that product and market diversification strategies are implemented consistently throughout the remainder of the year.

## Unlocking Opportunity for Local SMEs

ASITA's village tourism push opens substantial opportunities for **micro, small, and medium enterprises (SMEs)** across Bali's rural areas. From weavers and wood carvers to farmers offering agrotourism experiences and performers presenting traditional dance — all stand to benefit from greater direct access to a more diverse pool of visitors.

This initiative aligns with the broader vision of making Bali a tourism destination that not only delights its visitors, but also distributes its prosperity equitably across all layers of the local community.
