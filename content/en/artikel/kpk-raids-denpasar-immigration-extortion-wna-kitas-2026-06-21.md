## KPK Conducts Three-Site Raid Across Bali

Indonesia's Corruption Eradication Commission (KPK) executed search-and-seizure operations across three locations in Bali between June 17 and 19, 2026, as part of its investigation into alleged systematic extortion targeting foreign nationals. The three sites raided were the **Denpasar Class I TPI Immigration Office (Kantor Imigrasi Kelas I TPI Denpasar)**, **PT Visa Empat Bali**, and **CV Visa Agung Bali Teratai Promanende**.

During the raids, KPK investigators secured significant quantities of **documents and electronic devices** believed to be directly connected to an organized extortion scheme operating within Bali's immigration system.

## The Scheme: Extorting Foreigners Over Residency Permits

The KPK investigation centers on alleged extortion targeting foreign nationals seeking to process their **Kartu Izin Tinggal Terbatas (KITAS)**—a limited stay permit—and **Kartu Izin Tinggal Tetap (KITAP)**—a permanent stay permit. Suspects allegedly demanded unofficial payments beyond the legal fees as a condition for processing these documents smoothly.

KITAS and KITAP are essential documents for foreigners living and working in Indonesia for extended periods. With a substantial expat population in Bali—including digital nomads, retirees, and international business owners—immigration document processing represents a market particularly vulnerable to abuse.

## 8 Suspects Named, Including Former Director General of Immigration

KPK has named **eight suspects** in the case. The most high-profile name is **Silmy Karim**, the former Director General of Immigration who served in that role from 2023 to 2024. The other seven suspects are Saffar Muhammad Godam, Jaya Saputra, Ronald Arman Abdullah, Tessar Bayu Setyaji, Bagus Bramantyo, Juniadi Sri Priambudi, and Gusti Benardiansyah.

The total value of the alleged extortion scheme under investigation is estimated at **Rp145.5 billion**—a figure that speaks to the scale and longevity of the corrupt practices within Bali's immigration apparatus.

## Origins: A Sting Operation in Early June 2026

The case traces back to a **sting operation (Operasi Tangkap Tangan/OTT)** carried out by KPK on June 2–3, 2026. In that operation, authorities apprehended **17 individuals simultaneously**—comprising eight state officials or civil servants (ASN) and nine private-sector parties. This initial operation was subsequently developed into a full investigation, culminating in the three-site raid across Bali in mid-June 2026.

The pattern of the raids signals that the extortion ring was not the work of isolated bad actors, but a structured and institutionalized scheme involving players both inside and outside the government.

## Implications for Bali's Expat and Tourism Ecosystem

Bali has long been celebrated as one of the world's most welcoming destinations for expatriates. The presence of digital nomads, foreign retirees, and international professionals contributes meaningfully to the local economy—through property rental, service consumption, and direct investment. However, extortion practices in the immigration bureaucracy—such as those now under KPK scrutiny—risk damaging Bali's reputation as a safe and trustworthy base for foreign residents.

The Bali Provincial Government and the Ministry of Law and Human Rights had previously listed immigration service reform in Bali as a priority, as part of a broader **integrated immigration pilot program**. The exposure of this case may, paradoxically, accelerate the clean-up and overhaul of immigration bureaucracy, helping ensure Bali retains its global standing as a premier destination.

KPK confirmed that investigations are ongoing and that additional suspects or related cases may emerge as the probe advances.
