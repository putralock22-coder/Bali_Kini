June in Bali marks the deepest transition into the dry season (musim kemarau). For travellers, this means reliable sunshine, low humidity in coastal areas, and near-zero chance of the all-day rain that can disrupt itineraries between November and March. For Balinese farmers and fishermen, it means the beginning of a months-long water management challenge.

Here is the complete weather picture for Bali in June 2026, based on BMKG Wilayah III Denpasar's latest data.

**Traveller note:** Bangli province (which includes the Kintamani-Batur volcano area) runs 10–12°C cooler than coastal Bali. If planning a sunrise volcano trek, a light jacket is essential — temperatures at the 1,717m crater rim before dawn can drop below 12°C.

## Wave Warning Update

BMKG issued a **high-wave warning for 1–4 June** for waters south of Bali, with swells reaching 2.5–4 metres in the open ocean and 1.5–2.5 metres in nearshore zones.

As of 4 June, the warning has expired and sea conditions are improving.

**Current sea state:**
- North Bali (Buleleng): 0.5–1.25 m — safe for most marine activities
- South Bali (nearshore): 0.5–2.0 m — calming, exercise caution
- South Bali (offshore): up to 2.5 m — recreational vessels should check with operators

For **Nusa Penida fast boats** operating from Sanur: operators are resuming normal schedules. Morning crossings (departing before 10:00) benefit from calmer conditions before afternoon trade winds build.

## Drought Season Outlook

BMKG and BPBD Bali (Provincial Disaster Management Agency) are tracking an intensifying drought pattern that is projected to worsen through August–September.

**Current drought extent (as of early June):**
- Areas already experiencing dry conditions: ~30% of Bali, concentrated in Nusa Penida, eastern Klungkung, and parts of Gianyar

**Projected by September:**
- Approximately **90% of Bali's land area** will be experiencing drought conditions
- Peak severity expected: **August 2026**
- Temperatures forecast to exceed 2025 levels by 1–2°C in most regions

The provincial government has allocated **Rp50 billion (BTT emergency funds)** for drought response, covering emergency water distribution to affected villages and fire prevention in dry-forest areas.

**Implications for visitors:**
- Rice terraces and agricultural landscapes may look drier than usual by July–August
- Water rationing is possible in some rural accommodations — check with your property
- Wildfire smoke is occasionally visible in northern Bali during prolonged dry spells; monitor BMKG air quality updates

**June sits at the sweet spot:** sunshine reliable, temperatures pleasant (not yet at August's peak heat), and the island's cultural calendar is at its richest — Galungan (17 June), Bali Arts Festival (13 June–11 July), and Bali Kite Festival (6–7 June) all fall in the same month.

## Practical Weather Tips

**Sun protection:** UV index in June averages 9–11 (Very High to Extreme). Apply SPF 50+ 20 minutes before outdoor activity, reapply every 90 minutes, and seek shade between 10:00–14:00.

**Hydration:** Dehydration is the most common health complaint among visitors. Carry at least 1.5 litres of water during any outdoor activity. Electrolyte sachets are available cheaply at all pharmacies.

**Ocean swimming:** Check the flag system at each beach. Even in dry season, the Indian Ocean's south swell can generate dangerous shore breaks — red flags are not suggestions.

**Morning vs afternoon:** Nearly all of Bali's best outdoor experiences — volcanic treks, snorkelling, temple visits — are better in the morning. By early afternoon, coastal haze and heat reduce visibility and comfort significantly.

June 2026 is one of the best times of year to be in Bali. The weather cooperates, the festivals are extraordinary, and the island is running at its most vibrant. Pack light, bring sunscreen, and go early.
