One of Bali's most anticipated infrastructure projects has undergone a major transformation. Indonesia's Ministry of Public Works (Kementerian PU) has officially announced a significant rescoping of the Gilimanuk-Mengwi Toll Road — a project originally spanning nearly 97 kilometers that has now been reduced to approximately **42 kilometers**, covering only the Pekutatan-to-Mengwi corridor. Section I, connecting Gilimanuk to Pekutatan, has been formally cancelled.

## From 97 km to 42 km: What Changed?

The Gilimanuk-Mengwi Toll Road was originally envisioned as a major east-west connectivity solution for western and southern Bali, stretching from the ferry port of Gilimanuk all the way to Mengwi in Badung. However, following a thorough review by the Ministry of PU, the decision was made to continue with only two sections:

- **Section II: Pekutatan–Soka**
- **Section III: Soka–Mengwi**

Together, these two sections form a route of approximately 42 kilometers. **Section I (Gilimanuk–Pekutatan)** has been officially dropped from the project, as it was deemed economically unfeasible.

## The Core Reason: Investors Stayed Away

This decision did not emerge overnight. According to the Acting Director General of Infrastructure Financing at the Ministry of Public Works, the original project faced serious difficulties attracting private investors. The capital expenditure required for a nearly 97-kilometer toll road was simply too large to generate an attractive return on investment for prospective business entities.

By scaling the project down to 42 kilometers, the government aims to:

1. **Reduce investment requirements** — bringing the project's cost within a range more accessible to private consortiums
2. **Improve financial viability** — improving the ratio between construction cost and projected toll revenue
3. **Accelerate procurement** — a smaller, more defined scope should enable faster progress through regulatory and tender processes

The revised investment estimate for the 42-kilometer route stands at approximately **Rp12.7 trillion** — significantly lower than the original full-route estimate.

## What Comes Next: AMDAL Review and 2027 Tender Target

Throughout 2026, the Ministry of PU is focused on completing a range of supporting technical documents before the project can move to procurement, including:

- **AMDAL** (Environmental Impact Assessment) — to be reviewed and updated in line with the revised route
- **Andalalin** (Traffic Impact Analysis) — assessing impacts on the existing road network
- Additional technical and financial feasibility documents required under Indonesian infrastructure regulations

Once these documents are completed and approved, the government is targeting the **business entity tender process to launch in 2027**. Physical construction would only begin after a concessionaire is selected through the tender.

## What This Means for Bali's Connectivity

The cancellation of the Gilimanuk-Pekutatan section raises understandable concerns about accessibility in western Bali. However, the government has affirmed that existing national road capacity improvements and maintenance will continue to ensure smooth vehicle flow to and from Gilimanuk Port — Bali's main maritime gateway to Java.

For central and southern Bali, the Pekutatan-Mengwi route remains genuinely significant. This corridor would substantially cut travel time from Jembrana Regency and Bali's west coast to the economic and tourism centers of Badung and Denpasar — a stretch that regularly sees heavy congestion, particularly during peak holiday seasons.

Business operators and academics hope that this long-awaited clarity on the project's direction will soon be followed by concrete action, ensuring that Bali does not fall behind other Indonesian regions in infrastructure development. While no single toll road can solve all of Bali's transportation challenges, this project — if executed well — remains an important enabler of sustainable economic growth for the island.
