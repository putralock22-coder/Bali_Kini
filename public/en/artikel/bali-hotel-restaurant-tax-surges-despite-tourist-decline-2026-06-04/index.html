Bali's Hotel & Restaurant Tax Hits Rp2.9 Trillion Despite 7% Tourist Drop

Bali's Hotel and Restaurant Tax (PHR) surged Rp300 billion to Rp2.9 trillion through May 2026 despite a 7% drop in foreign tourist arrivals. Governor Koster says it signals higher-quality visitors.

Despite a notable decline in foreign tourist arrivals, Bali’s tourism revenue is painting a surprisingly optimistic picture. The province’s Hotel and Restaurant Tax (PHR) through the end of May 2026 reached Rp2.9 trillion — a jump of approximately Rp300 billion compared to the same period in 2025, when the figure stood at Rp2.62 trillion.

PHR Figures: January–May 2026

Bali Governor Wayan Koster revealed the data at several tourism forums in late May and early June 2026. The official provincial figures show:

  • Total PHR (January 1 – May 27, 2026): Rp2.89 trillion
  • Projected through May 31, 2026: Rp2.9 trillion
  • Hotel tax component: Rp1.8 trillion
  • Restaurant tax component: Rp1.1 trillion

Compared to the same period in 2025, this represents an increase of approximately 11.4 percent — a remarkable achievement given the global headwinds pressuring tourist arrivals.

Why Tourist Numbers Fell: Geopolitical Disruption

Foreign tourist arrivals to Bali experienced a sharp correction in early 2026. In April 2026, the number of international visitors dropped by around 9 percent year-on-year, while May 2026 saw a decline of approximately 7 percent.

Governor Koster attributed the slump largely to the conflict between the United States and Iran, which destabilized the Middle East region. Many European airlines that routinely routed flights to Bali via Middle Eastern transit hubs were forced to suspend or reroute their services.

“Flight routes from Europe to Bali that typically transit through the Middle East were disrupted by the ongoing conflict. This directly reduced the number of arrivals, particularly from Europe,” Koster stated.

Despite this, the governor was quick to point out that falling visitor numbers do not reflect poorly on Bali’s overall tourism performance.

The Quality Tourist Effect

The most striking takeaway from this data is that PHR revenue climbed even as the number of foreign visitors fell. For Governor Koster, this is a clear signal that those arriving in Bali today are higher-quality tourists — travelers with greater spending power who choose licensed accommodations that pay taxes.

“The tourists visiting now are quality tourists. They stay at licensed star-rated hotels, dine at registered restaurants, and contribute substantially to regional revenue,” said Koster.

Three Key Drivers Behind This Trend

  1. Higher spending per visitor — Today’s visitors tend to opt for premium accommodations and more expensive curated experiences.
  2. Star-rated hotels remain well-occupied — Despite the overall dip in arrivals, occupancy rates at Bali’s star-rated hotels held steady at strong levels.
  3. Shift away from unlicensed properties — Since PHR is only collected from licensed establishments, rising tax receipts indicate that more visitors are choosing legal hotels over informal accommodation.

The Illegal Villa Problem

Alongside the good news, Governor Koster flagged a persistent challenge: the proliferation of unlicensed villas and informal accommodations operating across Bali without permits and without paying taxes. These properties often undercut licensed hotels on price, attracting budget-sensitive travelers who do not contribute to the official tax base.

Koster announced that the provincial government, working alongside the Ministry of Tourism, intends to tighten oversight of Online Travel Agents (OTAs) to prevent them from listing unlicensed properties. The move is expected to push more tourists toward legitimate accommodation, boosting both tax revenue and fair competition.

Outlook for the Second Half of 2026

Looking ahead to June–July 2026, Bali’s tourism sector is expected to gain further momentum as Indonesia’s school holiday season begins. Vice President Gibran Rakabuming Raka, speaking at the opening of the Bali & Beyond Travel Fair (BBTF) 2026 — which recorded a remarkable Rp6.9 trillion in potential transactions with participation from 44 countries — reaffirmed the central government’s commitment to elevating the quality of Indonesia’s tourism sector.

The government’s strategic focus is on increasing average length of stay and spending per visit, rather than simply chasing visitor headcounts. This aligns with Bali’s broader vision of sustainable, quality tourism as the guiding policy direction for 2026 and beyond.
